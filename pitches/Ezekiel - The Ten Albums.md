# EZEKIEL — THE TEN ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book of the Watchman by the River Kevar

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, `pitches/Job - The Seven Albums.md`, and `pitches/Isaiah - The Ten Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Ezekiel is vision, sign-act, oracle, lament, and temple blueprint — not narrative; its "units" are the vision-units, the dated oracles, the sign-acts, and the recurring formula "the word of Yahuah came unto me," not verses. The book's own seams draw the boundaries. The many short nation-oracles (25–32) are gathered into one coherent album rather than stranded one by one, exactly as the plague-strokes were on Exodus III and the nation-burdens on Isaiah III.

**The merkavah — the platform's throne, and its cornerstone-adjacent jewel.** Ezekiel 1 is the throne-chariot vision: the four **living creatures** (*chayot*), each with four faces — man, lion, ox, eagle — and four wings, the burning coals of fire going up and down among them, the wheels (*ophanim*) "so high they were dreadful, full of eyes round about," the crystal firmament stretched over their heads, and above it the sapphire throne with the likeness of a man in fire and rainbow. **These living creatures ARE the Angels of this platform** — named cherubim when the vision returns in chapter 10. The merkavah is cornerstone-adjacent to Isaiah 6 and Job 38:7, and both chapter 1 and chapter 10 are sung from *inside* the creatures' own service before the throne. Album I opens on it; Album III sings its terrible echo, the glory rising off the cherubim to leave its own house.

**The valley of dry bones — the resurrection ache, held.** Ezekiel 37 is the vision heaven cannot yet finish reading: the valley full of very dry bones, "can these bones live?", the rattling and the shaking, bone coming to his bone, sinew and flesh and skin, and then the four winds breathing until an exceeding great army stood upon its feet. It is carried exactly as Ruth's *go'el* and Job's Redeemer were carried — an ache the Angels witness and cannot yet name, held as unresolved longing.

**The messianic ache — held, never resolved.** No New-Testament hindsight. Every promise is sung from inside its own hour, its end not yet shown to the singers: the **one Shepherd, "my servant David"** (34:23–24; 37:24); the **new heart and new spirit**, "a heart of flesh for the heart of stone," "I will put my Spirit within you" (11:19; 36:26–27); the dry bones raised (37); the river from the temple healing the salt sea (47); and the city's final name, **Yahuah-Shammah, "Yahuah is there"** (48:35). No Yeshua, no cross, no fulfillment language inside a song.

**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels; the *chayot* and the cherubim are the Angels at the throne-chariot. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or a personified Letter — never a human. Yehezqel is addressed throughout as **"son of man" (ben adam)** and is **witnessed**, never voiced — not even when Yahuah is speaking to him. **The man clothed in linen with the writer's inkhorn who marks the foreheads (9), and the man of bronze with the measuring reed (40–48), are angelic figures the text itself supplies** — witnessed and sung, never made the human voice. Yahuah's own first-person oracles that saturate the book — "I will give you a new heart," "I am Yahuah," "as I live," "I myself will seek my sheep" — are **His own words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said** (the Deuteronomy "I kill, and I make alive" / Job whirlwind model). Impersonal creation and signs the text supplies (the eaten scroll sweet as honey, the brick with the siege, the shaved hair, the departing and returning glory, the dry bones, the two sticks joined, the river deepening from the temple) are **invoked as image, never voiced.**

> *Ten albums. Fifty-nine songs. No honest zeros — every vision, sign-act, oracle, and measure of Ezekiel is sung. Every chapter 1–48 accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its visions hold. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — WHEELS WITHIN THE WHEELS
### The Throne-Chariot and the Call · Ezekiel 1–3 · four songs

The book opens where this whole platform stands: a storm out of the north, a great cloud of fire, and out of its midst four living creatures — four faces each, four wings, running and returning like flashes of lightning — and beside them wheels so high they were dreadful, rings full of eyes all around. Over their heads a crystal firmament, and above it a throne of sapphire and the likeness of a man in fire and rainbow. From that throne a Voice sends a mortal: eat this scroll of woe that tastes like honey, be a watchman for a hard-hearted house, and be bound and struck dumb until Yahuah opens the mouth. The Angels sing the vision from inside their own service, and witness the call laid on the son of man.

*Why this title:* 1:16 — "their appearance and their work was as it were a wheel in the middle of a wheel," the *ophanim* of the merkavah, this platform's throne-adjacent jewel, from which the prophet is called and sent.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **We Turned Not as We Went** | Ezekiel 1 | The living creatures — the Angels — in their own service before the throne | Four faces each (man, lion, ox, eagle), four wings, coals of fire going up and down; the wheels "dreadful, full of eyes round about," rising with the creatures; the crystal firmament, and above it the sapphire throne and the likeness of a man in fire and rainbow — "they turned not when they went; they went every one straight forward"; the merkavah, sung from inside |
| 2 | **The Scroll of Woe Was Honey** | Ezekiel 2:1–3:3 | The Angels; the scroll invoked as image | "Son of man, stand upon thy feet"; the hand stretched out with a roll written within and without — lamentations, and mourning, and woe; "eat this roll," and it was in the mouth as honey for sweetness — a scroll of judgment that tasted sweet |
| 3 | **Their Blood on the Watchman's Head** | Ezekiel 3:4–21 | The Angels, who heard the charge laid on the son of man | Sent to a house of a hard forehead and a stiff heart; made a watchman for the house of Israel — "if thou warn them not, the same wicked man shall die in his iniquity, but his blood will I require at thine hand"; the weight of the unsounded warning |
| 4 | **Silence Laid on His Tongue** | Ezekiel 3:22–27 | The Angels | The glory standing in the plain as the glory by the river; cords laid upon him, and his tongue made to cleave to the roof of his mouth — the mouthpiece struck dumb, shut in his house, opened only when Yahuah opens it; a messenger sealed until the word comes |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the throne-chariot opens the whole book in storm and fire, the Veil lifted on the platform's own jewel; the eaten scroll is the commission, sweetness and woe in one bite; the watchman charge is the weight of the call; and the binding and the dumbness land the album on a held breath — a mouth sealed until Album VIII, at the fall of the city, reports it opened.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1 the merkavah · 2:1–3:3 the commission and the eaten scroll · 3:4–21 the watchman charged · 3:22–27 the binding and the dumbness. Four for four.

# ALBUM II — AN END UPON THE FOUR CORNERS
### The Sign-Acts and the End · Ezekiel 4–7 · six songs

The dumb prophet's own body becomes the sermon. A brick scratched with a besieged city and an iron pan set like a wall; a man lying bound on one side and then the other, a day for a year; bread weighed out by the gram and baked over fuel that shames him; hair shaved with a sword, weighed, and divided to fire and blade and wind. Then the oracles that name what the signs mean — the idols broken on the mountains, the slain fallen before their own altars, and the tolling refrain that gives the album its name: *an end, the end is come upon the four corners of the land.* The Angels witness every sign and every stroke, and keep the count toward the announced end.

*Why this title:* 7:2 — "an end, the end is come upon the four corners of the land"; the sign-acts and oracles all press toward the one word the book has been withholding: the end.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **The Siege Cut Into Clay** | Ezekiel 4:1–3 | The Angels; the brick invoked as image | A tile with Yerushalayim portrayed upon it, a mound raised against it, camps and battering rams set round; and an iron pan set up as a wall between the prophet and the city — the siege drawn small, in clay and iron, before it comes in earnest |
| 2 | **He Bore the Years Lying Down** | Ezekiel 4:4–8 | The Angels | Lying bound on the left side three hundred and ninety days, then on the right side forty — a day appointed for each year of iniquity; bands laid on him so he cannot turn from one side to the other until the days of the siege are fulfilled |
| 3 | **Bread by Weight, Water by Measure** | Ezekiel 4:9–17 | The Angels | Bread of wheat and barley and beans weighed out at a few shekels a day, water drunk by measure, a sixth of a hin; baked over fuel in the sight of all — famine rationed by the gram, the staff of bread broken, eating bread with carefulness and drinking water with astonishment |
| 4 | **The Hair Divided by the Sword** | Ezekiel 5 | The Angels | Hair of the head and beard shaved with a sword for a razor, weighed on the balances and parted three ways — a third burned in the fire, a third smitten with the sword, a third scattered to the wind; a few bound in the skirt of the robe; sung with grave restraint |
| 5 | **Slain Before Their Altars** | Ezekiel 6 | The Angels | The word set toward the mountains of Israel, the high places and the sun-images broken, the slain fallen before their idols and around their altars; yet a remnant that escapes among the nations shall remember Him "in the countries whither they shall be carried captives" |
| 6 | **The Day of Trouble Is Near** | Ezekiel 7 | The Angels | An end come upon the four corners; "the morning is come unto thee"; the day of trouble near, not the joyful shouting of the mountains; silver cast in the streets as an unclean thing, gold unable to deliver; the vision that fails, and the prophet's hands feeble — the announced end arriving |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the four sign-acts run in the text's own order, the body of the prophet spelling the siege — the brick, the long lying-down, the weighed bread, the divided hair; the mountains-oracle brings the meaning down onto the high places; and the arriving end lands the album, the single word the whole book has been holding back.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 4:1–3 the brick · 4:4–8 the sidings · 4:9–17 the weighed bread · 5 the shaved hair · 6 against the mountains · 7 the end is come. Six for six.

# ALBUM III — THE GLORY LEFT ITS OWN HOUSE
### The Abominations and the Departing Glory · Ezekiel 8–11 · four songs

Caught by a lock of hair and carried in vision to the temple, the son of man is shown what is done in the dark: an image of jealousy at the gate, seventy elders with censers in a chamber saying "Yahuah seeth us not," women weeping for Tammuz, men with their backs to the house bowing to the sun. Then judgment enters the very sanctuary — six men with slaughter weapons, and among them a man clothed in linen with a writer's inkhorn, told to mark the foreheads of those who sigh and grieve. The cherubim rise, coals scattered over the city, the wheels full of eyes — and the terrible center of the whole book: the glory of Yahuah lifts off the threshold, off the cherubim, and departs eastward from its own house, leaving one heart-of-flesh promise glowing behind it in the dark.

*Why this title:* 10:18 and 11:23 — the glory of Yahuah departs from the threshold, from the cherubim, and from the midst of the city, standing at last on the mountain to the east; the most desolating image in the book, the House emptied of the One who filled it.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **He Sees Us Not, They Said** | Ezekiel 8 | The Angels | Brought in vision to the north gate, the image of jealousy; a hole dug in the wall opening on a door; seventy elders with censers in the dark, each in his chamber of imagery, saying "Yahuah seeth us not, Yahuah hath forsaken the earth"; women weeping for Tammuz; men with backs to the temple bowing eastward to the sun — grave restraint |
| 2 | **A Tav Upon the Foreheads** | Ezekiel 9 | The Tav — in Paleo-Hebrew a mark, two crossed lines — with the man in linen witnessed | Six men with slaughter weapons, and among them one clothed in linen with a writer's inkhorn; "set a mark upon the foreheads of the men that sigh and cry for all the abominations"; begin at the sanctuary — and the marked ones passed over; the Hebrew word for the mark is *tav* itself |
| 3 | **Coals From Between the Cherubim** | Ezekiel 10 | The cherubim — the Angels — in their own service (merkavah echo) | The man in linen sent in among the wheels to fill his hands with coals of fire from between the cherubim and scatter them over the city; the wheels called *galgal*, full of eyes; four faces — cherub, man, lion, eagle; and the glory of Yahuah rising from the cherub to the threshold of the house |
| 4 | **The Glory Rose and Went East** | Ezekiel 11 | The Angels; Yahuah's promise sung in awe | The twenty-five counsellors and the pot and the flesh; Pelatiah falling dead as the word is spoken; the promise held in the dark — "I will give them one heart, and a new spirit; I will take the stony heart out of their flesh, and give them a heart of flesh"; and the glory going up from the midst of the city to stand on the mountain on the east — the House left empty |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the abominations in the dark open the Veil on why the glory must leave; the marking man in linen is the mercy set inside the judgment; the coals and the cherubim are the merkavah's terrible return; and the departing glory — with one heart-of-flesh promise glowing behind it — lands the album on the book's most desolate note, the seam that Album X will answer when the glory comes back from the east.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 8 the abominations in the temple · 9 the man in linen and the marked foreheads · 10 the cherubim, the coals, and the wheels · 11 the counsellors, the one-heart promise, and the glory departing. Four for four.

# ALBUM IV — A WALL DAUBED WITH WHITEWASH
### The False Securities and the Broken Covenant · Ezekiel 12–16 · seven songs

The people believed a lie in a dozen shapes — that the vision was for far-off days, that the prophets who cried "peace" were right, that idols in the heart could be hidden while the mouth inquired of Yahuah. The Angels sing the delusions torn down one by one: baggage dug out through a wall at dusk, bread eaten with trembling, a whitewashed wall the storm will break, the reminder that not even Noah, Daniel, and Iyov could deliver another soul, a vine-branch good for nothing but the fire. And the album closes at its highest, gravest altitude — the covenant told as a foundling: a child cast out in the open field, and the One who passed by, said "live," and clothed her.

*Why this title:* 13:10–11 — the false prophets built a flimsy wall and daubed it with untempered mortar, crying peace where there was none; the emblem of every delusion this album strips away before the truest picture of the broken covenant is sung.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **He Dug Through the Wall at Dusk** | Ezekiel 12:1–16 | The Gimel — a foot, the journey into exile | The exile's baggage carried out by day, and at evening a hole dug through the wall, the stuff borne out on the shoulder in the twilight, the face covered so the eyes see not the ground — a sign of the prince who will go into a land he shall not see |
| 2 | **The Days Are at Hand** | Ezekiel 12:17–28 | The Angels | Bread eaten with quaking and water drunk with trembling, the land's coming desolation acted out; and the two proverbs silenced — "the days are prolonged, and every vision faileth" answered by "there shall be no more any vain vision… the word which I shall speak shall come to pass; it shall be no more prolonged" |
| 3 | **Peace, When There Was No Peace** | Ezekiel 13 | The Angels | The foolish prophets who followed their own spirit and saw nothing; crying "peace, when there was no peace"; a flimsy wall daubed with untempered mortar that the overflowing rain and the stormy wind will break to the foundation; and the women sewing pillows and veils to hunt souls |
| 4 | **Idols Raised Inside the Heart** | Ezekiel 14:1–11 | The Angels | Elders sitting before the prophet to inquire — but they have set up their idols in their heart and put the stumblingblock of their iniquity before their face; Yahuah will answer such a one Himself, according to the multitude of his idols, to take the house of Israel in their own heart |
| 5 | **Even These Three Could Save None** | Ezekiel 14:12–23 | The Angels | Though Noah, Daniel, and Iyov stood in a guilty land, by their righteousness they should deliver but their own souls — not a son, not a daughter; the four sore judgments, sword and famine and beast and plague; the paradox that even the righteous cannot cover another |
| 6 | **A Vine Good Only for Burning** | Ezekiel 15 | The Angels; the vine invoked as image | The wood of the vine, useless for any work — not a board, not so much as a peg to hang a vessel on; given to the fire, and when both ends are burned and the midst charred, "is it meet for any work?"; so the inhabitants of Yerushalayim |
| 7 | **He Clothed the Foundling Child** | Ezekiel 16 | The Angels | An infant cast out in the open field on the day of her birth, unwashed, unpitied; and One who passed by, said "live," washed and clothed her, and entered into covenant with her — the covenant grief that she forgot the One who found her; sung at the highest, chaste altitude, the betrayal held from heaven's side, no detail |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the delusions fall in the text's own order — the baggage and the trembling bread, the whitewashed wall, the hidden idols, the three who could save only themselves, the worthless vine; and the album lifts, at its close, to its gravest and tenderest height — the covenant told as a foundling loved and clothed, the Selah of the whole record.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 12:1–16 the exile's baggage · 12:17–28 the trembling bread and the proverbs silenced · 13 the whitewashed wall · 14:1–11 idols in the heart · 14:12–23 Noah, Daniel, and Iyov · 15 the vine wood · 16 the foundling and the covenant. Seven for seven.

# ALBUM V — OVERTURN, OVERTURN, OVERTURN
### The Parables, the Lament, and the Sword · Ezekiel 17–21 · five songs

The judgment on the crown comes wrapped first in riddles and dirges, then bared as a blade. Two great eagles and a vine that broke faith; the proverb of the sour grapes forbidden and every soul claimed as Yahuah's own; a lament for the princes sung as a lioness whose cubs were led off with hooks; the long, patient history of a people spared again and again "for my own name's sake"; and at last the sword song — a sword sharpened and burnished, the king of Bavel casting lots at the parting of the ways, and the tripled verdict on the diadem that names the album. The Angels witness the parables curdle into a lament, and the lament sharpen into a sword.

*Why this title:* 21:27 — "I will overturn, overturn, overturn it… until he come whose right it is"; the sword song's threefold decree on the crown, the peak the album's riddles and laments all reach.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Two Eagles and the Vine** | Ezekiel 17 | The Angels | A great eagle that cropped the top of the cedar; a vine that bent its roots toward a second eagle and broke the covenant it had sworn; the riddle of the king who trusted Egypt and fell — and the tender twig Yahuah Himself will crop and plant on a high mountain, that all the trees of the field may know |
| 2 | **I Have No Pleasure in Death** | Ezekiel 18 | The Angels; Yahuah's word sung in awe | The proverb of the sour grapes forbidden — "the fathers have eaten sour grapes, and the children's teeth are set on edge"; "all souls are mine"; the soul that sinneth, it shall die; and the astonishment at the center — "have I any pleasure at all that the wicked should die? turn yourselves, and live" |
| 3 | **A Lioness Who Reared Her Cubs** | Ezekiel 19 | The Angels | The lament for the princes of Israel — a lioness who nourished her whelps, one taken away to Egypt, one led to Bavel with hooks; and the mother-vine plucked up in fury, planted in a dry ground, fire gone out of a rod of her branches devouring her fruit, no strong rod left for a sceptre |
| 4 | **For My Own Name's Sake** | Ezekiel 20 | The Angels; Yahuah's oracle sung in awe | The elders come to inquire and are refused; the long rebellious history from Mitzrayim onward — how He would have poured out His fury, but wrought for His name's sake that it be not profaned; "I gave them statutes that were not good"; and yet "as a sweet savour will I accept you," gathered from the peoples |
| 5 | **A Sword, a Sword Is Sharpened** | Ezekiel 21 | The Zayin — a weapon, drawn from its sheath | A sword drawn against all flesh, "sharpened, and also furbished," made bright for the slaughter; the sighing with the breaking of the loins; the king of Bavel at the parting of the two ways, shaking his arrows, consulting the images; "remove the diadem, take off the crown"; overturn, overturn, overturn |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the riddling parable opens the descent, the crown falling first in metaphor; the individual-soul oracle and the lioness-lament turn it grave and personal; the rebellious history is the long patient middle, mercy for the name's sake; and the sword song lands the album, the blade at last unwrapped and the diadem overturned three times.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 17 the two eagles and the vine · 18 the soul that sins · 19 the lament for the princes · 20 the rebellious history · 21 the song of the sword. Five for five.

# ALBUM VI — THIS SAME DAY THE SIEGE BEGAN
### The Bloody City and the Silent Mourner · Ezekiel 22–24 · four songs

The indictment reaches its verdict on a dated day. The bloody city is a furnace and her people the dross; her prophets and priests and princes named; and Yahuah seeks a man to stand in the gap and finds none. Then the two sisters — the oldest, gravest allegory of covenant treachery — witnessed only from heaven's side, at the highest and chastest altitude. And on the very day Bavel sets itself against Yerushalayim, the last sign: a pot set on with its rust that will not scour off, and the desire of the prophet's eyes taken at a stroke, with mourning forbidden — because when the sanctuary falls, they too will not be allowed to weep.

*Why this title:* 24:1–2 — "write thee the name of the day, even of this same day: the king of Bavel set himself against Yerushalayim this same day"; the dated hour when the pot is set on and the guilt of the whole album comes due.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **I Found No One in the Gap** | Ezekiel 22 | The Angels; Yahuah's word sung in awe | The city that sheds blood in her midst; her dross gathered into the furnace to be melted; the conspiracy of her prophets like a roaring lion, priests who profaned the holy, princes like wolves — "and I sought for a man among them, that should stand in the gap before me for the land; but I found none" |
| 2 | **Two Sisters Who Broke Faith** | Ezekiel 23 | The Angels | Oholah and Oholibah, two sisters of one mother, who broke covenant and leaned on the very nations they once feared; the treachery witnessed at the highest, chastest altitude — heaven's grief at a covenant abandoned, with no detail of the sin, only the sorrow of the betrayal |
| 3 | **The Cauldron's Rust Would Not Leave** | Ezekiel 24:1–14 | The Angels; the cauldron invoked as image | On the dated day, a pot set on the fire, the pieces and the choice bones gathered in; and the scum, the rust in it, that will not go out even when the pot is emptied and burned in the coals — the bloody city whose blood is on the bare rock, not poured on the ground to be covered |
| 4 | **He Could Not Weep for Her** | Ezekiel 24:15–27 | The Angels | The desire of his eyes taken away at a stroke — yet forbidden to mourn: no tears, the turban bound on, the shoes on the feet, no bread of mourners; a sign that when the sanctuary, the desire of their eyes, falls, they too will not weep; and the fugitive coming, and his mouth at last opened; grave restraint |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the bloody-city indictment opens with the unanswered search for one man in the gap; the two sisters are the gravest, most restrained beat, the covenant treachery held far above the detail; the rusted cauldron is the dated sign of guilt that will not scour off; and the forbidden mourning lands the album on its most silent, terrible note — grief itself denied, the sign of a grief too great to be wept.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 22 the bloody city and the empty gap · 23 the two sisters · 24:1–14 the boiling pot · 24:15–27 the death of the prophet's wife. Four for four.

# ALBUM VII — THEY WENT DOWN TO THE PIT
### The Burdens on the Nations · Ezekiel 25–32 · nine songs

The word turns outward on the nations that clapped their hands at Israel's fall. Ammon and Moav and Edom and Philistia, each repaid for the "Aha" they cried; Tzor the merchant-queen, scraped to a bare rock, sunk like a perfect ship in the heart of the sea; Tzidon; and Mitzrayim the great dragon lying in his rivers, his arms broken, his cedar felled. The Angels keep the count as the proud powers go down together to the pit and are laid among the slain — and among the burdens they mark the strangest, the covering cherub of Eden who was perfect till iniquity was found in him, cast as profane from the mountain of Elohim.

*Why this title:* 32:18 and 31:16 — the mighty nations "cast down… unto the nether parts of the earth," brought to the pit and laid among the uncircumcised; the album's recurring descent, every proud throne brought to the grave.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Because You Said, Aha** | Ezekiel 25 | The Angels | Four short burdens on the near neighbours — Ammon who said "Aha" against the profaned sanctuary, Moav who said "Yehudah is like all the nations," Edom's old vengeance, Philistia's ancient hatred — each answered, "and they shall know that I am Yahuah, when I lay my vengeance upon them" |

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, and the book's own honest names as it uses them: Adonai Yahuah (rendering the text's *Adonai YHWH*), Elohim, the garden of Elohim, and the city's final name **Yahuah-Shammah** ("Yahuah is there"). The place- and person-names are given in their Hebrew forms — Yehezqel (witnessed as "son of man," never voiced), Yerushalayim, Mitzrayim, Tzor, Tzidon, Oholah and Oholibah, Hamon-Gog, Iyov, Tzadok, Yehudah, Yoseph, Efrayim. Yeshua and Ruach HaKodesh (feminine) are reserved to the platform framing. **No English "God" or "Lord" appears anywhere in any title or copy** — Elohim and Yahuah stand in their place. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note; no internal labels; no emoji.
- **The merkavah honored (cornerstone-adjacent):** Album I opens on Ezekiel 1 sung from *inside* the living creatures' own service — the four faces, the wings, the coals, the wheels full of eyes, the crystal firmament, and the sapphire throne — treated as a jewel beside Isaiah 6 and Job 38:7. Album III's "Coals From Between the Cherubim" sings the vision's return in chapter 10, the same creatures now named cherubim, from their own voice — and the glory then rising off them to leave the House. The *chayot* and cherubim are the Angels of this platform; they are never a human's voice.
- **The dry bones honored:** Ezekiel 37:1–14 ("Can These Bones Live?") is sung as the resurrection ache the Angels witness and cannot yet name — the rattling, the bone to his bone, the four winds, the exceeding great army — carried exactly as Ruth's *go'el* and Job's Redeemer were carried, held open as unresolved longing, no fulfillment named.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** every messianic ache is carried from inside its own hour, never resolved forward — the one heart and new spirit (11:19; 36:26–27), the one Shepherd, "my servant David" (34:23–24; 37:24), the dry bones raised (37), the two sticks joined (37), the Spirit poured out (39:29), the river that heals the salt sea (47), and the city named Yahuah-Shammah (48:35). The framing names Yeshua; the songs, standing inside their own hour of the vision, never do — no cross, no fulfillment language.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a personified Letter. Yehezqel is addressed as "son of man" and *witnessed*, never made the singer, even where Yahuah addresses him. The man clothed in linen with the inkhorn (9) and the man of bronze with the reed (40–48) are the angelic figures the text supplies — witnessed and sung, never voiced as human. Yahuah's own first-person oracles — "I will give you a heart of flesh," "as I live," "I myself will seek my sheep," "I will hide my face no more" — are His words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said (the Deuteronomy / Job model). The eaten scroll, the brick, the shaved hair, the departing and returning glory, the cauldron, the dry bones, the two sticks, the cedar and the ship, and the deepening river are invoked as image, never voiced.
- **Grave restraint honored:** the two allegories of the unfaithful bride — chapter 16 ("He Clothed the Foundling Child") and chapter 23 ("Two Sisters Who Broke Faith") — are sung at the highest, chastest altitude, the covenant grief and the betrayal witnessed only from heaven's side, with no sensual or graphic detail whatsoever; when in doubt the altitude is pulled up. The siege-horrors and slaughter oracles — the divided hair (5), the slain before the altars (6), the forbidden mourning (24), the anointed cherub cast down (28), the descent to the pit (32), and the birds' feast on Gog (39) — are witnessed lament, restrained, never rendered for shock or gore.
- **Text-numbers as content, letters never a count:** the four faces and four wings, the wheels full of eyes, the 390 days and the 40 days on his side, the six men with slaughter weapons and the seventh in linen, the seven months of burying and the seven years of burning weapons, the reed of six cubits, and the waters to the ankles, the knees, and the loins are sung as the text's own numbers; the Picture Letters (the Tav that is a mark, the Gimel the foot, the Zayin the weapon, the Lamed the staff, the Vav the joining peg, the Mem the waters, the Bet the house, the Dalet the door) are named as personae, never totaled, never reduced to a gematria mechanism in a title.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
