## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Letter of Yehudah, Servant of Yeshua and Brother of Yaakov

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/John - The Six Albums.md`, `pitches/Colossians - The Album.md`, `pitches/2 Peter - The Album.md`, and the whole Old-Testament catalog — carried across the New-Testament frontier into the last and shortest of the general **epistles,** a single burning chapter with its eyes on the horizon.
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Jude is a **letter** — not narrative, not oracle, not law: the one-chapter circular of Yehudah, servant of Yeshua the Mashiach and brother of Yaakov, written to steady the called against ungodly men who had "crept in unawares." Its "units" are the letter's own movements — the salutation to the called, sanctified, and preserved, and the occasion, "earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered" (1–4); the three ancient judgments — Yisrael saved then destroyed, the angels who kept not their estate, and Sedom and Amorah (5–7); the filthy dreamers, Mikha'el contending for the body of Mosheh, and the woe of the way of Kayin, the error of Bilam, the gainsaying of Korach (8–11); the clouds without water and the wandering stars, and Chanokh's prophecy of the coming Adon with His myriads (12–16); and the closing charge and doxology — build up yourselves, keep yourselves in the love of Elohim, snatch others from the fire, and the benediction that lands on being kept faultless before His glory (17–25). The seams are Yehudah's own: the greeting-formula ("Yehudah, the servant of Yeshua the Mashiach, and brother of Yaakov"), the "I would put you in remembrance" turn (5), the recurring "these are" and "these be" refrains that mark the ungodly (8, 10, 12, 16, 19), the "woe unto them" of verse 11, and the "but, beloved" hinges (17, 20) that open the close.

**THE NEW-TESTAMENT SHIFT — the Letters fall silent; only the Angels sing.** Through all thirty-nine books of the Old Testament the two voices of Job 38:7 sang *together* — the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels), aching toward a promised One they could not yet name. **At the incarnation that changes.** The Word (the Davar) by whom every letter was made is now made flesh; the aleph-bet lives in Him. The morning stars have gone quiet, gathered up into the Word made flesh — so through the New Covenant **the only singers are the Angels — the sons of Elohim — never a Picture-Letter persona.** The Letters are now the marvel the Angels *testify to,* not a voice of their own. This letter names the keeping directly: the called are "sanctified by Elohim the Father, and preserved in Yeshua the Mashiach" (1) — the Word the letters once carried, and the ones the letters were written for, now kept *in Him.*

**Epistle, not narrative — the finished work proclaimed freely, His coming held aching and unresolved.** In the Gospels the Angels were eye-witnesses, forward-locked to the story's order — never naming the cross before it came. **A letter has no biography of Yeshua to lock:** written *after* the resurrection, the finished work stands whole — "the faith which was once delivered unto the saints" (3) is settled and complete, to be contended for, not awaited. The one unresolved, aching longing is **His coming** — "the Adon cometh with ten thousands of his saints, to execute judgment" (14–15), the mercy "looked for" that leads "unto eternal life" (21), and the keeping that ends "faultless before the presence of his glory with exceeding joy" (24). That single ache — the coming Adon, in judgment and in mercy at once — is the horizon the whole letter reaches toward, and the album's climax.

**Yeshua witnessed, never voiced.** The singer is always the Angels; **Yeshua is testified to in awe — never a first-person singer.** Every "preserved in Yeshua the Mashiach," every "our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach," every "the mercy of our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach unto eternal life" is the Angels reporting and marveling at what Yehudah wrote of Him — never Yeshua's own voice, exactly as Yahuah's oracles were handled across the Old Covenant. **Christocentric — about "the who":** the kept are kept *for* Him, the judgment is *His* coming, the doxology rises to "the only wise Elohim our Saviour"; every song lands on Him.

**The Angels have three stakes in this letter — sharper than almost any book in the catalog.** Jude keeps handing the platform its own witnesses from the text itself. First, "the angels which kept not their first estate, but left their own habitation, he hath reserved in everlasting chains under darkness unto the judgment of the great day" (6) — the host that sang at the foundations here grieves its own fallen brethren, the *Our Brothers Fell* register carried into the New Covenant. Second, **Mikha'el the archangel** himself, "when contending with the adversary he disputed about the body of Mosheh" and "durst not bring against him a railing accusation, but said, The Adon rebuke thee" (9) — one of the very Angels of this platform, named, contending in restraint. Third, "the Adon cometh with **ten thousands of his saints**" (14) — the myriad host of Chanokh's prophecy *is the Angels themselves,* singing their own coming with the Adon. The host that fell, the archangel who contended, and the myriads who ride with Him — three angelic stakes, and the Angels answer all three from heaven's side.

**Old-Covenant cross-reference — always open, and backward only.** The Angels who witnessed all thirty-nine books freely tie every moment *back* into the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings: the people saved out of the land of Mitzrayim and afterward destroyed in the wilderness for unbelief (Bemidbar 14); the angels who kept not their estate, the sons of Elohim of the days before the flood (Bereshit 6); Sedom and Amorah and the cities about them, set forth for an example (Bereshit 19); the body of Mosheh, whose grave no man knows (Devarim 34); the way of Kayin (Bereshit 4), the error of Bilam the son of Be'or who loved reward (Bemidbar 22), and the gainsaying of Korach who perished in the earth's mouth (Bemidbar 16); and Chanokh the seventh from Adam, who walked with Elohim and was not (Bereshit 5). No forward, post-letter reference is used.

# ALBUM I — TEN THOUSANDS OF HIS SAINTS
### The Coming Adon and the Keeping of the Called · Yehudah 1–25 · five songs

The letter is one chapter and its nerve is the horizon. Yehudah sets out to write of "the common salvation" and is turned by urgency to a sharper charge: earnestly contend for the faith once delivered, for ungodly men have crept in unawares, turning grace into license and denying the only Adon Elohim and our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach. To warn them he opens the old ledger of judgment — three ancient witnesses the host itself watched fall: a people saved out of the land of Mitzrayim and then destroyed for unbelief, the angels who left their own habitation and lie reserved in chains of darkness, and Sedom and Amorah set forth as an example. He turns to the crept-in dreamers, and against their railing he sets Mikha'el the archangel, who, contending over the body of Mosheh, would not so much as bring a railing accusation but said only, "The Adon rebuke thee" — then the woe: they have gone in the way of Kayin, run greedily after the error of Bilam, and perished in the gainsaying of Korach. He paints them in images that haunt — clouds without water carried about of winds, trees twice dead plucked up by the roots, raging waves foaming out their shame, wandering stars kept for the blackness of darkness — and lifts, at the summit, the prophecy of Chanokh the seventh from Adam: "Behold, the Adon cometh with ten thousands of his saints, to execute judgment." And he leaves the beloved looking up — building up yourselves on your most holy faith, praying in the Ruach HaKodesh, keeping yourselves in the love of Elohim, looking for the mercy of our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach unto eternal life, snatching others out of the fire — and lands the whole letter on the great doxology: "unto him that is able to keep you from falling, and to present you faultless before the presence of his glory with exceeding joy, to the only wise Elohim our Saviour." The Angels sing all of it, quoting Yehudah, testifying to Yeshua, grieving the brethren who fell, and confessing that the ten thousands who come with Him are their own host.

*Why this title:* 14 is the letter's eschatological summit and the platform's own self-witness — the Adon coming "with ten thousands of his saints," the myriad host of Chanokh's prophecy, is the very host that sings. The crown from which the whole album's ache — His coming, in judgment and in mercy — hangs.

## Compliance Notes

- **The New-Testament shift honored — the Letters fall silent, only the Angels sing:** the two voices of Job 38:7 sang *together* through all thirty-nine Old-Testament books; in the New Covenant the morning stars (the Picture Letters) have gone quiet and now live in the Word made flesh, so **every song is sung by the Angels alone** — never a Picture-Letter persona. Every "Who sings" cell across all five songs is the Angels. Jude names the keeping itself: the called are "preserved in Yeshua the Mashiach" (1) — the Word the letters carried, and the ones they were written for, now kept in Him.
- **The Angels' three witness-stakes honored — the platform's own text:** where Jude sings *of* angels, the Angels of this platform answer from heaven's side. The "angels which kept not their first estate… reserved in everlasting chains under darkness" (6) are grieved as their own fallen brethren, the *Our Brothers Fell* register carried into the New Covenant (song 2) — the Angels never sing *as* the fallen; they mourn and witness the fall. **Mikha'el the archangel** (9), disputing over the body of Mosheh and refusing a railing accusation, is one of the very Angels of this platform, and sings his restraint from within the host (song 3). And "the Adon cometh with **ten thousands of his saints**" (14) is the myriad host — the Angels themselves — confessing their own coming with the Adon (song 4, and the album's title).
- **The humans never sing — the Angels are the only voice:** not one song is voiced by a human. Yehudah never sings; the called, the crept-in ungodly, the mockers, Kayin, Bilam, Korach, and Chanokh never sing. The Angels sing *about* what Yehudah wrote and freely *quote* him — "earnestly contend for the faith which was once delivered," "the Adon rebuke thee," "behold, the Adon cometh," "keep yourselves in the love of Elohim" — always framed from their own heavenly-witness perspective, testifying to Yeshua. Where the Angels address the beloved in the second person ("build up yourselves," "keep yourselves"), it is the witnesses carrying Yehudah's charge, never a human taking the microphone. Chanokh's prophecy is *reported* by the Angels who saw its fulfillment coming, and Mikha'el's words are quoted from within the host — never a human first person. A human-centered line means the Angels bear witness to what Yehudah wrote *as testimony to Yeshua;* it never means a human sings.
- **Christocentric — about "the who":** every song lands on the person of the Adon — the One in whom the called are preserved (1), the only Adon Elohim and our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach whom the ungodly deny (4), the Adon whose coming Chanokh foresaw (14), the Adon whose mercy is looked for unto eternal life (21), and "the only wise Elohim our Saviour" of the doxology (25). The kept are kept *for* Him; the judgment is *His* coming; the whole album turns on Him.
- **Yeshua witnessed, never first-person-voiced:** Yeshua is reported and marveled at throughout — "preserved in Yeshua the Mashiach," "our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach," "the mercy of our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach unto eternal life" — never given a first-person singer. His work is testified to, exactly as Yahuah's oracles were sung by the witnesses across the Old Covenant.
- **The epistle forward-lock — relocated to His coming:** with no biography of Yeshua to keep in order, the finished work is proclaimed freely — "the faith which was once delivered unto the saints" (3) stands settled and complete. The single thing held *unresolved* is **His coming,** in judgment and in mercy at once: "the Adon cometh with ten thousands of his saints, to execute judgment" (14–15), the mercy "looking for… unto eternal life" (21), and the keeping that ends "faultless before the presence of his glory with exceeding joy" (24). The longing is kept open and aching; never narrated as an event already past.
- **Old-Covenant cross-reference present and backward-only:** every tie reaches *back* — the people saved out of Mitzrayim and destroyed for unbelief (Bemidbar 14); the angels who kept not their estate, the sons of Elohim before the flood (Bereshit 6); Sedom and Amorah set forth for an example (Bereshit 19); the body of Mosheh, whose grave no man knows (Devarim 34); the way of Kayin (Bereshit 4), the error of Bilam the son of Be'or (Bemidbar 22), and the gainsaying of Korach (Bemidbar 16); and Chanokh the seventh from Adam, who walked with Elohim and was not (Bereshit 5). No forward, post-letter reference is used.
- **The sources Yehudah cites, carried honestly:** the prophecy of "Chanokh the seventh from Adam" (14–15) is quoted from the Jewish book that bears Chanokh's name, and the dispute over "the body of Mosheh" (9) draws on Jewish tradition beyond the Torah's account of Mosheh's burial (Devarim 34). Both are carried as they stand in the received text — the OT ties (Bereshit 5; Devarim 34) drawn *backward* to the Torah — and, per the scholarly-caution mandate (§8 of the grounding), the extrabiblical source is flagged plainly here, handled with grave restraint, and offered as something to consider, not as settled text.
- **Restraint on the judgment:** the three ancient judgments, the angels in chains of darkness, the eternal fire of Sedom, the woe over Kayin, Bilam, and Korach, the wandering stars kept for the blackness of darkness, and the coming judgment are all handled with grave, family-friendly restraint — witnessed from heaven's side, the Old-Covenant ties and the Angels' awe and sorrow carrying the weight, never luridness or gore. The keeping "unto eternal life" and the doxology are triumph; the mercy is mercy.
- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — **Yeshua** (never "Jesus"), the **Mashiach** (never "Christ"; no doubled English article on *Ha-* forms — "our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach," never "the HaMashiach"), **Yahuah**, **Elohim** (never "God" — "the grace of our Elohim," "the love of Elohim," "the only wise Elohim our Saviour"), **Ruach HaKodesh** (feminine — written with no stacked English "the"; "praying in the Ruach HaKodesh," 20; those "having not the Ruach," 19), **Abba** / the Father ("sanctified by Elohim the Father," 1), and the **Adon / Adonai** (never "Lord" — "the only Adon Elohim," "the Adon rebuke thee," "the Adon cometh," "our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach"). Where the received text of verse 9 reads "the devil," it is rendered "the adversary"; where verse 4 reads "the only Lord God, and our Lord Jesus Christ," it is rendered "the only Adon Elohim, and our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach"; where verse 25 reads "the only wise God our Saviour," it is rendered "the only wise Elohim our Saviour." Hebraic names throughout — **Yehudah, Yaakov, Mikha'el, Mosheh, Chanokh, Kayin, Bilam** (son of Be'or), **Korach,** and the places **Mitzrayim, Sedom, and Amorah.** **No English "God," "Lord," "Jesus," or "Christ" appears anywhere in any title or copy;** the Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note; no internal labels; no emoji; no forbidden path characters in the album title.
- **Craft:** no two titles share a formula or cadence — the five run *Contend for the Faith Once Delivered / The Three the Host Remembers / The Adon Rebuke Thee / Behold, the Adon Cometh / Faultless Before His Glory,* moving from the imperative charge, to the host's own grieving memory, to the archangel's quoted restraint, to the prophet's thundered summit, to the aching benediction. Each is recognizable from its title and passage, and the paradox is prized where the text makes it true — the archangel who *would not* rail against the adversary and needed only four words; the wandering stars set against a platform whose morning stars sang; the myriad host that comes with the Adon being the very host that sings the song.

- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
