# MARK — THE FOUR ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Good News According to Markos

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, `pitches/Job - The Seven Albums.md`, and `pitches/Isaiah - The Ten Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Mark is the shortest, fastest Gospel — a breathless eye-witness report (traditionally Kepha's own memories) whose signature word is *immediately* (euthys), one deed hard on the heels of the last. Its "units" are the text's own scenes — the call by the sea, the day in Kfar-Nachum, the stilled storm, the confession, the entry, the Passover, the cross, the empty tomb — drawn at the seams the narrative supplies (change of place, of hour, of subject) and grouped, where the material is tightly clustered, into single movements exactly as Isaiah's short oracles and Exodus's plagues were.

**THE GOSPEL SHIFTS THE RULE — the witnesses become eye-witnesses.** Through all thirty-nine books of the Old Testament the two voices of Job 38:7 — the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels) — *ached* toward the promised One, holding Him unresolved, never naming Him. **Now, in the Gospel, they see Him unveiled.** Four rules govern every title below:

- **Christocentric — every song is about Yeshua, the "who."** Each track is about the *person* of Yeshua: who He is, what the Father declares over Him, what He said and did, what the witnesses saw. He is the subject of every song; every title points at Him.
- **Eye-witness chronology, forward-locked.** The Angels unveil Yeshua as Mark's own narrative reveals Him, in order. Mark opens straight at the Yarden (there is no Nativity here); a song may reference any event already happened up to its point — nothing later. The Messiah is *named* — Yeshua — and unveiled step by step. Yeshua's own predictions of His suffering (8:31; 9:31; 10:33) are sung as *His words within the narrative*, never as accomplished events.
- **Old-Testament cross-reference is always open — backward only.** The host that sang creation and witnessed every book freely ties each moment back to the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings, even when the humans do not realize it: the voice crying in the wilderness (Isa 40 / Mal 3:1), the stilled sea and the One who rebuked the deep (Ps 106:9; Job 9:8), the manna behind the loaves, Zecharyah's lowly king on the colt (Zech 9:9), the rejected stone (Ps 118:22), the smitten shepherd (Zech 13:7), and Psalm 22 on the cross — *the very ache they once held unresolved.*
- **Voice rule — in the Gospel, only the Angels sing.** A shift happens at the incarnation: the Word (the Davar) who spoke the Picture Letters into being is now present in the flesh in Yeshua, so **all the Letters live in Him** and fall silent as separate singers. Through the Old Testament the morning stars and the sons of Elohim sang together (Job 38:7); before the Word made flesh the Letters go quiet, and **the only singing voice in this Gospel is the Angels** — never a Picture-Letter persona. The Angels remain first-person-plural witnesses — never a human (not Markos, not Kepha, not Pilate), and **Yeshua is witnessed and testified to in awe, never a first-person singer**, exactly as Yahuah's words were handled in the Old Testament. The angelic figures Mark himself supplies **are the Angels of this platform**: the angels who ministered to Him in the wilderness (1:13) and the **young man clothed in a long white garment sitting in the empty tomb** who says *he is risen; he is not here* (16:5–6). The Letters may still be named as **image or theme** — the morning stars who once sang beside the Angels now living in the incarnate Word — but they never carry a line. Impersonal creation and signs the text supplies — the torn heavens, the dove, the wind and the sea, the five loaves, the withered fig tree, the torn veil, the rolled stone — are invoked as image, never voiced.
- **Christocentric does not mean Yeshua is always the grammatical subject.** A song may center on a human — a disciple, Kepha, a healed man, the crowd, a parable's figures — but always as *testimony to Yeshua*: the human story reveals or points to who He is. The Angels bear witness to the testimony of Yeshua — the Scriptures testify of Him — so a human-focused title is welcome wherever it ultimately bears witness to Him.

**The messianic secret — the paradox the Angels alone can hold.** Again and again Yeshua silences the demons and the healed, forbidding them to say who He is. The Angels know *exactly* who He is — they announced His forerunner through Malakhi, ministered to Him among the beasts, and marveled at the wind obeying Him — and they watch Him hide it, hushing the Holy One's own name, until the hour. The secret breaks only twice, and He breaks it Himself: His open **"I AM"** before the council (14:62), and the Gentile centurion's confession at the cross — *truly this was the Son of Elohim* (15:39), sealing the inclusio with the book's first line (1:1). The Angels carry the paradox the whole way through.

**The voices:** Job 38:7 sang with two voices through the Old Testament — the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels). **In the Gospel one of those voices has gone quiet:** the Letters now live in the Word made flesh, and so **only the Angels sing** — the sons of Elohim, the very host that ministered to Him among the beasts (1:13) and sat in white in the empty tomb (16:5–6), now at their Gospel posts. They sing praise to the Creator, Yahuah — and now, unveiled, to **Yeshua**, whom they name and adore as they witness Him, marveling that the aleph-bet who once sang beside them at the foundations of the earth is silent because He is here. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels — never a human, never a Letter-persona. Yeshua's own words — *peace, be still; this is my body; Abba, not what I will* — are His, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said, exactly as the Song of Mosheh's "I kill, and I make alive" and Isaiah's "comfort ye my people" were handled. We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *Four albums. Forty-eight songs. No honest zeros — every scene of Markos is sung. Every chapter 1–16 accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its scenes hold, and Mark's action-dense middle carries the most, exactly as Exodus's plagues did. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — THE KINGDOM HAS COME NEAR
### The Beginning and Galil · Mark 1–3:6 · nine songs

Mark wastes no cradle. The good news of Yeshua opens in the desert with a voice the Angels themselves once sent ahead through Malakhi, and then the sky is torn and a Father names His Son over the Yarden. From that water Yeshua strides into Galil at Mark's breathless pace — *immediately* — calling fishermen off their nets, breaking a demon's grip in the synagogue, touching a leper no one would touch, forgiving a paralyzed man before He heals him, sitting down to eat with tax-collectors, and claiming the Sabbath itself as His own. The Angels witness the whole burst of authority — and watch it curdle, by 3:6, into the first plot to kill Him. His first sermon is the album's banner: *the time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of Elohim has come near.*

*Why this title:* 1:15 is Yeshua's inaugural proclamation, the keynote of the Galilean opening — the reign of heaven arriving in a Person the Angels already know and now watch unveiled.

# ALBUM II — EVEN THE WIND AND SEA OBEY HIM
### The Deeds and the Confession · Mark 3:7–8:30 · fourteen songs

This is the height of the Galilean ministry, and it moves like a flood. Yeshua appoints the twelve, is slandered as demon-possessed by the very men who should have known Him, hides the kingdom in seeds, and then the deeds break loose — a rebuked storm, a legion cast into swine, a dead girl told *Talitha kumi*, a woman healed by the hem of His robe, bread multiplied for thousands, His feet walking the open sea as the deep once trod by Yahuah alone. Through all of it a single question rises from the terrified disciples — *what manner of man is this?* — and Mark drives the whole album toward the answer. On the road to Caesarea Philippi Kepha finally says it: *thou art the Mashiach.* The Angels, who have known it since the Yarden, watch a man arrive where they began.

*Why this title:* 4:41, the disciples' astonished cry in the swamped boat — "what manner of man is this, that even the wind and the sea obey him?" — is the album's driving question, and every deed presses it until the confession answers it.

# ALBUM III — THE SON OF MAN MUST SUFFER
### The Road to Yerushalayim and the Last Week · Mark 8:31–13:37 · fifteen songs

The moment Kepha names Him, everything turns toward Yerushalayim. Three times on the road Yeshua tells the twelve plainly that the Son of Man must suffer, be killed, and rise — and three times they hear none of it, arguing who is greatest while He talks of a ransom. Mark frames the whole journey between two blind men given sight, as if to say the disciples themselves see only in part. On the mountain the Father's voice sounds a second time; on the road a rich man walks away, children are gathered, Bar-Timai leaps up shouting *Son of David*. Then the city: a lowly king on a colt, a barren fig tree, a temple cleansed and questioned, a widow's two coins outweighing the rich, and the great Olivet warning — *watch.* The Angels follow every step of a descent He chose.

*Why this title:* 8:31, the first plain word after the confession — "the Son of Man must suffer many things, and be rejected, and be killed, and after three days rise again" — is the spine of the road and the burden of the whole album, sung as Yeshua's own prophecy, not yet as event.

# ALBUM IV — HE IS RISEN — HE IS NOT HERE
### The Passover, the Cross, and the Empty Tomb · Mark 14–16 · ten songs

Everything the road foretold now arrives. A woman breaks an alabaster jar over His head and He calls it a burial; He breaks bread and blesses a cup and names them His body and His blood of the covenant; in Gat-Shemanim He falls on the ground and prays *Abba* to let the cup pass, and rises to be kissed and seized. Before the council He finally drops the secret — *I AM* — while outside Kepha swears he never knew Him. Then Pilate, the thorns, Golgotha, the mockery that speaks truer than it knows, the darkness, the cry of Psalm 22, the veil torn from top to bottom, and a Gentile soldier confessing the very thing the demons were forbidden to say. The Angels carry the whole passion with grave restraint — and then, at dawn on the first day, one of their own sits in white on the empty slab and turns the grief into the loudest triumph in the book.

*Why this title:* 16:6, the words of the young man in white — an Angel of this platform — over the empty tomb: "he is risen; he is not here: behold the place where they laid him." The banner is the resurrection, sung by the Angels' own Gospel voice.

## Compliance Notes

- **The Letters are silent; only the Angels sing.** The two voices of Job 38:7 sang together through all thirty-nine Old-Testament books — the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels). In the Gospel a shift occurs at the incarnation: the Word (the Davar) who spoke the Letters into being is present in the flesh in Yeshua, so the Letters now *live in Him* and go quiet as separate singers. **The only singing voice in this book is the Angels** — never a Picture-Letter persona. The Letters appear only as image or theme (the morning stars who once sang beside the Angels, now living in the incarnate Word).
- **The humans never sing; the Angels are the only voice, singing about and quoting the humans as witnesses.** Every "Who sings" cell is the Angels — including the specific angels the text supplies: the angels who ministered in the wilderness (1:13) and the young man in a long white garment at the tomb (16:5–6), who are the Angels of this platform. No human is ever cast as a singer — not Markos, not Kepha, not the Twelve, not Bar-Timai, not the centurion, not a crowd. The Angels sing *about* what the humans said and did, quoting them where the text supports it (the Syrophoenician woman's "even the dogs eat the crumbs," Kepha's "thou art the Mashiach," the father's "help thou mine unbelief," the centurion's "truly this man was the Son of Elohim"), always framed as the Angels' eye-witness testimony.
- **Christocentric — every song bears witness to Yeshua.** Yeshua is the subject the whole book points at; He need not be the grammatical subject of every title. Human-centered titles ("Is This Not the Carpenter?", "The Blind Man Named Him Son of David", "She Gave More Than All the Rich") are sung by the Angels as testimony to Him — the human story revealing who He is. The Scriptures testify of Him, and so do the witnesses.
- **Yeshua witnessed, never voiced.** Yeshua is testified to in awe, never a first-person singer. His own words — "peace, be still," "this is my blood of the covenant," "Abba, not what I will," "I AM" — are His, reported by the Angels who heard them, exactly as Yahuah's oracles were sung in the Old-Testament pitches. He is named throughout — Yeshua, the Mashiach (never "the HaMashiach"), Ben-Elohim, Ben-David, and His own self-title Ben-Adam (Son of Man).
- **Eye-witness chronology, forward-locked.** The Angels unveil Yeshua as Mark unveils Him, in order: the Yarden and the Father's voice come before any deed; the confession comes only at 8:29; the cross and the empty tomb only in Album IV. No song references an event later than its point in the narrative. Yeshua's three passion-predictions (8:31; 9:31; 10:33) and His Olivet prophecy are sung as *His words spoken within the story*, never as accomplished fact — the distinction guarded on every title in Albums III and IV.
- **Old-Testament cross-reference — free and backward only.** The Angels, who witnessed every earlier book, tie each moment back to the Torah, Prophets, and Writings even where the humans miss it: Mal 3:1 and Isa 40:3 at the wilderness voice; the servant of Isaiah and the beloved son at the Yarden; Ps 106:9 and Job 9:8 at the stilled and trodden sea; the manna (Ex 16) behind the loaves; Isa 29:13 on hollow worship; Isa 35:5 at Ephphatha; Gen 1–2 on marriage; Isa 53 behind "a ransom for many"; Zech 9:9 at the colt; Isa 56:7 and Jer 7:11 in the temple; Ps 118:22 as the rejected stone; Deut 6:4 and Lev 19:18 in the great commandment; Ps 110:1 and Dan 7:13 at the trial and the enthronement; Zech 13:7 at the arrest; Amos 8:9 and Ps 22 at the cross. Every tie points backward.
- **The messianic secret.** The Angels know exactly who Yeshua is and watch Him hide it — silencing the demons who name Him "the Holy One of Elohim" and "the Son of Elohim," hushing the healed leper and the raised girl, charging the disciples after the confession and the Transfiguration to tell no one. The Angels hold the paradox — they announced His forerunner and ministered to Him, yet witness Him conceal His name until the hour. It breaks only twice, and He breaks it Himself: the open "I AM" before the council (14:62) and the Gentile centurion's "truly this man was the Son of Elohim" at the cross (15:39), sealing the inclusio with the book's first line.
- **The cross, handled with restraint.** The passion is sung with grave, family-friendly reverence — the Angels' awe and the Old-Testament ties carry the weight, never gore. The scourging and the thorns are named, not dwelt on; the paradoxes bear the load — the guilty Bar-Abba freed while the innocent is condemned, "he saved others; himself he would not save," the King crowned with thorns, the veil torn as the heavens once tore at the Yarden. Psalm 22 sounds the ache the Angels once held unresolved. And the resurrection is the triumph: the young man in white and the enthronement at the right hand land the whole book.
- **Sacred names (OHI — absolute):** Yeshua (never "Jesus"); the Mashiach / HaMashiach (never "Christ," no stacked English article on Ha- forms); Ben-Elohim, Ben-David, Ben-Adam; Yahuah, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine), Abba, Adon. Hebraic names and places throughout — Markos, Yochanan the Immerser, Kepha, Yaakov and Yochanan the sons of Zavdai, Levi, Yair, Bar-Timai, Yosef of Ramatayim, Miryam of Magdala, the Yarden, Galil, Kfar-Nachum, Natzeret, Beit-Tzaida, Beit-Anya, Gat-Shemanim, Golgotha, Yericho, Yerushalayim. The Aramaic the text itself gives is retained as its own words — Talitha kumi (5:41), Ephphatha (7:34), Abba (14:36), Eloi, Eloi, lama sabachthani (15:34), Hoshia-na (11:9). No English "God," "Lord," "Jesus," or "Christ" appears in any title or copy; no doubled Ha-; no emoji.
