**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Song of Songs is eight chapters of love poetry read as the tradition reads it: the covenant love between **Yahuah and His people** — the Beloved seeking, the bride longing, the love "strong as death" (8:6). It is one continuous song-cycle, so it is one album. Every song below is one movement; every unit boundary sits on the poem's own seams — the opening desire, the voice leaping on the mountains, the night search and the wedding procession, the garden set apart, the beloved at the door, the settled mutual belonging, and the great seal-of-love climax.
**The reading and the restraint:** the physical imagery of the text is rendered **chastely** and pointed to its covenant meaning — reverent, family-friendly at every line, nothing sensual. The Angels witness heaven's own longing for union with the people, and the people's longing answered. The recurring refrain — "I am my beloved's, and my beloved is mine" — and the charge "stir not up love until it please" are the spine.
**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator, Yahuah — and, from *within* each moment of the poem, toward the covenant love promised but not yet unveiled in its fullness. **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own hour, with no New-Testament hindsight — Yeshua is named and adored in the platform's framing, but inside a Song-of-Songs moment the Beloved who comes leaping upon the mountains, the seeking through the night streets, the love "strong as death… a very flame of Yah" (8:6, the book's one veiled use of the Name), and the "many waters" that cannot drown it (8:7) are carried as mystery and longing — never resolved forward to any later identification. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or the personified Letters — never a human voice (not the bride/Shulammite, not the beloved/king, not the daughters of Yerushalayim, not the watchmen) or anything derived from one; the lovers' words are *witnessed and testified to*, never voiced. Impersonal creation the text supplies (the vineyards, the rose of Sharon and the lily of the valleys, the little foxes, the seal upon the heart, the garden enclosed and the sealed spring, the banner of love, the many waters) may be *invoked as image*, never made the singer. We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

# ALBUM I — LOVE AS STRONG AS DEATH
### The Covenant Love of Yahuah and His People · Song of Songs 1–8 · seven songs

Eight chapters of love poetry, read as the tradition has always read them — the covenant love between Yahuah and His people, the Beloved who comes leaping upon the mountains and the bride who seeks Him through the night streets and will not let Him go. The Angels and the Letters witness the whole aching cycle of seeking and finding — the drawing and the running after, the first sounding of "my beloved is mine," the guarded wedding march up from the wilderness, the garden enclosed and the fountain sealed, the voice at the door and the door opened too late, the one undefiled dove chosen out of the many — and carry it to the climax where love is set as a seal, named strong as death, a very flame of Yah that many waters cannot drown. Not the lovers' own voices, but heaven's testimony to a love that will not be quenched.

*Why this title:* the whole cycle is built to arrive at 8:6 — "set me as a seal upon thine heart… for love is strong as death; jealousy is cruel as the grave." The covenant love the Song sings is not fragile longing but a bond unbreakable as the grave and unquenchable as the flame of Yah; the title names the theological anchor the whole book climbs toward, and the reading that keeps the poem reverent — a love strong enough to be sung of Yahuah and His people.

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the opening longing is the Veil — the ache stated, the drawing begun; the Beloved leaping down the mountains is the Approach, and the first "my beloved is mine" the first light; the guarded wedding march up from the wilderness is the Unveiling; the garden enclosed and sealed is the intimate Selah — the covenant set apart; the beloved at the door and the door opened too late is the Turn, the covenant ache at its sharpest; the settled mutual belonging answers it, "I am my beloved's" thundered as the Declaration; and the seal set on the heart — love strong as death, the flame of Yah the many waters cannot drown — lands the album as the Benediction. The Song is already built as a seeking that turns to finding and back again, circling three times through the charge "stir not up love until it please" (2:7; 3:5; 8:4), so the text's own cyclical order *is* the artistic shape; no reordering improves on it.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1 the opening longing and the vineyards · 2 the voice leaping on the mountains, the foxes, the first "mine" · 3 the night search and the wedding procession · 4 the bride all fair, the garden enclosed and the sealed spring · 5 the beloved at the door, the second search, the watchmen who wound · 6–7 the mutual belonging, the one dove, and the longing for the vineyards · 8 the seal, love strong as death, and "make haste, my beloved." Seven for seven.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine). The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy. The one veiled use of the Name in the book itself — *shalhevetyah*, "a very flame of Yah" (8:6) — is carried as the text gives it, noted as the book's single glimpse of the Name and never over-read. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **The reading and the restraint honored:** the Song is handled through the traditional Hebraic allegorical reading — the covenant love of Yahuah and His people. Grave restraint governs every line: no sensual or erotic detail; the physical imagery of the text (the garden, the fountain, the seeking) is rendered chastely and pointed to its covenant meaning; family-friendly throughout. The Angels witness the love; they never voice its intimacies.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** inside every song the covenant love is carried as mystery and longing — the Beloved leaping on the mountains, the ache of the One sought through the night, love strong as death and the flame no flood can drown. Yeshua is not named and the allegory is not resolved forward to any New-Testament identification; the divine-covenant reading stays within its own hour — heaven's love for the people, aching and unnamed in its fullness. The framing names and adores Yeshua; the songs, standing inside the poem, do not.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter — the Chet keyed to the enclosure and *chatum* ("sealed," the garden shut up and the fountain sealed), the Dalet to *delet* (the door the Beloved knocked upon), the Vav to the joining "and" of the belonging refrain, the Mem to the many waters that could not drown the flame. The bride, the beloved, the daughters of Yerushalayim, and the watchmen are *witnessed*, never made the singer; the lovers' words — "my beloved is mine," "set me as a seal" — are testified to from heaven's side, never sung as theirs. The vineyards, the rose of Sharon and the lily of the valleys, the little foxes, the banner of love, the guarded litter, the garden enclosed and the sealed spring, and the many waters are invoked as images, never voiced.
- **Picture Letters, never a count:** the Letters are named as personae where used; no total is ever stated and no gematria mechanism is sung. The text's own numbers are content, not method — the threescore valiant men about the litter (3:7), the threescore queens and fourscore concubines (6:8), the "chiefest among ten thousand" (5:10).
- **Craft:** no two titles in the set share a formula or cadence — the seven open on *Drawn / He / Threescore / A / She / Hers / Many*, and their shapes run from participle to chiasmus to the letter's own confession; each is recognizable from its title and passage; paradox is prized where the text makes it true (a garden that is a person, love equal in strength to death, waters that cannot drown a flame).
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — the oldest reading of this book — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
