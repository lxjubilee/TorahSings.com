# ZECHARIAH — THE FOUR ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book of Zecharyah

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Daniel - The Three Albums.md`, `pitches/Isaiah - The Ten Albums.md`, `pitches/Ezekiel - The Ten Albums.md`, and `pitches/Micah - The Three Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Zecharyah is fourteen chapters, one of the Twelve, and the most vision-and-angel-dense of them. Its seams are the book's own: the opening call to return (1:1–6); the **eight night visions** (1:7–6:8), each mediated and explained; the crowning of Yehoshua and the Branch (6:9–15); the fasting question and the promise to Tzion (7–8); and the two **"burdens" (massa)** — the first (9–11) and the second (12–14). One vision, one oracle, one song.

**The interpreting angel — Zecharyah's distinctive, and this record's cornerstone.** No book walks its prophet through vision after vision by the hand of one guide the way Zecharyah does. **"The angel that talked with me" — *ha-malakh ha-dover bi*** — steps in at the first vision and stays: he answers "what are these?" at the myrtle trees, the four horns, the flying scroll, the ephah, and the chariots; he wakes the prophet "as a man wakened out of his sleep" for the lampstand (4:1); he runs to explain and to send the second angel to the man with the measuring line (2:3–4). He is exactly the role Gavriel plays in Daniyel — **the Angel of this platform doing his own scriptural work, the explainer given his own voice.** Beside him stands **the angel of Yahuah** (*malakh Yahuah*), who cries the great intercession "how long wilt thou not have mercy?" among the myrtles (1:12) and who stands in the fourth vision to rebuke the accuser and command the filthy garments taken away (3:1–5). Both are the Angels of the text, sung and witnessed as the text sends them, and held distinct from any platform persona; they are never conflated with one.

**The messianic ache — held, never resolved forward.** Zecharyah carries some of the sharpest messianic material in the Twelve, and every strand is sung as **unresolved longing — no New-Testament hindsight, no Yeshua-unveiled inside a song.** The **Branch** — "I will bring forth my servant the Branch" (3:8) and "Behold the man whose name is The Branch… he shall build the temple of Yahuah" (6:12) — held as the mystery the Angels carry toward the One promised. The king "lowly, and riding upon an ass," who "shall speak peace unto the heathen" (9:9–10). The **thirty pieces of silver** weighed for the shepherd's price and cast to the potter in the house of Yahuah (11:12–13). "They shall look upon me whom they have pierced, and they shall mourn" (12:10). The **fountain opened** for sin (13:1). "Awake, O sword… smite the shepherd, and the sheep shall be scattered" (13:7), with the third part brought through the fire and refined. And the great close — the Day of Yahuah, the Mount of Olives split, living waters going out from Yerushalayim, "Yahuah shall be king over all the earth: in that day shall there be one Yahuah, and his name one" (14:9), and **HOLINESS TO YAHUAH** on the bells of the horses (14:20). Every one is held as the ache the Angels witness and cannot yet name.

**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels; **the interpreting angel and the angel of Yahuah are the Angels by their scriptural office.** They sing praise to the Creator — **Yahuah, Yahuah Tzeva'ot** — and, from *within* each moment of the book, toward the Branch and the everlasting kingdom longed for but not yet unveiled. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or a personified Letter — never a human (not Zecharyah, not Yehoshua the high priest, not Zerubbavel). The visions are sung **from the interpreting angel's own voice where the text gives him the explanation**, and Zecharyah is *witnessed*, never voiced; his own first person is quoted only when clearly framed as quotation. Yahuah's first-person oracles — "I am returned unto Yerushalayim with mercies" (1:16), "not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit" (4:6), "I will pour upon the house of David… the spirit of grace" (12:10), "Yahuah shall be king over all the earth" (14:9) — are **His own words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said.** Impersonal creation and sign the text supplies — the myrtle trees in the bottom, the patrolling horses, the four horns and four craftsmen, the measuring line, the filthy-then-clean garments, the stone with seven eyes, the lampstand and the two olive trees, the flying scroll, the ephah with its lead weight, the four chariots, the crown, the king on the ass, the thirty silver pieces, the fountain, the smitten shepherd, the split mountain, the living waters, the bells inscribed *Holiness to Yahuah* — is **invoked as image, never voiced.** The accuser (*ha-satan*) rebuked in the fourth vision is rendered as the text renders him — witnessed, never glamorized, never a singer.

> *Four albums. Twenty-nine songs. No honest zeros — every vision and every oracle of Zecharyah is sung. Every chapter 1–14 accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its visions and oracles hold, one per unit and never a split to pad. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — THE EIGHT NIGHT VISIONS AND THE BRANCH
### The Call, the Eight Visions, and the Crowning · Zechariah 1–6 · ten songs

A word comes in the eighth month: *turn ye unto me, and I will turn unto you.* Then, in one night, the heavens open eight times, and the interpreting angel walks the prophet through every one — riders returned from patrolling the earth, four horns and four who cast them down, a young man with a measuring line, a high priest reclothed while his accuser is silenced, a lampstand fed by two living trees, a curse on wings, wickedness sealed under lead and flown to Shinar, and four chariots loosed to the four winds. It ends at daybreak with a crown of silver and gold set on a priest's head and a name spoken over him — the Branch, who shall build the house of Yahuah. The Angels sing the visions the way the angel gave them: as questions asked, and answered.

*Why this title:* the album is the one night of revelation (1:7–6:8) the interpreting angel walks the prophet through, framed by the call that opens it (1:1–6) and the crowning that lands it — "Behold the man whose name is The Branch" (6:12), the promise every vision leans toward, held unresolved.

# ALBUM II — I AM RETURNED UNTO TZION
### The Fasting Question and the Promise to Tzion · Zechariah 7–8 · five songs

Two years on, men come to ask a question: should we go on weeping and fasting in the fifth month, as we have done these seventy years? The answer runs two chapters. First it turns the question back — *did ye fast unto me, even to me?* — and recalls the plain thing the fathers would not do: execute true judgment, show mercy, and they made their hearts adamant and were scattered with a whirlwind. Then the promise breaks: *I am returned unto Tzion.* Old men and old women shall yet sit in the streets, each with a staff for age, and the streets shall be full of boys and girls playing. The Angels witness the fasts turned to feasts, and ten men of the nations catching hold of a single Jew's sleeve.

*Why this title:* 8:3 — "thus saith Yahuah; I am returned unto Tzion, and will dwell in the midst of Yerushalayim" — the hinge of the whole two-chapter answer, the word that turns mourning-fasts into feasts and empties the seventy years of their weeping.

# ALBUM III — BEHOLD, THY KING COMETH
### The First Burden · Zechariah 9–11 · seven songs

The first burden falls. Yahuah's word marches down through Hadrach and Dammesek, past Tzor and Tzidon and the Philistine cities, until He pitches camp around His own house so no oppressor passes through again. And into that cleared land the king comes — not on a war-horse but lowly, riding upon an ass, speaking peace to the nations. The Angels witness the prisoners of hope turned back to the stronghold, Tzion's sons raised against the sons of Yavan, and the rain asked of Yahuah and not of idols. Then the light darkens: a shepherd of the flock of slaughter, two staves named Beauty and Bands snapped, thirty pieces of silver weighed out and flung to the potter, and a worthless shepherd left over a scattered flock.

*Why this title:* 9:9 — "behold, thy King cometh unto thee: he is just, and having salvation; lowly, and riding upon an ass" — the heart of the first burden and the hope the whole march reaches for, held unresolved before the burden darkens into the priced and abandoned shepherd.

# ALBUM IV — YAHUAH SHALL BE KING OVER ALL THE EARTH
### The Second Burden · Zechariah 12–14 · seven songs

The second burden begins with the One who stretched the heavens and formed the spirit of man within him, and He makes Yerushalayim a cup that sets all who surround her reeling, a burdensome stone that gashes every people who tries to heave it away. Then the deepest turn in the book: He pours out the spirit of grace, and *they shall look upon me whom they have pierced, and they shall mourn* — family by family, apart. A fountain is opened for sin, the idols and false prophets cut off; the sword awakes against the shepherd and the sheep scatter, and a third part is brought through the fire and refined as silver. And the Day comes — the Mount of Olives split east and west, living waters flowing out summer and winter, Yahuah king over all the earth, one Yahuah and His name one, and even the bells of the horses reading *Holiness to Yahuah.*

*Why this title:* 14:9 — "and Yahuah shall be king over all the earth: in that day shall there be one Yahuah, and his name one" — where the second burden lands after the cup of trembling, the pierced One mourned, the fountain, and the smitten shepherd: the whole book's horizon, held as longing.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yahuah Tzeva'ot, Elohim, Yeshua, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine). The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy — Elohim and Yahuah stand in their place. Hebraic forms are used honestly throughout — Zecharyah, Yehoshua (the high priest — Yeshua reserved to the Son and the platform framing), Zerubbavel, Yerushalayim, Tzion, Yehudah, Yisrael, Ephrayim, Yosef, Beit-El, Yavan, Levanon, Bashan, Yarden, Dammesek, Chamath, Tzor, Tzidon, Ekron, Mitzrayim, Ashur, Shinar, Sukkot. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled English article on *Ha-* forms — *ha-malakh ha-dover bi* and *ha-satan* named only in this framing, never stacked with an English "the"). No internal labels appear anywhere listener-facing; no emoji.
- **The interpreting angel and the angel of Yahuah honored:** **"the angel that talked with me"** (*ha-malakh ha-dover bi*) is the through-line singer of Album I — the explainer given his own voice, answering "what are these?" across the visions (1:9, 19; 4:1; 5:2; 6:4) and running to send the second angel to the man with the measuring line (2:3–4), exactly the office Gavriel holds in Daniyel. **The angel of Yahuah** sings his own intercession "how long wilt thou not have mercy?" (1:12) and stands to rebuke the accuser and command the garments taken away (3:1–5). Both are the Angels of the text, sung as the text sends them, held distinct from any platform persona and never conflated with one. The accuser (*ha-satan*) is witnessed and silenced, never glamorized, never a singer.
- **The messianic ache honored:** the Branch (3:8; 6:12), the lowly king on the ass who speaks peace to the nations (9:9–10), the thirty pieces cast to the potter (11:12–13), "whom they have pierced" and the mourning (12:10), the fountain opened for sin (13:1), the smitten shepherd with the refined third (13:7–9), and the Day of Yahuah — living waters, "one Yahuah, and his name one," and "Holiness to Yahuah" on the bells (14) — are all sung as unresolved longing. No New-Testament hindsight, no cross, no fulfillment language, no Yeshua-unveiled inside a song. The framing names and adores Yeshua; the songs, standing inside Zecharyah's own hour, never do. **12:10 is held with special care:** the Angels carry the mystery of the pierced One and the family-by-family mourning toward the One promised, testifying to Yahuah's oracle ("they shall look upon me whom they have pierced") in awe, and never naming it forward.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter. Zecharyah, Yehoshua the high priest, and Zerubbavel are *witnessed*, never made the singer; the prophet's own first person is quoted only when clearly framed as quotation. Yahuah's first-person oracles — "I am returned to Yerushalayim with mercies" (1:16), "not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit" (4:6), "I am returned unto Tzion" (8:3), "I will pour… the spirit of grace" (12:10), "Yahuah shall be king over all the earth" (14:9) — are His words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said. The myrtle trees, the patrolling horses, the four horns and four craftsmen, the measuring line, the filthy-then-clean garments, the stone with seven eyes, the lampstand and the two olive trees, the flying scroll, the ephah with its lead weight, the four chariots, the crown, the king on the ass, the thirty silver pieces, the fountain, the smitten shepherd, the split mountain, the living waters, and the inscribed bells are invoked as images, never voiced.
- **Text-numbers as content; the Letters never a count:** the eight visions, the four horns and four craftsmen, the seven eyes on the stone, the seven lamps, the two olive trees, the four chariots and four spirits/winds, the seventy years, the thirty pieces of silver, the two staves, and the two-thirds cut off with the third refined (13:8–9) are all sung as the text's own numbers — content, never a gematria mechanism. The Picture Letters are named as personae where used; no total is ever stated.
- **Restraint:** the marching judgment of the first burden (9:1–8), the wail over Levanon (11:1–3), the idol shepherd (11:15–17), the smitten shepherd (13:7), and the tumult among the nations (14:12–15) are handled with grave, family-friendly restraint, witnessed from heaven's side, the harshest images named but never rendered for shock.
- **Craft:** no two titles share a formula or cadence within any album, and each is recognizable from its title and passage; paradox is prized where the text makes it true — a city without walls walled by fire, a high priest reclothed clean while his accuser stands silenced, a temple finished "not by might, but by my Spirit," a king who rides a donkey and speaks peace, a shepherd priced at thirty and flung to the potter, living water from a landlocked city, and horse-bells turned into holy vessels. Zecharyah 9:9 is titled off its own image ("Lowly, Riding Upon an Ass") so it does not twin any shipped record.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — the vision-and-burden reading of the Twelve's most angel-dense book — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
