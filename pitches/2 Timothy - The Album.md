**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. 2 Timothy is a **letter** — not narrative, not oracle, not law: Shaul's final letter, written from a Roman chain with his death in plain view (4:6), to Timotheos his "dearly beloved son." Its "units" are the letter's own movements — the greeting and the inherited faith and the gift to be rekindled (1:1–7), the gospel Shaul suffers for, "who hath abolished death," and "I know whom I have believed" (1:8–18), the good soldier, the athlete, the farmer, and the faithful saying (2:1–13), the sure foundation and the vessel unto honour (2:14–26), the perilous last days and the Elohim-breathed scriptures (3:1–17), the charge to preach, the good fight, and the crown at His appearing (4:1–8), and the lonely farewell where the Adon stands and the closing greetings land (4:9–22). The seams are Shaul's own: the thanksgiving-formula ("I thank Elohim… when I call to remembrance"), the "wherefore" hinges (1:6; 2:1), the faithful-saying formula (2:11), the "but" turns ("but continue thou," 3:14), the solemn "I charge thee" (4:1), and the personal roll that closes the letter.

**THE NEW-TESTAMENT SHIFT — the Letters fall silent; only the Angels sing.** Through all thirty-nine books of the Old Testament the two voices of Job 38:7 sang *together* — the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels), aching toward a promised One they could not yet name. **At the incarnation that changes.** The Word (the Davar) by whom every letter was made is now made flesh; the aleph-bet lives in Him. The morning stars have gone quiet, gathered up into the Word made flesh — so through the New Covenant **the only singers are the Angels — the sons of Elohim — never a Picture-Letter persona.** This letter names the reason in its brightest line: Yeshua the Mashiach "hath abolished death, and hath brought life and immortality to light through the gospel" (1:10) — the Word the letters once carried, now made flesh, has broken open the grave the letters could never open. The Letters are the marvel the Angels *testify to,* not a voice of their own.

**Epistle, not narrative — the finished work proclaimed freely, and one aching longing held open.** In the Gospels the Angels were eye-witnesses, forward-locked to the story's order — never naming the cross before it came. **A letter has no biography of Yeshua to lock:** written *after* the resurrection — indeed at the very end of Shaul's life, in bonds, "ready to be offered" — the accomplished work is proclaimed openly and in full. So the Angels sing freely of Yeshua the Mashiach "of the seed of David… raised from the dead" (2:8), who "hath abolished death, and hath brought life and immortality to light" (1:10). The forward-lock does not vanish — it **relocates** to the one thing this farewell still holds unresolved, and holds with a dying man's whole heart: **His appearing.** "I have fought a good fight, I have finished my course, I have kept the faith: henceforth there is laid up for me a crown of righteousness, which the Adon, the righteous judge, shall give me at that day: and not to me only, but unto all them that love his appearing" (4:7–8). That appearing — the crown not yet given, the Adon not yet seen — is the album's aching center, kept open exactly as the Old-Testament catalog held its ache unresolved.

**Yeshua witnessed, never voiced.** The singer is always the Angels; **Yeshua is testified to in awe — never a first-person singer.** Every "he abideth faithful," every "the Adon knoweth them that are his," every "the Adon stood with me" is the Angels reporting and marveling at what Shaul wrote of Him — never Yeshua's own voice, exactly as Yahuah's oracles were handled across the Old Covenant. **Christocentric — about "the who":** the whole letter is a dying emissary's testimony to the person of his Adon, and every song lands on Him.

**Old-Covenant cross-reference — always open, and backward only.** The Angels who witnessed all thirty-nine books freely tie every moment *back* into the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings: Yeshua "of the seed of David" (2:8), tied to the oath sworn to David (2 Shemuel 7:12–13; Ps 132:11); the twofold seal on the sure foundation — "the Adon knoweth them that are his" and "let every one that nameth the name of the Adon depart from iniquity" (2:19) — tied straight to Korach's rebellion, where "the Adon will shew who are his" and Israel is told to "depart… from the tents of these wicked men" (Bemidbar 16:5, 26; cf. Yeshayahu 52:11); the vessel unto honour, meet for the Master's use (2:21), tied to the potter and the clay (Yeshayahu 64:8; Yirmeyahu 18:1–6); Yannes and Yambres who "withstood Mosheh" (3:8), tied to the magicians who matched then failed before the rod at Pharaoh's court (Shemot 7:11–12); the Adon "the righteous judge" (4:8), tied to the Judge of all the earth who does right (Bereshit 18:25; Ps 7:11); and the deliverance "out of the mouth of the lion" (4:17), tied to the shut lions' mouths of Daniyel and the cry of the psalm, "save me from the lion's mouth" (Daniyel 6:22; Ps 22:21). No forward, post-letter reference is used.

# ALBUM I — A CROWN AT HIS APPEARING
### The Last Charge, the Good Fight, and the Longing for His Appearing · 2 Timothy 1–4 · seven songs

This is the last thing Shaul ever wrote. The letter is short and its air is the air of a cell with the door about to open onto death — and yet it is not morbid but steady, tender, and finally triumphant. A dying father charges a beloved son: rekindle the gift, and put away fear, for Elohim gave a spirit not of cowardice but of power and love and a sound mind. He is not ashamed of his chain, because he knows the One he suffers for — the One who "hath abolished death, and hath brought life and immortality to light." He hands Timotheos three pictures to live by — the soldier who will not be entangled, the athlete who strives lawfully, the farmer who labours first — and the faithful saying that suffering ends in reigning, for "he abideth faithful: he cannot deny himself." He anchors him on the sure foundation whose seal reads *the Adon knoweth them that are his,* warns him of perilous last days and of men with a form of godliness who deny its power, and sets against them the scriptures a child of the covenant knew — every one of them breathed out by Elohim. Then comes the charge that gathers the whole letter: *preach the word.* And then the summit — the good fight fought, the course finished, the faith kept, and the crown laid up, not for Shaul only but "unto all them that love his appearing." He ends alone: forsaken by men, upheld by his Adon, delivered from the lion's mouth, and kept for the heavenly kingdom. The Angels sing all of it, quoting Shaul, testifying to Yeshua, and holding the one longing the letter leaves open — His appearing.

*Why this title:* 4:8 is the letter's summit and its aching center — "a crown of righteousness, which the Adon, the righteous judge, shall give me at that day: and not to me only, but unto all them that love his appearing." The crown is in the righteous Judge's hand, and it is given *at His appearing;* the whole album hangs from that not-yet.

## Compliance Notes

- **The New-Testament shift honored — the Letters fall silent, only the Angels sing:** the two voices of Job 38:7 sang *together* through all thirty-nine Old-Testament books; in the New Covenant the morning stars (the Picture Letters) have gone quiet and now live in the Word made flesh, so **every song is sung by the Angels alone** — never a Picture-Letter persona. Every "Who sings" cell across all seven songs is the Angels. The letter names the reason itself: Yeshua "hath abolished death, and hath brought life and immortality to light through the gospel" (1:10) — the Word the letters carried, now made flesh, has broken open the grave.
- **The humans never sing — the Angels are the only voice:** not one song is voiced by a human. Shaul, Timotheos, Loïs, Eunike, and Onesiphoros never sing; the Angels sing *about* what Shaul wrote and freely *quote* him — "stir up the gift," "I know whom I have believed," "if we suffer, we shall also reign," "the Adon knoweth them that are his," "I have fought a good fight," "the Adon stood with me" — always framed from their own heavenly-witness perspective, testifying to Yeshua. 2 Timothy names no angel in its text, so the Angels here sing as they always do — as the witnessing host of Job 38:7 — not as any text-supplied character; where a song addresses Timotheos in the second person ("fan into flame," "watch thou in all things"), it is the witnesses carrying Shaul's charge, never a human taking the microphone. A human-centered line means the Angels bear witness to what Shaul wrote *as testimony to Yeshua;* it never means a human sings.
- **Christocentric — about "the who":** every song lands on the person of the Adon — the One who abolished death, the seed of David raised, the faithful One who cannot deny Himself, the One who knows His own, the righteous Judge who holds the crown, and the One who stood when every man had fled. The album title, "A Crown at His Appearing," lands christocentrically: the crown is what Yeshua the righteous Judge gives at His appearing, and that appearing — His, not Shaul's reward — is the aching center.
- **Yeshua witnessed, never first-person-voiced:** Yeshua is reported and marveled at throughout — "who hath abolished death," "he abideth faithful," "the Adon shall give me… a crown," "the Adon stood with me" — never given a first-person singer. His work is testified to, exactly as Yahuah's oracles were sung by the witnesses across the Old Covenant.
- **The epistle forward-lock — relocated to the appearing:** with no biography of Yeshua to keep in order, the finished work is proclaimed freely and fully — the abolished grave (1:10) and Yeshua of David's seed raised from the dead (2:8). The single thing held *unresolved* is His **appearing** — "the crown… which the Adon, the righteous judge, shall give me at that day: and not to me only, but unto all them that love his appearing" (4:8), and His judging "at his appearing and his kingdom" (4:1) — carried as the album's one aching, forward-looking longing (song 6), never as an event already narrated. This is a dying man's letter, so the longing is intensely present and kept open to the last.
- **Old-Covenant cross-reference present and backward-only:** every tie reaches *back* — the oath to David behind "of the seed of David" (2 Shemuel 7:12–13; Ps 132:11); Korach's rebellion behind the twofold seal, "the Adon will shew who are his… depart from the tents of these wicked men" (Bemidbar 16:5, 26; cf. Yeshayahu 52:11); the potter and the clay behind the vessel unto honour (Yeshayahu 64:8; Yirmeyahu 18); the magicians of Pharaoh who matched then failed before the rod, behind Yannes and Yambres who "withstood Mosheh" (Shemot 7:11–12); the Judge of all the earth who does right behind "the righteous judge" (Bereshit 18:25; Ps 7:11); and the shut lions' mouths of Daniyel and "save me from the lion's mouth" behind the deliverance "out of the mouth of the lion" (Daniyel 6:22; Ps 22:21). No forward, post-letter reference is used.
- **Restraint — a death faced with dignity, not morbidity:** Shaul writing with his execution in view ("ready to be offered," "the time of my departure is at hand"), the perilous last days, and the lion's mouth are handled with grave, family-friendly restraint — witnessed from heaven's side, the Old-Covenant ties and the Angels' awe carrying the weight, never luridness or gore. The triumph — the good fight fought, the crown laid up, the Adon standing, the deliverance unto His heavenly kingdom — is triumph.
- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — **Yeshua** (never "Jesus"), the **Mashiach** / **Yeshua the Mashiach** / **the Mashiach Yeshua** (never "Christ"; no doubled English article on *Ha-* forms — "the Mashiach," never "the HaMashiach"), **Ben-Elohim** / the Son, **Yahuah**, **Elohim** (wherever the received text names the Deity in English; 3:16's "inspiration" clause rendered "given by the breath of Elohim" — Elohim-breathed; 3:17's "man of the Deity" rendered "the man of Elohim"), **Ruach HaKodesh** (feminine; rendered **Ruach Kodesh** where preceded by English "the," so 1:14 reads "keep by the Ruach Kodesh which dwelleth in us"), **Abba** / the Father, and the **Adon / Adonai** (never "Lord"; "our Saviour Yeshua the Mashiach," 1:10, keeps "Saviour"). Hebraic and Grecized names throughout — Shaul, Timotheos, Loïs, Eunike, Mosheh, David, Yannes and Yambres, Phygellos, Hermogenes, Onesiphoros, Hymenaeus, Philetos, Demas, Loukas, Markos, Karpos, Alexandros, Prisca and Aquila, Erastos, Trophimos, and the places Antiochia, Ikonion, Lystra, Troas, and Miletos. **No English "God," "Lord," "Jesus," or "Christ" appears anywhere in any title or copy;** the Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note; no internal labels; no emoji; no forbidden path characters in the album title.
- **Craft:** no two titles share a formula or cadence — the seven run *Fan Into Flame the Gift / I Know Whom I Have Believed / If We Suffer, We Shall Reign / The Adon Knoweth Them That Are His / All Scripture Is Elohim-Breathed / They That Love His Appearing / No Man Stood With Me, But One*, moving from the kindled-ember imperative, to the first-person confession of the safe deposit, to the conditional that ends in reigning, to the sealed foundation, to the breathed-out word, to the aching noun-phrase of the appearing, to the paradox of the forsaken man upheld by One. Each is recognizable from its title and passage, and the paradox is prized where the text makes it true (an ember rekindled at the edge of death; suffering that ends in reigning; a man abandoned by every friend and never once alone).

- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
