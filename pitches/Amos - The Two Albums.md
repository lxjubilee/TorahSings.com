# AMOS — THE TWO ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book of the Herdman of Tekoa

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, `pitches/Job - The Seven Albums.md`, `pitches/Isaiah - The Ten Albums.md`, and `pitches/Hosea - The Two Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Amos is nine chapters and one of the Twelve — the shepherd-prophet, a herdman and dresser of sycomore trees from Tekoa, taken from the flock two years before the earthquake. Its "units" are the book's own three great seams: the roll-call oracle-formula ("for three transgressions of X, and for four, I will not turn away the punishment thereof") that marches around the nations and comes home to Israel (1–2); the "hear this word" oracles of the lawsuit (3–6); and the five visions with the two encounters (7–9). The book splits at the visions (7:1) into two records — the roar and the oracles, then the visions and the raising.

**The roll-call, and the roar (how the refrain and the numbers are handled).** The signature of Amos is the marching refrain — *for three transgressions of Damascus, and for four; of Azzah, and for four; of Tzor… of Edom… of Ammon… of Moav… of Yehudah… of Israel.* Eight times the count climbs, lulling the hearer into applause for judgment on the neighbors, until the eighth turn snaps home on Israel itself. That refrain and its numbers are sung as **the text's own content — the roll marched, the count kept — never as a gematria mechanism and never as a total of the Picture Letters.** Under it all runs the book's keynote: *Yahuah will roar from Zion* (1:2), and *the lion hath roared, who will not fear?* (3:8) — the roar that opens the book, that recurs, and that compels the prophet to speak. And the three hymn-fragments the text plants in its own oracles — *he that formeth the mountains, and createth the wind… Yahuah, the Elohim of hosts, is his name* (4:13); *seek him that maketh the seven stars and Orion… Yahuah is his name* (5:8); *Adonai Yahuah Tzeva'ot… buildeth his stories in the heaven… calleth for the waters of the sea, Yahuah is his name* (9:6) — are sung as one creation-praise triad by the Angels, threaded through the judgment like a held chord.

**On-theme, and a cornerstone-adjacent song — Amos 5:8.** *Seek him that maketh the seven stars (the Pleiades, Kimah) and Orion (Kesil)… and turneth the shadow of death into the morning… Yahuah is his name.* This is a direct morning-stars / Job-38:7 resonance, and the making of the constellations is given to the Angels there — the same host that sang when those stars were first set. "Who Made the Seven Stars and Orion" (Album I, song 8) is sung from inside that making, a jewel beside Job 38:7.

**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator — **Yahuah** — and, from *within* each moment of the book, toward the raising longed for but not yet unveiled. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or a personified Letter — never a human (not Amos the herdman of Tekoa, not Amatzyah the priest of Beit-El, not Yerav'am, not the kine of Bashan, not the ones at ease in Zion). Amos's own protest — "I was no prophet… but a herdman, and a gatherer of sycomore fruit; and Yahuah took me" (7:14–15) — is **witnessed and testified to from heaven's side, never voiced as his own first person.** Yahuah's first-person oracles that fill the book — "you only have I known of all the families of the earth" (3:2); "seek me, and live" (5:4); "I hate, I despise your feast days" (5:21); "let judgment run down as waters" (5:24); "I will raise up the tabernacle of David that is fallen" (9:11) — are **His own words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said** (the Deuteronomy / Job / Isaiah / Hosea model). Impersonal creation the text supplies (the lion that roars, the plumb line dropped by the wall, the basket of summer fruit, the cart pressed with sheaves, the seven stars and Orion He made, the shepherd tearing scraps from the lion's mouth, the sieve that loses no grain, the mountains dropping sweet wine) is **invoked as image, never voiced.**

**Narrative-time — the messianic ache held, never resolved forward.** No New-Testament hindsight anywhere. The book's great hope is carried within its own hour: **"in that day will I raise up the tabernacle of David that is fallen, and close up the breaches thereof, and raise up his ruins, and build it as in the days of old"** (9:11) — the fallen booth of David raised, given its full weight and held as unresolved longing, the collapsed shelter of the royal house not yet standing. So too the plowman overtaking the reaper and the mountains dropping sweet wine (9:13). And **"the famine… not a famine of bread… but of hearing the words of Yahuah"** (8:11) — the word withheld, on-theme for the Letters, sung as the ache of a land that would not read the carriers of the word. No Yeshua, no cross, no fulfillment language inside a song. David is named because the text names him (9:11), his line's far end held as ache.

> *Two albums. Twenty-one songs. No honest zeros — every oracle and vision of Amos is sung. All nine chapters accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its oracles and visions hold, one per unit and never a split to pad. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — THE LION HAS ROARED
### The Roll-Call and the Oracles · Amos 1–6 · twelve songs

The book opens with a sound: Yahuah roaring from Zion until the shepherds' pastures mourn and the top of Karmel withers. Under that roar the count begins — for three transgressions and for four — marching around the nations who cheer to hear their enemies weighed, then turning without warning onto Yehudah, and onto Israel itself, who sold the righteous for silver and the poor for a pair of shoes. The Angels keep the roll and carry the whole long lawsuit that follows: the election that condemns, the altars of Beit-El and the ivory houses, the fat cows of Bashan, the fivefold "yet ye have not returned unto me," the Maker of the Pleiades and Orion who bids a fallen people seek Him and live, the day of Yahuah that is darkness and not light, the feasts He cannot bear answered by justice demanded to roll like a river, and the ones at ease who feel nothing for the ruin of Yoseph.

*Why this title:* the roar is the book's keynote and the album's unifying sound — "Yahuah will roar from Zion" (1:2) and "the lion hath roared, who will not fear? Adonai Yahuah hath spoken, who can but prophesy?" (3:8) — the voice under which every oracle from the roll-call to the last woe is spoken.

# ALBUM II — THE FALLEN BOOTH OF DAVID
### The Visions and the Raising · Amos 7–9 · nine songs

The book turns from oracle to vision: *thus hath Adonai Yahuah shewed me.* A swarm of locusts, and a devouring fire — each shown, and each turned back by the plea "by whom shall Yaakov arise? for he is small." Then a plumb line dropped in the midst of the people, and no reprieve; a priest at Beit-El who tells the seer to flee, and heaven's record that this was no prophet but a herdman taken from the flock; a basket of summer fruit whose very ripeness spells the end; a famine, not of bread, but of the word withheld; Adonai standing beside the altar where no pit is deep enough to hide. And then, past every ending, the thing the visions were climbing toward — a sieve that loses not one grain, a fallen shelter of branches raised and its breaches closed, and a harvest so vast the plowman overtakes the reaper and the mountains run with sweet wine. The Angels hold the raising as longing, its end not yet shown.

*Why this title:* the album lands on 9:11 — "in that day will I raise up the tabernacle of David that is fallen, and close up the breaches thereof… and build it as in the days of old" — the collapsed booth of the royal house raised again; the visions are the long descent into ending, and the fallen booth is where the descent is answered, held by the Angels as unresolved ache.

Nine for nine — the first two visions held as one song (the pair Amos turned back), each remaining vision and encounter given its own movement, and the restoration split so that the fallen booth (9:11) and the plowman's harvest (9:13) each carry their full weight.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine), and the text's own honest titles as it uses them: Adonai Yahuah, Yahuah Tzeva'ot, "Yahuah, the Elohim of hosts" (the doxologies), El. The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy — Elohim and Yahuah stand in their place, and "the day of Yahuah" renders the text's *yom YHWH*. Place and person names are the text's own Hebrew, rendered honestly — Tekoa, Beit-El, Gilgal, Shomron, Zion, Yerushalayim, Karmel, Bashan; Yaakov, Yitzchak, Yoseph, David; Damascus, Azzah, Tzor, Edom, Ammon, Moav, Yehudah, Kush, Peleshet, Kaphtor, Aram, Kir; the star-idols Sikkuth and Kiyyun; the Pleiades (Kimah) and Orion (Kesil); Amatzyah and Yerav'am (witnessed, never voiced). The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **The roll-call refrain and the numbers:** "for three transgressions… and for four" and every count in the book are sung as the text's own content — the roll marched and the count kept — never as a gematria mechanism and never as a total of the Picture Letters. The eight refrain-oracles are held as two songs so the rhetorical trap (applause for the neighbors, then the snap onto Israel) lands as the text builds it.
- **The star-maker honored (the cornerstone-adjacent song):** Amos 5:8 — "seek him that maketh the seven stars and Orion… Yahuah is his name" — is sung from *inside* the making of the constellations by the Angels, the morning stars who sang when those stars were first set (Job 38:7), a jewel beside the platform's cornerstone. The three hymn-fragments of Amos (4:13; 5:8–9; 9:5–6) are carried as one creation-praise triad threaded through the judgment.
- **The fallen booth given full weight (narrative-time gate):** Amos 9:11 — "I will raise up the tabernacle of David that is fallen… and build it as in the days of old" — is the album banner and its own climactic song ("I Will Build It as of Old"), sung as unresolved longing: the collapsed shelter of the royal house not yet standing, its end not yet shown to the singers. So too the plowman overtaking the reaper and the mountains dropping sweet wine (9:13), and the famine "not of bread… but of hearing the words of Yahuah" (8:11), the word withheld. David is named because the text names him (9:11), his line's far end held as ache. No Yeshua, no cross, no fulfillment language inside a song. The framing names and adores Yeshua; the songs, standing inside Amos's own hour, do not.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter — the Letters keyed to the carriers of the word withheld in the famine (8:11), the morning stars keyed to the making of the seven stars and Orion (5:8). Amos the herdman of Tekoa, Amatzyah the priest, Yerav'am, the kine of Bashan, the ones at ease in Zion, and the swindlers of the ephah are *witnessed*, never made the singer; Amos's protest "I was no prophet… but a herdman" (7:14–15) is testified to from heaven's side, never voiced as his own first person. Yahuah's first-person oracles — "you only have I known," "seek me, and live," "I hate your feast days," "let justice roll down," "I will raise the fallen booth" — are His words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said. The roaring lion, the plumb line, the basket of summer fruit, the cart pressed with sheaves, the shepherd's torn scraps, the sieve, and the mountains dropping wine are invoked as images, never voiced.
