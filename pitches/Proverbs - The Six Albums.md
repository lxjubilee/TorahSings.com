# PROVERBS — THE SIX ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book of Wisdom's Cry

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, and `pitches/Job - The Seven Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Proverbs is wisdom **poetry**, not narrative; it has almost no story. Its "units" are the text's own labeled collections and the discourse-movements of chapters 1–9, and its scattered sentence-proverbs are gathered into coherent **thematic** songs — the tongue, the sluggard and the ant, wealth and the poor, the adulteress and fidelity, friendship, the king and justice, the fear of Yahuah — one song per real theme or unit, never one-per-verse, never padding, never collapsing a genuine seam. Where a song gathers proverbs strewn across many chapters, the tracklist says so (methodology §3.3).

**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator — **Yahuah** throughout, and **Eloah / El / Elohim** where the text's own mouth uses them (Agur's oracle). **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or a personified Letter — never a human (not Shlomo, not Agur, not Lemuel, not Lemuel's mother). The father-to-son address that runs through the book is **witnessed**, never voiced. **Lady Wisdom (Chokmah)**, who cries in the streets (1, 8, 9), and **Lady Folly** are personifications the text itself supplies — they are **invoked as image and witnessed**, never taken as the singing voice: the Angels sing *about* Wisdom crying and Wisdom rejoicing beside the Maker, they do not become her. Impersonal creation the text supplies — the ant with no ruler, the honeycomb, the apples of gold in settings of silver, the well-built house on its seven pillars, the far-more-precious rubies, the eagle in the air, the four small wise things — is invoked as image, never voiced. **Narrative-time rule:** no New-Testament hindsight. Proverbs 8's Wisdom present beside Yahuah before the world was — *"when He appointed the foundations of the earth, then I was by Him… rejoicing always before Him"* (8:27–30) — is a direct companion-image to Job 38:7, the morning stars singing as the foundations were laid; it is carried as mystery and wonder, never resolved forward to any later identification. Agur's question *"what is His name, and what is His Son's name?"* (30:4) is held open as ache, never answered. Yeshua and Ruach HaKodesh (feminine) live in the platform's framing, never inside a song here.

> *Six albums. Forty-seven songs. No honest zeros — every chapter of Proverbs is accounted for. The fear of Yahuah is the spine: it opens the book (1:7), it is named the beginning of wisdom at the hinge of the discourse (9:10), and it crowns the final acrostic (31:30).*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its themes hold. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — WHERE ALL KNOWING BEGINS
### The Prologue and the Calls to Wisdom · Proverbs 1–4 · eight songs

The book opens not with a story but with a summons: a father teaching, a purpose stated, and a single sentence set like a keystone over everything that follows — *the fear of Yahuah is the beginning of knowledge.* Then Wisdom herself takes to the streets and cries aloud to the simple, the search for her is likened to mining for silver, and a father hands down what his own father gave him — get wisdom, guard the heart, walk the path that only brightens. The witnesses overhear it all, and mark the first glimpse of the jewel the whole book keeps circling: Yahuah founded the earth by wisdom.

*Why this title:* the album's keystone (1:7) is the sentence every proverb after it depends on — knowledge does not begin in cleverness but in the fear of Yahuah, and the whole record is built on that first door.

# ALBUM II — TWO HOUSES ON THE SAME STREET
### The Adulteress, Wisdom at the Founding, the Two Banquets · Proverbs 5–9 · eight songs

Two women call from two doors on the same street, and the whole discourse is the space between them. One drips honey and ends in wormwood; the other was the Maker's own delight before the world was made. Between the warnings the father sets down hard, plain wisdom — flee a rash pledge, learn from the ant, count the things Yahuah hates — and then the discourse lifts into its highest air: Wisdom beside Yahuah at the founding of the earth, rejoicing before Him, an image the Angels remember from their own first morning. It closes where the book's spine is restated — the fear of Yahuah is the beginning of wisdom — with two banquets set and every passer-by made to choose.

*Why this title:* chapters 5–9 are built as a contrast of houses and doorways — the strange woman's house that inclines to death (7:27), Wisdom's house hewn with seven pillars (9:1), Folly's seat at her own door (9:14) — two invitations, one street, the same simple ones walking past.

# ALBUM III — A JUST WEIGHT IS HIS DELIGHT
### The Proverbs of Shlomo, Gathered by Theme · Proverbs 10:1–22:16 · nine songs

The book's largest collection is a great scatter of single sentences — hundreds of two-line proverbs with no story to string them. So this album does what the methodology built §3.3 to do: it gathers the scattered proverbs into coherent thematic songs, each pulling one thread through all twelve chapters. The tongue that holds death and life; the heart Yahuah assays like silver; the righteous rooted while the whirlwind passes; the rich and the poor who share one Maker; pride walking ahead of the fall; the field the sluggard would not plow; the child bent while the sapling is green; the wise woman who builds her house; and over all of it, the King whose own heart Yahuah turns like water. Larger than seven because the collection is larger than seven, and every thread is real (§3.3; the same move that carried a ten-song album on Exodus III).

*Why this title:* the whole collection turns on Yahuah weighing what men think is hidden — "a false balance is abomination to Yahuah: but a just weight is His delight" (11:1); He assays the heart, the motive, the measure no one else can see.

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |

**Narrative-unit receipt (thematic units gathered across 10:1–22:16):** the tongue · the assayed heart · the two destinies · wealth and the poor · pride and humility · diligence and the sluggard · the child and the rod · the household and the wife · Yahuah's sovereignty over plans and kings. Nine themes, nine songs; every chapter from 10 through 22:16 feeds the gathering (see the Coverage Ledger). *(Friendship proverbs 17:17 and 18:24 are gathered forward to Album V's chapter-27 friendship song, and the wine of 20:1 to Album IV's drunkard song, per §3.3.)*

# ALBUM IV — INCLINE YOUR EAR TO THE WISE
### The Sayings of the Wise · Proverbs 22:17–24:34 · seven songs

Here the book changes hand and voice: a new collection opens with its own summons — *bow down thine ear, and hear the words of the wise* — thirty sayings and then a further appendix "these things also belong to the wise." The counsel turns intimate and urgent: guard the poor at the gate, do not sit down greedy at a ruler's table, refuse the eye that watches you eat, buy the truth and never sell it. Its darkest song is the drunkard who cannot feel the blows he took; its bravest is the command not to walk past the perishing; and it lands, as the collection itself does, at a fence fallen and a vineyard swallowed by thorns.

*Why this title:* the collection names its own posture in its first line — "bow down thine ear, and hear the words of the wise, and apply thine heart unto my knowledge" (22:17) — an ear inclined, a heart applied, before a single saying is spoken.

# ALBUM V — THE GLORY OF THE HIDDEN THING
### Copied Out by the Men of Chizqiyahu · Proverbs 25–29 · eight songs

Centuries after Shlomo, the scribes of a righteous king copied out a second trove of his proverbs — and the Picture Letters remember being copied, remember being the very things drawn out onto the scroll. The collection opens with a line this whole platform could have been founded on: it is the glory of Elohim to conceal a thing, and the honor of kings to search it out. From there it turns to the craft of speech and the company we keep — a word fitly spoken like apples of gold in silver, coals of fire heaped by kindness on an enemy's head, the impossible double command about answering a fool, the comic sluggard and his lion in the street, the friend whose wounds are faithful and whose iron sharpens iron, and the people who perish where there is no vision.

*Why this title:* the collection's opening proverb (25:2) is the platform's own thesis in Shlomo's mouth — "it is the glory of Elohim to conceal a thing: but the honour of kings is to search it out" — a hidden thing set in the text on purpose, waiting to be searched.

# ALBUM VI — HER PRICE ABOVE RUBIES
### Agur, Lemuel, and the Woman of Valor · Proverbs 30–31 · seven songs

The book ends in two borrowed voices and one alphabet. Agur confesses he is too brutish to know — then asks the question the Angels alone could answer, who gathered the wind in His fists, and what is His name, and His Son's name? He prays only for the middle portion, neither poverty nor riches, and lays out his riddles of three and four: the things never satisfied, the things too wonderful, the small things wiser than the wise. Then a king's mother teaches him to open his mouth for those who have none. And the whole book closes as it opened, with the alphabet — the Picture Letters spelling out, line by line from Aleph to Tav, a woman of valor whose worth is beyond rubies and whose crown is the fear of Yahuah.

*Why this title:* the finale asks "who can find a virtuous woman? for her price is far above rubies" (31:10), and the acrostic that answers, letter by letter to its close, "a woman that feareth Yahuah, she shall be praised" (31:30) — the book's spine returning at the very end.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah as the book's divine name; Eloah, El, and Elohim used exactly where the text's own mouth uses them (Agur's oracle, 30:1–9; 25:2); Yeshua and Ruach HaKodesh (feminine) reserved to the platform framing. No English "God" or "Lord" anywhere in any title or copy. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled English "the" on any *Ha-* form). Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing; no emoji. Shlomo, Chizqiyahu, Yahudah, Agur, and Lemuel carry their Hebraic forms.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a personified Picture Letter. No human is ever the voice — not Shlomo, not Agur, not Lemuel, not Lemuel's mother; the father-to-son address that runs through the book is witnessed and quoted, never voiced. **Lady Wisdom (Chokmah)** and **Lady Folly** are invoked as image and witnessed — the Angels sing *about* Wisdom crying in the street (1:20–33; 8:1–3) and *about* Wisdom rejoicing beside the Maker (8:22–31), and never become her; she is a personification the text supplies, not a character-narrator this catalog adopts. Impersonal creation is invoked as image only — the ant with no ruler (6:6–8), the honeycomb (16:24), the apples of gold in settings of silver (25:11), Wisdom's seven-pillared house (9:1), the far-more-precious rubies (31:10), the eagle and the ship and the serpent on the rock (30:19), the four small wise things (30:24–28), the field lost to thorns (24:31).
- **The chapter-8 jewel, on-theme and un-resolved:** Wisdom present beside Yahuah before the world — "then I was by Him… rejoicing always before Him" (8:30) — is sung by the Angels and the Letters as a direct companion to Job 38:7, the morning stars singing as the foundations were laid; and the first glimpse of the same jewel (3:19, "Yahuah by wisdom hath founded the earth") sets it up on Album I. Both are carried as mystery and wonder from *inside* the moment, with no New-Testament hindsight and no resolution forward to any later identification.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** Agur's "what is His name, and what is His Son's name?" (30:4) is held open as ache, exactly as Job's go'el and Ruth's kinsman-redeemer were — the Angels who have ascended and descended hold the answer as mystery, never speaking it as accomplished fact. The "Redeemer" who pleads the fatherless cause (23:11) is likewise carried unresolved. The framing names Yeshua; the songs, standing inside the book's own hour, do not.
- **Text-numbers as content; the Picture Letters never a count:** the "six things… yea, seven" that Yahuah hates (6:16), the seven hewn pillars of Wisdom's house (9:1), the just man who falls seven times and rises (24:16), Agur's riddles of "three… yea, four" (30:15, 18, 21, 29), and the four small wise things are sung as the text's own numbers. The Picture Letters are named as personae — the Peh a mouth, the Bet a house, the Gimel a foot, the Mem water, the Zayin a blade, the Nun a serpent, the Tav a mark, the Ayin an eye — and never totaled, never reduced to a gematria mechanism, never given a fixed count; the closing acrostic (31:10–31) is sung by the whole alphabet as their own, Aleph to Tav, without ever naming their number.
- **The gathering method, disclosed:** Albums III (10:1–22:16), IV, and V's chapter-27 friendship song gather sentence-proverbs strewn across many chapters into thematic songs, per methodology §3.3 — the same move that built *The Names Outlived the Graves* on Genesis and carried the ten-song plague album on Exodus. Each such tracklist states plainly that it gathers scattered proverbs, and the Coverage Ledger shows every chapter feeding a song.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
