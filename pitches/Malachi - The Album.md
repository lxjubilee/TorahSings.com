**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable

**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Malakhi is **three chapters in the Hebrew (four in the English versification — English 4:1–6 = Hebrew 3:19–24)**, and it is built as **six disputations**: Yahuah asserts, the people answer back "wherein?", and He replies. Those six back-talk seams are the book's own outline, and they draw the songs: "I have loved you" (1:1–5); the despised Name and the blemished offerings (1:6–2:9); the profaned covenant of marriage (2:10–16); "where is the Elohim of judgment?" and the messenger of the covenant (2:17–3:5); "return unto me" — the robbed tithes and the book of remembrance (3:6–18); and the closing oracle of the burning Day and the Sun of righteousness (4:1–3), sealed by the book's own colophon — remember the Torah of Mosheh, and *behold, I will send you Eliyah* (4:4–6). **This pitch ships one album of seven songs: one per disputation, with the finale split at the 4:3/4:4 seam so the eschatological Day and the Sun of righteousness land as one movement and the closing charge — the Torah of Mosheh and the forerunner Eliyah — lands as the last, and the whole Old Testament's last, unresolved word.** With this record the Torah Sings Old-Testament catalog is complete: Malakhi is Book 39, the last of the Twelve, the final book before the long silence.

**"My messenger" — the thread the whole book turns on.** *Malakhi* means **"my messenger,"** and messengers run the length of the book like a single struck cord: the priest who *"is the messenger of Yahuah Tzeva'ot"* and should keep knowledge on his lips (2:7); *"behold, I send my messenger, and he shall prepare the way before me,"* and *"the messenger of the covenant, whom ye seek"* (3:1); and, at the very end, **Eliyah the prophet sent before the great and dreadful Day** (4:5–6). A book named "my messenger," reaching across three messengers toward a coming that has not come — that thread is felt in every song below and named in the album's own title.

**The messianic ache — held, never resolved. Malakhi is the door the Old Testament closes on.** Three passages carry the heaviest weight, and every one is sung as **unresolved longing — never resolved forward to Yeshua, no New-Testament hindsight, no cross, no fulfillment language, no Messiah unveiled.** **(1) The messenger of the covenant and the refiner's fire** (3:1–3) — *"but who may abide the day of his coming? and who shall stand when he appeareth? for he is like a refiner's fire, and like fullers' soap"* — a fire longed for and dreaded at once, held open. **(2) The Sun of righteousness** (4:2) — *"unto you that fear my name shall the Sun of righteousness arise with healing in his wings"* — a dawn the Angels reach toward that has not yet broken. **(3) Eliyah before the great and dreadful Day** (4:5–6) — the forerunner promised, the last sentence of the Old Testament, sung as the mystery heaven holds on the threshold of a silence it cannot yet see past. This is the OT's closing gate, and it is guarded on every title below: the whole book — and the whole covenant it ends — lands reaching toward a coming that has not yet come.

**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator — **Yahuah, Yahuah Tzeva'ot** — and, from *within* each disputation, toward the messenger and the dawn longed for but not yet unveiled. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or a personified Letter — never a human (not Malakhi, not the priests, not the sons of Yaakov). The people's back-talk — *"wherein hast thou loved us?"*, *"wherein have we despised thy name?"*, *"wherein have we robbed thee?"* — is **witnessed and quoted, never endorsed**: the Angels sing *about* the disputing people, never as them. Yahuah's own first-person oracles — *"I have loved you"*; *"my name shall be great among the nations"*; *"I am Yahuah, I change not"*; *"prove me now"*; *"they shall be mine… in that day when I make up my jewels"*; *"unto you that fear my name shall the Sun of righteousness arise"* — are **His own words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said** (the Deuteronomy / Job / Isaiah model). Impersonal creation the text supplies (the blind and lame offerings, the polluted altar-bread, the pure incense from the rising to the setting sun, the refiner's fire and fullers' soap, the storehouse and the opened windows of heaven, the rebuked devourer, the book of remembrance, the oven that burns, the Sun of righteousness with healing in its wings, the ashes under the feet) is **invoked as image, never voiced.** We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *One album. Seven songs. No honest zeros — every disputation of Malakhi is sung. All four chapters accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, and this book lands exactly there: six disputations, the finale split at the colophon so the Old Testament's last word gets its own room. The count follows the disputations, never a padded ceiling. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — THE MESSENGER BEFORE THE DAWN
### The Six Disputations and the Sun Not Yet Risen · Malakhi 1–4 · seven songs

The Old Testament ends in an argument. *I have loved you,* Yahuah says — and His own people answer back, *wherein hast thou loved us?* Six times He asserts and six times they dispute Him: over His love, over His honour, over the wife of the youth, over His justice, over the tithe, over whether it is even worth serving Him. The Angels witness the whole quarrel — the blind lambs shoved onto a polluted altar while a pure offering rises among the nations at the far ends of the earth, the altar drowned in the tears of the betrayed, the priest who should have been a messenger and became a stumbling-block — and through it all the thread of messengers pulls tight: the messenger of the covenant coming to a temple like a refiner's fire that no one is sure they can survive, and at the last the forerunner Eliyah promised before a great and dreadful Day. The record — and the covenant it closes — lands on that threshold: a Sun of righteousness not yet risen, a healing not yet come, heaven holding its breath before the silence.

*Why this title:* the book is *Malakhi*, "my messenger," and it turns on messengers from the priest of 2:7 to the messenger of the covenant of 3:1 to Eliyah the forerunner of 4:5 — while its closing oracle promises the *Sun of righteousness* that has not yet arisen (4:2) *before the great and dreadful day of Yahuah* (4:5). "The Messenger Before the Dawn" is the whole book in four words: the last messengers of the Old Testament, sent ahead of a daybreak that has not broken — the covenant ending mid-reach, in the dark before the sun.

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the six disputations run in the book's own order, and the album with them. *A Love They Dared Dispute* opens in the Veil — the astonishment that a people would argue back at love; the despised Name and the profaned marriage bring the quarrel down through the altar and the home; *Who May Abide His Coming* is the Turn, the longed-for and dreaded refiner's fire; *Prove Me Now* is the Selah that stoops to tenderness, the storehouse thrown open and the faithful written down as jewels; and the finale splits at the book's own colophon-seam (4:3 / 4:4) so the Declaration and the Benediction each get their room — *Healing in His Wings* thunders the burning Day and the Sun that heals, and *I Will Send You Eliyah* lands the record, and the whole Old Testament, on the threshold: the forerunner promised, the dawn withheld, the silence about to begin. Malakhi is already built as an argument that resolves into a promise reaching past its own last verse, so the text's own order *is* the artistic shape; no reordering improves on it.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1:1–5 the disputed love and Esav's waste · 1:6–2:9 the despised Name, the blemished offerings, the Name great among the nations, and the corrupted covenant of Levi · 2:10–16 the treachery and the wife of the youth, "he hateth putting away" · 2:17–3:5 "where is the Elohim of judgment?", the messenger of the covenant, and the refiner's fire · 3:6–18 "return unto me," the robbed tithes, "prove me now," and the book of remembrance and the jewels · 4:1–3 the Day that burns as an oven and the Sun of righteousness · 4:4–6 the Torah of Mosheh remembered and Eliyah sent before the great and dreadful Day. Seven for seven — the six disputations, the finale split at the 4:3/4:4 colophon so the Old Testament's last word stands alone.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine); Yahuah Tzeva'ot rendered as the text names Him ("saith Yahuah Tzeva'ot," 1:6, 3:1, 3:17). The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy — Elohim and Yahuah stand in their place. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing. The prophet is Malakhi, the patriarchs Yaakov and Esav, the lawgiver Mosheh and the mount Horev, the tribe and priest-covenant Levi, the nation Yehudah, and the forerunner **Eliyah** the prophet (Hebraic, never anglicized "Elijah"). The name **Yeshua is reserved for the Son** and is not used inside any song; He is named and adored only in the platform's framing.
- **Narrative-time rule honored (the Old Testament's closing gate):** every messianic ache is carried as unresolved longing, never resolved forward — the messenger of the covenant and the refiner's fire, *"who may abide the day of his coming?"* (3:1–3); the *Sun of righteousness* arising with healing in his wings (4:2); and *Eliyah the prophet* sent before the great and dreadful Day (4:5–6). All three are held as the mystery the Angels reach toward the One promised and never name forward — no Yeshua, no cross, no fulfillment inside a song. The record deliberately lands on that open, aching threshold: the covenant closes mid-reach, a forerunner promised and a dawn withheld, heaven holding its breath before the long silence that follows the Old Testament's last verse.
- **The "my messenger" thread honored:** *Malakhi* means "my messenger," and the book's three messengers are felt across the record and named in its title — the priest who is *"the messenger of Yahuah Tzeva'ot"* (2:7, song 2), the messenger of the covenant who comes to the temple (3:1, song 4), and Eliyah the forerunner before the Day (4:5, song 7). "The Messenger Before the Dawn" carries the thread and the ache together.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter — the Shin keyed to *shemesh* (the sun) and to the fire that consumes (song 6, where the oven-fire and the Sun of righteousness meet), and the Letters as the ledger-keepers of the book of remembrance (song 5). Malakhi and the priests and the sons of Yaakov are *witnessed*, never made the singer; their back-talk — *"wherein hast thou loved us?"*, *"wherein have we despised thy name?"*, *"wherein have we robbed thee?"*, *"it is vain to serve Elohim"* — is quoted and witnessed, **never endorsed and never voiced as the singer's own.** Yahuah's first-person oracles are His words, sung in awe by the witnesses who report what He said. The blind and lame offerings, the polluted altar-bread, the pure incense from the rising to the setting sun, the refiner's fire and fullers' soap, the storehouse and the opened windows of heaven, the rebuked devourer, the book of remembrance, the oven, the Sun with healing in its wings, and the ashes under the feet are invoked as images, never voiced.
- **Picture Letters, never a count:** the Letters are named as personae where used (the Shin, the ledger-keeping Letters); no total is ever stated and no gematria mechanism is sung. The text's own numbers are content, not method — the tithes and offerings, the windows of heaven, the "in that day" when He makes up His jewels.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Restraint:** the judgment strokes — Esav's mountains laid waste, the swift witness against sorcerers and oppressors, the Day that burns the proud to stubble and ashes — are handled with grave, family-friendly restraint, witnessed from heaven's side, never rendered for shock.
- **Catalog completion:** Malakhi is Book 39, the last of the Twelve and the final book of the Old Testament; with this album the Torah Sings Old-Testament catalog is complete, and it closes exactly where the covenant closes — reaching toward a coming not yet come.
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
