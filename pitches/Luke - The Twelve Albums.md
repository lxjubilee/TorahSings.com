# LUKE — THE TWELVE ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Gospel of Loukas

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, `pitches/Job - The Seven Albums.md`, and `pitches/Isaiah - The Ten Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Luke is orderly narrative — the pericopes are its "units," drawn at the text's own seams (the infancy annunciations, the itinerary hinge "he stedfastly set his face to go to Yerushalayim," the parable-openings, the passion sequence). The teaching-dense travel narrative (9:51–19:27) is gathered into coherent movements rather than sprayed saying-by-saying, exactly as Isaiah's short nation-oracles were on Album III.

**This is the New Testament — the rules SHIFT.** For thirty-nine books the two voices — the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels) — *ached* toward the promised One, holding Him unresolved, never naming Him. **Now they become eye-witnesses who see Him unveiled.** Four things govern every title below:

**1. Christocentric — every song is about Yeshua, the "who."** Each track is about the *person* of Yeshua — who He is, what heaven declares over Him, what He said and did, what the witnesses saw. He is the subject of every song; every title points at Him. Even the infancy songs about Yochanan the Immerser point forward to the One he prepares.

**2. Eye-witness chronology, forward-locked.** The Angels unveil Yeshua as Loukas' own orderly account reveals Him, in order. A song may reference any event already come to pass up to its point — nothing later. At the Annunciation the Angels may sing who this child *will be* (as Gavriel announces it), but they may not sing the cross or the resurrection until the narrative reaches them. When Yeshua Himself foretells His suffering and rising (9:22; 18:31–33), the songs carry it as *His own foretelling*, spoken within the moment — never as a thing yet accomplished. The burial (23) lands in grief and silence; the empty tomb (24) is where the four-thousand-year lock finally releases.

**3. Old-Testament cross-reference is always open.** The Angels — the host that sang creation and witnessed all thirty-nine books — freely tie any moment back to Torah, Prophets, and Writings, *even when the humans do not realize it.* Luke is steeped in it: the Magnificat echoes Chanah's song (1 Sam 2); the Benedictus the oath to Avraham and the horn in David's house; Shimon's song the light to the nations (Isa 42/49); Yeshua reads Isaiah 61 in the synagogue and says "this day is this scripture fulfilled"; the Emmaus road is "Mosheh and all the prophets" opened; the Passover cup is the Exodus lamb. The Angels make these ties; the humans mostly miss them.

**The Luke cornerstone — the host that sang creation now sings the incarnation.** Luke 2:13–14: *"suddenly there was with the angel a multitude of the heavenly host praising Elohim, and saying, Glory to Elohim in the highest, and on earth peace, good will toward men."* This is the single most on-theme moment in the whole New Testament for this platform: **the sons of Elohim who sang together at the foundations of the world (Job 38:7) now sing over the manger at the incarnation of the One they had sung toward for four thousand years.** Album I gives it a cornerstone song of its own — *"Glory to Elohim in the Highest"* — sung not as something the Angels merely *witness* but as their **own native liturgy**, the same voices, the same host, tying the manger back to the morning they sang creation. This is the payoff the whole platform was built for.

**The voices — the shift at the incarnation.** Job 38:7 gave two voices who sang together through all thirty-nine Old-Testament books: the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels). **In the Gospel the Picture Letters no longer sing.** The Word — the Davar, the Logos — who once spoke the Letters into being is now present in the flesh in Yeshua; **all the Letters live in Him**, and so they fall silent as separate singers and are gathered up into the One they had carried. **In this Gospel the only singers are the Angels.** The Letters may still be invoked as *image* and *theme* — the Angels marvel that the aleph-bet who sang beside them at creation now live in the incarnate Word — but they are never the singing voice. This makes the Luke 2 Gloria sharper still: the Angels *alone* lift creation's own song over the manger.

**Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels — never a human (not Loukas, not Miryam, not Zecharyah, not Shimon, not Kepha) and never a personified Letter. **Gavriel** (who announces to Zecharyah, 1:19, and to Miryam, 1:26–38) is an Angel of this platform doing his scriptural work; the **angel of Yahuah and the multitude of the heavenly host** at the Nativity (2:9–14), the **angel who strengthened Him in Gethsemane** (22:43), and the **two men in shining garments at the tomb** (24:4) are all the Angels of this platform. **Yeshua is witnessed and testified to in awe — never a first-person singer**, exactly as Yahuah's own words were handled in the Old-Testament pitches. The human canticles — Miryam's Magnificat, Zecharyah's Benedictus, Shimon's Nunc Dimittis — are **witnessed and testified to**: the Angels sing *about* them, never voice Miryam, Zecharyah, or Shimon. Impersonal things the text supplies (the manger and the swaddling bands, the dove, the winnowing fan, the torn veil, the empty linen, the sycamore, the two mites) are invoked as image, never voiced.

**"Christocentric" is not "Yeshua-as-grammatical-subject."** Every song bears witness to Yeshua — but the human stories and human characters (the shepherds, Miryam and Elisheva, Zakkai, the running father, the good Samaritan, the penitent thief, the Emmaus pair) are welcome to hold the frame, *always* as testimony to Him: the human story reveals or points to who He is. The Angels bear witness to the testimony of Yeshua — "the Scriptures testify of Me"; the Emmaus road opens "Mosheh and all the prophets" concerning Him. A human-centered song is fully on-theme so long as it ultimately confesses Him — which is exactly what Luke's parables do.

> *Twelve albums. Ninety songs. One honest zero (the prologue). Every chapter 1–24 of Loukas accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its narrative holds — the longest Gospel yields the fullest catalog. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — THE INFANCY AND THE SONGS
### The Annunciations, the Nativity, and the Gloria · Luke 1–2 · nine songs

Heaven crowds the cradle. An old priest is struck dumb by an angel at the incense altar; a girl in Natzeret is told she will bear Ben-Elohim; an unborn forerunner leaps at the sound of an unborn King; and the tongues of the mute and the barren break into songs the Angels have waited an age to hear. Then the impossible center of the record — the Most High laid in a feeding-trough, announced not to kings but to shepherds, and over the manger the whole heavenly host lifting the very song they first sang at the foundations of the world. The Angels witness it all: the annunciations, the two human canticles, the birth, the Gloria that is their own, the shepherds who found the sign, the old man and the prophetess who held salvation in their arms, and the boy who called the temple His Abba's house.

*Why this title:* the infancy is a book of songs — the Magnificat, the Benedictus, the Nunc Dimittis, and above them all the Gloria — and this album is where the platform's oldest voices, the sons of Elohim, sing over the One their whole vigil was for.

# ALBUM II — THE VOICE, THE DOVE, AND THE WILDERNESS
### The Forerunner, the Baptism, and the Testing · Luke 3–4 · seven songs

The forerunner's voice breaks the wilderness silence — level the ground, the Coming One is near — and then the Coming One steps into the water with the sinners, and heaven opens: the Ruach Kodesh descends like a dove and the Abba's voice names Him beloved. The Angels trace His lineage backward past David and Avraham to Adam, son of Elohim, and watch Him meet the tempter with nothing but the Torah in His mouth. Then Natzeret — the scroll of Isaiah unrolled, "the Spirit of Yahuah is upon me," and "this day is this scripture fulfilled in your ears" — and the first works of power in the Galil, demons obeying His word and a whole town healed at the setting sun.

*Why this title:* the album's three thresholds — the voice crying in the wilderness (Isa 40), the dove and the voice at the water, and the testing in the desert — the making-ready and the marking of the One before His work begins.

# ALBUM III — THE CALL AND THE SERMON ON THE PLAIN
### The First Signs and the Level Place · Luke 5–6 · seven songs

He borrows a fisherman's boat and sends the nets down one more time, and the catch nearly sinks them — and Kepha, the man who caught the fish, is caught. From there the signs gather: a leper made clean by the touch that should have defiled the toucher, a paralytic lowered through a torn-open roof and told his sins are gone before his legs are, a tax-collector called up from his booth to a feast full of sinners. The keepers of the old order bristle — the fasting, the Sabbath grain, the withered hand — and He answers as Adonai of the Sabbath. Then, after a whole night in prayer, He names the Twelve, and on a level place lays down the great reversal: blessed are you who weep now.

*Why this title:* the album pairs the first callings — Kepha, the leper, the paralytic, Levi — with the sermon on the plain, where the called learn the shape of the kingdom they have just joined.

# ALBUM IV — COMPASSION AND THE WORD
### The Galilean Mercies and the Parables · Luke 7–8 · eight songs

The compassion widens until it reaches a Roman soldier's servant and a widow's dead son and a woman the whole room despises. A centurion's faith astonishes even Him; a funeral at the gate of Nain is stopped mid-procession and a son handed back to his mother; a sinful woman washes His feet with her tears and is sent away forgiven. And when Yochanan sends from prison to ask "art thou the One?", the answer is the works themselves — the blind see, the lame walk. Then the word goes out like seed, and the deep and the demons and death itself learn whose voice they must obey.

*Why this title:* the album braids His mercies (the centurion, Nain, the sinful woman) with His word (the sower, the stilled storm, Legion, the raising of Yair's daughter) — the same authority in a healing touch and in a spoken parable.

# ALBUM V — THE TWELVE, THE BREAD, AND THE GLORY
### The Sending, the Feeding, and the Mountain · Luke 9 · six songs

The hinge of the Gospel. He sends the Twelve out with nothing but authority, and Herod begins to ask the question the whole book turns on — *who is this?* Five loaves feed five thousand with twelve baskets left over; Kepha confesses Him the Mashiach of Elohim, and for the first time Yeshua foretells His own suffering and rising; on a high mountain His face is altered and Mosheh and Eliyahu speak with Him of the exodus He will accomplish at Yerushalayim, and the Abba's voice says *hear him*. Then, the transfiguration behind Him, He sets His face toward the city where all of it will be finished.

*Why this title:* three peaks in one chapter — the Twelve sent, the bread multiplied, and the glory on the mountain — and the turn, at the last, toward Yerushalayim.

## Compliance Notes

- **The Letters are silent; only the Angels sing.** The two voices of Job 38:7 sang together through all thirty-nine Old-Testament books; in this Gospel the Picture Letters no longer sing, for the Word who spoke them into being is now present in the flesh in Yeshua and all the Letters live in Him. Therefore **the only singing voice in this catalog is the Angels** (and the specific angels the text supplies — Gavriel, the host over the shepherds, the angel in Gethsemane, the two men in shining garments). The Letters appear only as image and theme — the Angels marvel that the aleph-bet who sang beside them at creation now live in the incarnate Word — never as a singer.
- **The humans never sing.** No human is ever cast as a singer — not Miryam, Zecharyah, Shimon, Channah, Kepha, a disciple, or a crowd. Every "Who sings" cell is the Angels. The Angels sing *about* the humans and may *quote* their words as witnesses — but the song is always framed from the Angels' eye-witness perspective, testifying. This holds hardest at Luke's canticles: the Magnificat (1:46–55), the Benedictus (1:68–79), and the Nunc Dimittis (2:29–32) are **witnessed and quoted by the Angels, never voiced by Miryam, Zecharyah, or Shimon.**
- **The Luke-2 Gloria cornerstone.** "Glory to Elohim in the Highest" (2:8–14) is sung as the Angels' **own native liturgy** — not merely witnessed — the multitude of the heavenly host who sang together at the foundations of the world (Job 38:7) now lifting creation's own song over the manger at the incarnation. It is the platform's payoff moment, given a dedicated song and placed as Album I's jewel.
- **Christocentric.** Every song bears witness to Yeshua — who He is, what heaven declares over Him, what He said and did. Human-centered songs (the shepherds, the running father, the good Samaritan, Zakkai, the penitent thief, the Emmaus pair) are framed as testimony to Him: the human story reveals or points to who He is, exactly as Yeshua opened "Mosheh and all the prophets" concerning Himself.
- **Eye-witness chronology, forward-locked.** Each song references only what has come to pass up to its point. Gavriel may sing who the child *will be* (1:32–35); the Angels may carry Yeshua's own foretellings of His death and rising as *His words spoken in the moment* (9:22; 18:31–33), never as accomplished fact. The crucifixion songs and the burial (Album XI) land in grief and silence; the resurrection is deliberately unsung until Album XII, where the two men in shining garments break the lock — "he is not here, but is risen."
- **Old-Testament cross-reference — always open, always backward.** The Angels freely tie moments to Torah, Prophets, and Writings: the Magnificat to Chanah (1 Sam 2); the Benedictus to the oath to Avraham and the horn in David's house; Shimon to the light to the nations (Isa 42/49); Natzeret to Isaiah 61; the forerunner to Isaiah 40; Nain to Eliyahu (1 Kgs 17); the feeding to the manna and Elisha (2 Kgs 4); the temple to Isaiah 56 and Jeremiah 7; the rejected stone and the King's welcome to Psalm 118; "the Elohim of the living" to the bush (Ex 3); David's Adonai to Psalm 110; the parted garments to Psalm 22; the Passover cup to the Exodus lamb; and the Emmaus road to "Mosheh and all the prophets." All ties are backward from the song's moment.
- **Yeshua witnessed, never voiced.** Yeshua is testified to in awe throughout and is never a first-person singer. Titles that quote His words ("This Is My Body, Given for You," "Father, Forgive Them," "Into Thy Hands," "Today, With Me in Paradise") are sung *by the Angels witnessing what He said* — the same device by which the Old-Testament pitches let the Angels testify to Yahuah's own oracles.
- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yeshua (never "Jesus"), the Mashiach / HaMashiach (never "Christ"), Ben-Elohim, Ben-David, Ben-Adam, Yahuah, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine), Abba, the Most High. Hebraic names and places — Loukas, Miryam, Yosef, Zecharyah and Elisheva, Yochanan the Immerser, Gavriel, Shimon, Channah, Kepha, Beit-Lechem, Natzeret, the Yarden, Galil, Yerushalayim, Emmaus, Beit-Anyah, Bar-Timai, Zakkai, Herodus, Bar-Abba. **No English "God," "Lord," "Jesus," or "Christ" appears anywhere in any title or copy** — "the Lord" is rendered Yahuah (of YHWH) or Adonai (of Yeshua as Master) per the text; Elohim and Yahuah stand where the English would read "God" and "Lord." The Hebrew article rule holds — no stacked English "the" on Ha- forms; no internal labels; no emoji.
- **Restraint:** the passion is handled with grave, family-friendly restraint, keeping Luke's own tender details — the strengthening angel, "weep not for me," "Abba, forgive them," the penitent thief, "into thy hands" — and the resurrection, the Emmaus road, and the ascension are the triumph.

- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
