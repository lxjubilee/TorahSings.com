# 2 CHRONICLES — THE SIX ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book Sung From the Choir Loft of the House

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, and `pitches/1 Chronicles - The Five Albums.md`, whose second volume this is
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Every song below is one narrative unit; every unit boundary sits on the text's own seams — the building and dedication formulas, the regnal openings ("and X reigned… and did that which was right/evil in the sight of Yahuah"), the prophetic confrontations, and the great worship markers (the courses of singers, the trumpets, the Passovers) that Chronicles supplies in place of the Genesis *toledot*.

**The Chronicles angle:** 2 Chronicles retells Shlomo and the kings of Yehudah from the same **priestly, worship-centered** vantage that made 1 Chronicles the most on-theme book Torah Sings has reached — and it follows only the Davidic line of Yehudah, never the northern thrones. The house is built at last on Mount Moriyah, the ark is brought in, the singers make one sound and the cloud fills the room, fire falls from heaven on the altar, and the great reforms and Passovers turn on the Levites and the song. Where these chapters overlap 1 Kings and 2 Kings, these albums sing the Chronicler's distinct temple-and-song angle — the doors opened and shut, the courses restored, the fire that fell as glory and the fire that finally burned the house — never a repeat of the Kings record. That is the feature, not a flaw: the same kings, watched from where the singing is. This is the volume where the house David could not build (1 Chron) rises, is filled, is forsaken, is reopened, and is at last both burned and, on the final page, promised again.

**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator, Yahuah — and, from *within* each moment of the story, toward the Son promised but not yet unveiled. The Angels are the pattern of the heavenly worship the Levitical choirs were built to mirror, and heaven itself is on the scene at the dedication, when the fire fell and the glory filled the house (7:1–3). **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own moment, with no later hindsight — Yeshua is named and adored in the platform's framing, but inside a 2 Chronicles-moment song the aches are carried as mystery and longing, never named as fulfilled: a house for a Name the heaven of heavens cannot contain (6:18); the mercy conditioned on "if my people shall humble themselves and pray" (7:14); the Davidic lamp kept burning for the covenant's sake through the faithless kings (21:7); the seventy years of desolation and the decree of Koresh that ends the book on a door left open (36:21–23). **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or the personified Letters — never a human character (not Shlomo, not Yehoshafat, not Chizqiyahu, not Menasheh, not the Levites) or anything derived from one. Shlomo's dedication prayer (6), the singers' refrain "for His mercy endures forever" (5:13; 7:3; 20:21), every royal prayer and reform, and the dying repentance of Menasheh (33) are **witnessed and testified to by the Angels**, never sung as the human. Impersonal creation the text supplies — the cloud that filled the house, the fire from heaven on the altar, the two pillars Yakhin and Boaz, the molten sea on its twelve oxen, the ambush at the valley of Berakhah, the barred and reopened doors, the tithes heaped in heaps, the fetters that carried Menasheh, the found scroll — may be *invoked as image*, never voiced. Natural witness for the wars and the fall: **the Angel of Yahuah who cut off the camp of Assyria in a night** (32:21), and heaven at the altar when the fire fell (7:1). We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *Six albums. Forty-one songs. No honest zeros — every narrative unit of 2 Chronicles is sung. All thirty-six chapters accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its stories hold — the building and dedication of the house become one long record, the dark middle reigns another, exactly as the narrative demands. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — HE BUILT THE HOUSE AT LAST
### Shlomo, the House, and the Glory · 2 Chronicles 1–9 · ten songs

The house David was forbidden to build (1 Chron 22:8) now rises — on the very ground where the Angel's sword was sheathed, Mount Moriyah, the threshing floor of Ornan. A young king asks only for wisdom to judge, and is given the rest unasked; the cedars come down from Tsor with a pagan king's blessing on the Maker; the pillars are set and named; a sea of bronze stands on twelve oxen. And then the summit of the whole book: the singers and trumpeters make one sound, and the cloud fills the house so the priests cannot stand; the king confesses that no house — not even the heaven of heavens — can contain the Name; fire falls from heaven on the altar the moment the prayer ends; and by night the answer comes, *if my people humble themselves*. This is the record of the going-in, the glory taking up the room prepared for it.

*Why this title:* the book's opening arc is the house built and filled — the direct bookend to *The House He Could Not Build* (1 Chron, Album V); what David heaped up and handed on, Shlomo raises and dedicates.

Ten for ten.

# ALBUM II — WHOEVER LEANED ON YAHUAH STOOD
### The Kingdom Torn, and the Early Kings · 2 Chronicles 10–16 · seven songs

The kingdom tears in two over a young king's boast, and the ten tribes walk away — but the priests and Levites of the whole land walk the other direction, south to the altar that still stands. What follows is the Chronicler's plainest sermon, told through three kings: strength comes from leaning on Yahuah, and ruin from leaning anywhere else. Shishak strips the temple gold, and a humbled king keeps his throne. Aviyah, surrounded front and back, wins by the priests' trumpets. Asa faces a thousand-thousand army and prays that numbers are nothing to Yahuah — and then, grown old and afraid, buys Aram's help with the temple silver and dies seeking physicians instead of the One who once made numbers nothing.

*Why this title:* the album's refrain is the Chronicler's whole theology of the reigns — Aviyah leaned and stood, Asa leaned and stood and then let go; the kingdom itself is held or lost by the direction of a lean.

|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **My Little Finger Tore the Kingdom** | 2 Chron 10 | The Angels, watching a crown split on a sentence | The young king spurning the elders for the boys who grew up with him — "my little finger shall be thicker than my father's loins" — and ten tribes answering "to your tents, O Yisrael"; a kingdom torn on the promise of a heavier yoke |
| 2 | **The Priests Left Their Fields and Came South** | 2 Chron 11 | The Angels, who saw which way the Levites walked | While the tribes go north to golden calves, the priests and Levites abandon their suburbs and their possessions and come to Yerushalayim, where the altar still burns — and after them, everyone who set his heart to seek Yahuah; the true house strengthened for three years by who chose it |

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 10 the split · 11 Rechav'am fortifies and the priests come south · 12 Shishak and the humbling · 13 Aviyah's war and the trumpets · 14 Asa and the Cushite host · 15 Azaryah's word and the sworn covenant · 16 the Aramean alliance and the diseased feet. # ALBUM III — THE SINGERS WENT OUT BEFORE THE ARMY
### Yehoshafat and the Battle Won by Song · 2 Chronicles 17–20 · four songs

A king sends the book of the law out into his own cities, teachers going town to town, and the fear of Yahuah falls so heavily on the surrounding nations that none lift a sword against him. He nearly throws it all away in a marriage-alliance with the house of Achav — one true prophet against four hundred, and a random arrow finding a disguised king between the joints of his armor. Rebuked and chastened, he sets judges through the land to judge for Yahuah and not for man. And then the album's astonishment and the book's brightest jewel: three nations invade, and the king puts the singers *in front of the army* — and the moment they begin to praise "for His mercy endures forever," Yahuah sets the ambush, and the enemies turn their swords on each other, and Yehudah spends three days gathering spoil in a valley they name Berakhah, Blessing. A battle won without a sword drawn.

*Why this title:* 20:21 — "he appointed singers unto Yahuah… that should praise… as they went out before the army" — the most on-theme sentence in the histories, and the album's namesake and peak.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **He Sent the Law Into the Cities** | 2 Chron 17 | The Lamed — the staff that urges toward, that teaches | Princes and Levites carrying the book of the law from city to city through Yehudah, and the dread of Yahuah falling on the kingdoms round about so that they make no war — a realm kept safe not by its walls but by the spread of the word |
| 2 | **One Prophet Against Four Hundred** | 2 Chron 18 | The Angels, who saw the lying spirit sent out | Four hundred voices promising victory and one man telling the truth and struck on the mouth for it; a disguised king in another's armor, and an arrow drawn "at a venture" finding him between the joints — the word of Yahuah that does not miss, and an ally of the wicked nearly dying for the friendship |
| 3 | **Judge Not for Man, but for Yahuah** | 2 Chron 19 | The Angels, witnessing the charge to the judges | Yehu the seer meeting the returning king — "shouldest thou help the ungodly?" — and the chastened king setting judges through the land with one warning: you judge not for man but for Yahuah, who is with you in the judgment, with whom is no iniquity nor respect of persons |
| 4 | **They Praised, and the Ambush Turned** | 2 Chron 20 | The Angels — the heavenly praise the singers echo; the ambush invoked as image | A fast proclaimed, a Levite of the sons of Asaf crying "the battle is not yours, but Elohim's," the singers in holy array going out ahead of the spears to praise for His mercy that endures forever — and the three invading nations turning on each other, and three days of spoil gathered in the valley named Blessing |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the law sent into the cities opens the album in a strange, quiet safety; the prophet against the four hundred is the Turn — a compromise that nearly costs the king his life; the judges set to judge for Yahuah are the Approach back toward faith; and the singing army lands the album as the Declaration and the Benediction at once — praise sent to the front, the sword left in the sheath, the valley renamed for the blessing found there.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 17 the law taught through the cities · 18 the alliance with Achav and Michayahu · 19 Yehu's rebuke and the judges appointed · 20 the invasion, the singers before the army, and Berakhah (the reign's close and the failed ships, 20:31–37, gathered here). Four for four.

# ALBUM IV — THE LAMP THAT WOULD NOT GO OUT
### The Dark and Mixed Reigns · 2 Chronicles 21–28 · nine songs

Here the Davidic line burns down almost to nothing, and is kept alive only by the covenant. A king murders his own brothers to hold the throne and dies with his bowels falling out, unmourned — yet the lamp is not put out, "because of the covenant… to give a light to him and to his sons forever." A usurping queen kills every heir but one infant, hidden six years in the very house of Yahuah, and relit in the seventh. A boy-king repairs the house with a chest of the people's coins, then — the old priest dead — watches the priest's own son stoned in the temple court. A king carries home the gods of the nation he just beat and bows to them; another seizes a censer that is not his and is struck with leprosy on his forehead at the altar; one steady king refuses to force the door; and the worst of them shuts the doors of the house altogether. The lamp gutters, and does not go out.

*Why this title:* 21:7 is the album's engine — through murder, usurpation, apostasy, and leprosy, "Yahuah would not destroy the house of David, because of the covenant… to give a lamp to him and his sons for ever" — the thread that keeps the line alive when every king reaching for it fails.

Nine for nine. *(22 splits at 22:9 / 22:10; 24 splits at 24:16 / 24:17, the text's own seam "now after the death of Yehoyada.")*

# ALBUM V — THE DOORS SWUNG OPEN AGAIN
### Chizqiyahu's Passover and Menasheh's Return · 2 Chronicles 29–33 · six songs

The son of the king who shut the doors opens them in the first month of his first year — the Levites carry out the filth, and the moment the burnt offering begins, the song of Yahuah begins with it, trumpets and the instruments of David sounding until the whole assembly worships. Then the great Passover: couriers running from Beer-Sheva to Dan to call even the remnant of the north, some mocking and some humbling themselves, a feast so full of gladness they keep it seven days and then seven more — the like of which had not been since Shlomo. The tithes pile in heaps; an angel empties the camp of Assyria in a single night; a king's heart swells with his own healing and then bows again. And the door opens even for the worst: Menasheh, who filled Yerushalayim with idols and passed his sons through fire, dragged to Babylon in bronze fetters — and there, of all places, breaking, praying, and carried home to tear down what he built.

*Why this title:* the album is a sequence of openings — the barred doors of Achaz thrown open (29:3), the land opened to the north for the Passover (30:5), the fetters that finally opened a hardened heart (33:12–13); "again" because Achaz had shut what Chizqiyahu now reopens.

| 1 | **He Opened the Doors in the First Month** | 2 Chron 29 | The Dalet — the door itself, unbarred | The doors his father shut thrown open in the first month of the first year, the Levites carrying the uncleanness out of the holy place, and the instant the offering rises the song of Yahuah rises with it — trumpets and the instruments of David — until the whole assembly bows; worship set in order again in a day |
| 2 | **Seven Days, and Then Another Seven** | 2 Chron 30 | The Zayin — the seven the feast was doubled by | Couriers running from Beer-Sheva to Dan with letters calling even the north to come home to the Passover, kept in the second month because the priests were not yet enough; some laughing the messengers to scorn and some humbling themselves — and joy so great they keep it seven days, then seven more, like nothing since the days of Shlomo |

| 4 | **One Angel Emptied the Camp** | 2 Chron 32:1–23 | The Angels — the angel of the deliverance, invoked and testified | Sancheriv's letters taunting the living Elohim as though He were the wood-and-stone gods of the nations, the king and the prophet crying to heaven — and in a night an angel going through the camp of Assyria, cutting off the mighty men and the captains, so the great king goes home ashamed and is cut down in the house of his own god |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the reopened doors and the restored song open the album in relief; the great Passover is the Declaration, the north called home and the joy doubled; the heaped tithes are the glad, ordered center; the angel that empties the camp is the Unveiling of heaven's own hand; the swelling and bowing heart is the near-miss; and Menasheh's fetters land the album on the book's most astonishing mercy — the worst idolater of the whole line broken in Babylon and brought home, a door opened even there.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 29 the doors opened and the house cleansed · 30 the great Passover · 31 the courses restored and the tithes · 32:1–23 Sancheriv and the angel · 32:24–33 the pride, the sign, and the death · 33 Menasheh's fall, repentance, and return (Amon's refusal folded as the closing contrast, 33:21–25). Six for six.

# ALBUM VI — THE SCROLL, THE FIRE, AND THE OPEN DOOR
### Yoshiyahu to the Decree of Koresh · 2 Chronicles 34–36 · five songs

One last light, and then the dark. A boy-king purges the altars north and south, and in the middle of repairing the house a lost scroll is found in the rubble — and he tears his robes at the words no one had read in generations, and swears the covenant again, and a prophetess is sent for. He keeps a Passover so exact that none like it had been seen since the days of Shmuel, the Levites in their courses and the singers of Asaf in their place. Then he rides to a war that was not his and will not turn back even from a warning that came from the mouth of Elohim, and is struck at Megiddo, and Yirmeyahu makes a lament sung to this day. Four kings follow in swift succession, the messengers of Yahuah mocked "till there was no remedy," and the fire that once fell from heaven to fill the house is set to it by Babylon — the walls broken, the land left to keep its sabbaths seventy years. And on the very last page, a foreign king's decree, stirred by Yahuah: *who is there among you? let him go up.* A door left open.

*Why this title:* the album's three hinges and the book's own ending — the scroll found in the house (34:14), the fire that took the house at last (36:19), and the decree of Koresh that closes the whole chronicle not on ruin but on an open road home (36:23).

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the found scroll opens the album in a last, bright reform; the Passover like none since Shmuel is the Declaration, worship at its final summit; Megiddo is the Turn, the light struck down over a warning refused; the burned house and the seventy years are the Selah of the whole book, the fire of the dedication devastatingly reversed; and the decree of Koresh lands the album and the entire chronicle on the Benediction — a door left open, the last word "let him go up," the ache of the house held out as invitation rather than end.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 34 the purge, the found scroll, Chuldah, and the covenant · 35:1–19 the great Passover · 35:20–27 the death at Megiddo and the lament · 36:1–21 the last four kings, the fall, and the seventy years · 36:22–23 the decree of Koresh. Five for five.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine). The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy — always Elohim / Yahuah, including inside the scriptural phrases quoted (e.g., "the battle is not yours, but Elohim's"; "Blessed be Yahuah Elohim of Yisrael"). The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Pagan deities are rendered by the text's own names — Baal, the Baalim, the Asherim, the gods of Se'ir, the gods of Damascus. No anglicized divine names. Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing. No emoji.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** inside every 2 Chronicles-moment song the aches are carried as mystery and longing, never named as fulfilled — the house for a Name the heaven of heavens cannot contain (6:18); the mercy set on "if my people… shall humble themselves" (7:14); the Davidic lamp kept through the faithless kings for the covenant's sake (21:7); the seventy years' desolation and the door left open by the decree of Koresh (36:21–23). The exile and the decree are sung because the text itself records them, not as later hindsight. The framing names and adores Yeshua; the songs, standing inside their own hour, do not.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter — the Bet keyed to *bayit* (the house rising on Moriyah), the Mem to the water the bronze sea was named for, the Shin to the fire that fell as glory in ch 7 and, inverted, burned the house in ch 36, the Lamed to the staff that teaches (the law sent through the cities), the Nun to the seed/heir (the one hidden child), the Resh to the head where Uzziyahu's leprosy rose, the Dalet to the door reopened, the Zayin to the sevens of the doubled Passover, the Letters to the found and recovered scroll. Shlomo, Yehoshafat, Chizqiyahu, Menasheh, Yoshiyahu, and every king and Levite are *witnessed*, never made the singer; Shlomo's dedication prayer (6), the singers' refrain (5:13; 7:3; 20:21), the royal reforms, and Menasheh's Babylon repentance (33) are testified to from heaven's side, never sung as theirs. The cloud, the fire on the altar, the two pillars, the molten sea, the ambush at Berakhah, the barred and reopened doors, the heaped tithes, the bronze fetters, and the found scroll are invoked as images, never voiced. The Angel who cut off the camp of Assyria (32:21) is the natural witness at the deliverance — invoked and testified, never sung as a human.
- **Picture Letters, never a count:** the Letters are named as personae where used — chiefly the ledger/scroll-keepers of ch 34 — and no total is ever stated, no gematria mechanism ever sung. The text's own numbers are content, not method: the thousand burnt offerings at Giv'on (1:6); the six hundred sixty-six talents of gold a year (9:13); the molten sea on its twelve oxen (4:4); the thousand thousand and three hundred chariots of Zerach (14:9); the five hundred thousand of Yisrael fallen before Aviyah (13:17); the eighty priests who withstood Uzziyahu (26:17); the seven-plus-seven days of Chizqiyahu's Passover (30:23); the threescore and ten years of desolation (36:21). The 120 priests with trumpets and the one sound of the singers at the dedication (5:12–13) are sung as the Chronicler's own detail.
- **Chronicler fidelity over the Kings parallel:** where these chapters overlap 1–2 Kings, the pitch sings the Chronicler's distinct temple-and-song angle, never a repeat of the Kings record — the doors opened and shut, the courses of singers restored, the fire that fell and the fire that burned, the Passovers kept by the Levites. Where the Chronicler's own count differs from Kings/Isaiah, his words govern: the angel of ch 32 "cut off all the mighty men… and the leaders and captains" (32:21); the figure of 185,000 belongs to 2 Kings 19 / Yeshayahu 37 and is deliberately not imported here. Menasheh's Babylon repentance (33:11–13) is unique to Chronicles and carried as the book's signature mercy.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5) — fitting for the book that watches worship built into ranks, filled with glory, forsaken, and reopened.
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
