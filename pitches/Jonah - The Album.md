**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Jonah is four chapters and one continuous story, so it is one album. Every song below is one narrative unit; every unit boundary sits on the text's own seams — the call and the flight, the storm at sea, the great fish and the prayer from the deep, the city that repents, and the vine and the question that closes the book with a question and no reply.
**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator, Yahuah — and, from *within* each moment of the story, marvel at a mercy they cannot fully fathom. **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own hour, with no later hindsight — the three days and three nights in the belly of the great fish are carried as the mystery they are, sung as descent and deliverance, ache and wonder, and never resolved forward to any later meaning or sign. Yeshua is named and adored in the platform's framing; inside a Jonah-moment song He is not unveiled. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or the personified Letters — never a human character (not Yonah, not the shipmaster, not the mariners, not the king of Nineveh) or anything derived from one. Yonah's prayer from the belly is *witnessed and testified to*, never voiced as Yonah — the Angels sing *about* the man crying out of the belly of Sheol. Yahuah's closing question is His word, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said. Impersonal creation the text supplies (the great wind and the tempest, the lots, the great fish appointed to save, the weeds about his head, the gourd grown in a night, the worm, the vehement east wind, the sackcloth on man and beast) may be *invoked as image*, never made the singer. We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *One album. Five songs. No honest zeros — every narrative unit of Jonah is sung. All four chapters accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap, and the floor flexes below it: this book holds five songs, and five is what it gets. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — MERCY HE COULD NOT OUTRUN
### The Fleeing Prophet and the Pity of Yahuah · Jonah 1–4 · five songs

A prophet is sent to preach mercy to an enemy city and runs the other way — pays a fare, goes down to the harbor, goes down into a ship, goes down toward the edge of the world to get away from a Presence no ship can outrun. So the sea comes after him: a great wind hurled onto the water, pagan sailors praying while the prophet sleeps, a lot that finds him, and a great fish appointed — not to devour, but to catch him as he sinks. Out of the belly of Sheol he cries, and the deep gives him back. Then the errand again, and a single sentence topples a city from the king to the cattle; and the man who was spared by mercy sits down east of the town, furious that it was spared. The witnesses sing the whole strange chase — the flight, the storm, the deep's mercy, the city's turning, and the vine and the worm and the question Yahuah leaves hanging in the air.

*Why this title:* every turn of the book is a mercy Yonah tries to escape and cannot — the mercy-errand he flees, the deep that saves instead of drowning him, the pardon that spares an enemy he wanted destroyed, the very shade over his head. He runs from mercy, is rescued by mercy, resents mercy — and mercy outruns him at every step, all the way to a question he never answers.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **He Paid to Flee the Presence** | Jonah 1:1–3 | The Gimel — the foot, the journey — turned the wrong way; with the Angels | Sent to *arise and go* to Nineveh, Yonah instead rises to flee to Tarshish — the opposite horizon — and the text sinks him step by step: down to Yafo, down into the ship, the fare paid out of his own hand, going down "from the presence of Yahuah," a man buying passage away from the One who made the very sea he means to cross |
| 2 | **The Pagans Prayed While the Prophet Slept** | Jonah 1:4–16 | The Angels, watching the great wind hurled and the lot come down | Yahuah hurls a great wind, the tempest cracks the ship, and every mariner cries to his own god and throws the cargo overboard — while Yonah lies fast asleep in the hold and must be woken to pray; the lot falls on him, the sea will not be rowed against, and when they cast him in it goes still — and the pagans who feared their idols now fear Yahuah, and sacrifice |
| 3 | **A Fish Appointed to Save Him** | Jonah 1:17–2:10 | The Mem — the water, the deep and the many waters — with the Angels, testifying to the man who prayed | A great fish *appointed* — sent as rescue, not as monster — swallows him as he sinks, and three days and three nights the deep holds its mystery; out of the belly of Sheol he cries, weeds wrapped about his head, gone down to the bottoms of the mountains, the earth's bars behind him — and the water that should have been his grave becomes the road up, till "salvation is of Yahuah" and the fish sets him back on dry land |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the flight opens the album in descent — the Veil, a prophet going down and down away from the call; the storm is the Turn, the sea sent after him and the pagans out-praying him at the rail; the great fish and the prayer are the Selah — the still, sunk center where the three days are held as mystery and the deep turns to mercy; Nineveh's repentance is the Unveiling and the Declaration, a city and its beasts turning at a single sentence; and the vine and the question land the album as its Benediction — the pity of Yahuah stated as a question and left hanging, so the last note the witnesses hold is awe, not resolution. The book is already built as a descent that turns to deliverance and an anger that meets mercy; the text's own order *is* the shape, and no reordering improves on it.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1:1–3 the call and the flight to Tarshish · 1:4–16 the storm, the sleeping prophet, the lot, and the sea made still · 1:17–2:10 the great fish, the three days, the prayer from the deep, and dry land · 3 the second word, the forty days, and the city's repentance in sackcloth · 4 the anger, the gourd and the worm, the east wind, and Yahuah's closing question. Five for five.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine). The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy — always Elohim or Yahuah. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Yonah, Nineveh, Tarshish, and Yafo are kept in their Hebraic forms; the *kikayon* (gourd) and the *dag gadol* (great fish) are named as the text names them. Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** the three days and three nights in the belly of the great fish are carried as the mystery within their own hour — sung as descent, dread, and deliverance, never resolved forward to any "sign of Jonah" or later meaning. No New-Testament hindsight enters any song; Yeshua is named and adored in the framing, but not inside a Jonah-moment song. The book's astonishment — the pity of Yahuah for a pagan city, the mercy the prophet resents and heaven marvels at — is sung as the wonder it is, with the closing question left open exactly as the text leaves it.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter — the Gimel keyed to the foot and the journey turned the wrong way (the flight), the Mem keyed to the water and the deep (the belly and the many waters). Yonah, the shipmaster, the mariners, and the king of Nineveh are *witnessed*, never made the singer; Yonah's prayer from the belly is testified to from heaven's side — "out of the belly of Sheol cried he" — never sung as his own first person. Yahuah's closing question is sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said, never voiced as a human. The great fish is sung as the deep's strange mercy — *appointed* to rescue, not a monster; and the great wind, the lots, the weeds, the gourd, the worm, and the east wind are invoked as images, never voiced.
- **Picture Letters, never a count:** the Letters are named as personae where used; no total is ever stated and no gematria mechanism is sung. The text's own numbers are content, not method — the three days and three nights, the forty days of Nineveh, the "more than sixscore thousand persons that cannot discern between their right hand and their left," and the much cattle.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
