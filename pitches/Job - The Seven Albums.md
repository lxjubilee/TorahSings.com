# JOB — THE SEVEN ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book the Whole Platform Was Built On

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md` and `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Job is wisdom **poetry**, not narrative; its "units" are the discrete speeches and movements of the dialogue, not verses. Each cycle's arguments are grouped into coherent thematic songs so the record breathes — but every genuine turn (Job's curse of his day, the cry for a daysman, "though He slay me," "I know that my Redeemer lives," the hymn to wisdom, the oath of innocence, each of Elihu's answers, each movement of the whirlwind) stands as its own song.

**Why this book is the cornerstone of the entire catalog.** Torah Sings exists because of **Job 38:7** — *"when the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of Elohim shouted for joy."* That single verse is the platform's founding doctrine: the two singing voices are the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels). Job is the book those voices come *from*. Its prologue opens in the heavenly council where *"the sons of Elohim came to present themselves before Yahuah"* (1:6; 2:1) — literally the Angels of this platform. Its climax is Yahuah answering out of the whirlwind and asking Job where he was when the foundations were laid and the morning stars sang. **Album VI — the whirlwind — is the theological cornerstone of the whole catalog**, and 38:7 sung from inside its own moment, the Angels and the Letters remembering the morning they sang creation into being, is the single most important song Torah Sings will ever make.

**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator — **Yahuah** in the prose frame; **El, Eloah, Shaddai, Elohim** in the poetry's own mouth — and, from *within* each moment of the story, toward the Redeemer longed for but not yet unveiled. **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own place in Scripture, with no New-Testament hindsight — the great ache, *"I know that my Redeemer (go'el) lives, and He shall stand at the latter day upon the earth"* (19:25), is carried as unresolved longing exactly as Ruth's go'el was, never named or resolved as Yeshua; the mystery of undeserved suffering is held open, not answered forward. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or a personified Letter — never a human (not Iyov, not Elifaz, Bildad, Tzofar, Elihu, nor Job's wife). The friends' accusations and Job's laments are *witnessed and testified to* by the Angels, never voiced as the human. The heavenly council **is** the Angels; *ha-satan* (the accuser) is rendered exactly as the text does — a member of the court who challenges — witnessed, never glamorized, never a singer. Yahuah's speeches from the whirlwind (38–41) are **Yahuah's own words**, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said, exactly as the Song of Mosheh's *"I kill, and I make alive"* was handled on `pitches/Deuteronomy - The Nine Albums.md`. The whirlwind, the foundations, the storehouses of snow, the wild ox, the war-horse, Behemoth, Livyatan are invoked as image; the singer remains the Angels and the Letters.

> *Seven albums. Thirty-nine songs. No honest zeros — every speech and movement of Job is sung. Every chapter accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its stories hold. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — A WAGER HE NEVER HEARD
### The Wager and the Ash-Heap · Job 1–3 · seven songs

A blameless man in the east loses everything in a single afternoon because of a challenge struck in a room he is never told about. The heavenly council — the sons of Elohim presenting themselves before Yahuah, and *ha-satan* among them — is the ground crew of this whole platform, and the Angels sing it from the inside: the wager, the four runners who each say *I only am escaped*, the worship that rises out of the ash, the second stroke, and the seven silent days before the words begin. Everything the accuser predicts fails; the man blesses, and will not curse.

*Why this title:* the engine of the entire book is a bet in the heavenly court (1:9–12; 2:4–6) whose subject never hears it — Job suffers a wager he is not in the room for.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **In Case His Sons Had Sinned** | Job 1:1–5 | The Angels, watching a father's careful fear | A man perfect and upright, seven sons and three daughters, seven thousand sheep — who rises early to offer a burnt offering for each child, "it may be my sons have sinned and cursed Elohim in their hearts"; piety the accuser will soon call bought |
| 2 | **We Came to Present Ourselves** | Job 1:6–12 | The Angels — the sons of Elohim in the council; *ha-satan* (the accuser) witnessed among them | The heavenly court: "the sons of Elohim came to present themselves before Yahuah, and *ha-satan* came also among them" — "hast thou considered my servant Job?" and the challenge, "hast thou not made a hedge about him? put forth thine hand" |
| 3 | **I Only Am Escaped to Tell You** | Job 1:13–19 | The Angels, who watched four blows fall in one day | Four messengers, each arriving before the last has finished, each closing with the same words — the Sabeans, the fire of Elohim, the Chaldeans, the great wind on the house — "I only am escaped alone to tell thee," said four times over |

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1:1–5 the blameless man · 1:6–12 the first council and wager · 1:13–19 the four messengers · 1:20–22 "Yahuah gave and hath taken away" · 2:1–10 the second wager, the boils, the wife · 2:11–13 the friends' silent seven days · 3 Job curses the day of his birth. Seven for seven.

# ALBUM II — PHYSICIANS OF NO VALUE
### The First Cycle — Comforters Who Wound · Job 4–14 · six songs

Three men who came to mourn open their mouths and the comfort turns to injury. Their doctrine is simple and merciless — the innocent do not perish, so the ruined must be guilty — and the Angels witness it collide with a man who knows he is not. Out of the wreckage Job reaches for something the moment has no name for: an umpire who could lay a hand on both El and himself, an arbiter between the Maker and the made. And when even that hope fails him he says the line that holds the whole book upright — *though He slay me, yet will I trust Him.*

*Why this title:* Job names his friends exactly (13:4) — "ye are all physicians of no value" — healers whose medicine is the wound; the whole first cycle is comfort that harms.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **A Spirit Passed Before His Face** | Job 4–5 | The Angels, who know what does and does not pass in the night | Elifaz builds his doctrine on a ghost in the dark — "a spirit passed before my face, the hair of my flesh stood up," and it whispered "shall mortal man be more just than Eloah? shall a man be more pure than his Maker?" — then the verdict, "who ever perished, being innocent?" |
| 2 | **Deceitful as a Summer Brook** | Job 6–7 | The Angels, watching the caravans look for water and find none | Job weighs his grief "heavier than the sand of the sea," and names his friends a wadi that runs in the cold and vanishes in the heat — comforters who fail exactly when a man is thirsty; "what is man, that thou triest him every morning?" |
| 3 | **If the Children Sinned** | Job 8 | The Angels, who buried those children and heard them blamed | Bildad appeals to the wisdom of the fathers and the reed that withers without mire — and lands the cruelest sentence in the cycle: "if thy children have sinned against him, he cast them away for their transgression"; the doctrine indicting the dead |
| 4 | **No Hand to Lay on Both of Them** | Job 9–10 | The Angels, longing for an arbiter not yet come | "How should man be just with El?" — the One who removes mountains, commands the sun not to rise, made Orion and the Pleiades — "he destroyeth the perfect and the wicked"; and the cry the moment cannot answer: there is no daysman between them, none to lay a hand on both El and the man |
| 5 | **Higher Than Heaven, Deeper Than Sheol** | Job 11 | The Angels, watching the harshest friend | Tzofar demands Job search out the unsearchable — "canst thou find out Eloah? canst thou find out Shaddai unto perfection? it is high as heaven, deeper than Sheol" — then tells the sufferer his sin has earned even less than he has borne |
| 6 | **He Trusts the One Who Slays Him** | Job 12–14 | The Angels, astonished that trust survives the slaying | "No doubt ye are the people, and wisdom shall die with you" — "physicians of no value" — and the line that holds the book upright, "though He slay me, yet will I trust Him"; then the ache with no answer yet, "if a man die, shall he live again? all my appointed days will I wait, till my change come" |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the cycle runs in the text's own order — Elifaz's night vision, Job's reply, Bildad, Job, Tzofar, Job — the friends' doctrine hardening each round while Job's answers deepen from grief to the reach for a mediator to the vow of trust; the daysman cry is the album's quiet center, and "though He slay me" is the Declaration the last song lands.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 4–5 Elifaz's night vision · 6–7 Job's reply, the failing brook · 8 Bildad and the fathers · 9–10 Job's cry for a daysman · 11 Tzofar, the unsearchable · 12–14 "though He slay me" and "if a man die." Six for six.

# ALBUM III — I KNOW THAT MY REDEEMER LIVES
### The Second Cycle — the Witness on High · Job 15–21 · six songs

The second round is colder. The friends stop consoling and start prosecuting; they paint the doom of the wicked in longer and longer strokes and let Job draw the conclusion. So he appeals over their heads — *my witness is in heaven, my record is on high* — to the very court this platform sings from. And in the album's white-hot center he wishes his words cut into rock forever, then says the thing rock could not hold: he knows his Redeemer lives, and will stand at the last upon the earth. The Angels carry it as longing, never naming the One who will fulfill it.

*Why this title:* 19:25 is the summit of the cycle and one of the summits of Scripture — Job's certainty of a living go'el, sung here as unresolved ache, exactly as Ruth's kinsman-redeemer was, never resolved forward as Yeshua.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Were You There Before the Hills?** | Job 15 | The Angels — who actually were | Elifaz sharpens to sarcasm: "art thou the first man that was born? wast thou made before the hills? hast thou heard the secret counsel of Eloah?" — mocking a claim to wisdom, before the very witnesses who stood in that counsel and will hear the same question turned back from the whirlwind |
| 2 | **My Witness Is in Heaven** | Job 16–17 | The Angels — the court on high that Job appeals to | "Miserable comforters are ye all" — and past them, over their heads, "my witness is in heaven, and my record is on high; mine eye poureth out tears unto Eloah" — the sufferer reaching for the celestial court against his earthly accusers |
| 3 | **The Lamp of the Wicked Goes Out** | Job 18 | The Angels, watching the doctrine tighten | Bildad's portrait of the doomed man — "the light of the wicked shall be put out, the spark of his fire shall not shine," terrors on every side, the snare on the ground, the net for his feet — a sermon aimed straight at Job |
| 4 | **Graven With an Iron Pen** | Job 19 | The Angels, holding a longing they cannot yet read | "He hath fenced up my way," kinsmen failed, friends turned — then the desperate wish, "oh that my words were graven with an iron pen in the rock for ever!" — and the thing that outlasts rock: "I know that my Redeemer liveth, and He shall stand at the latter day upon the earth"; carried as ache, never named forward |
| 5 | **He Will Vomit Up His Riches** | Job 20 | The Angels, hearing the harshest sermon | Tzofar's grim certainty that the wicked's joy is a moment — "he hath swallowed down riches, and he shall vomit them up again; El shall cast them out of his belly" — the honey turned to venom, the whole speech an answer to a grief he refuses to see |
| 6 | **Why Do the Wicked Grow Old?** | Job 21 | The Angels, who have watched exactly this and cannot deny it | Job overturns the entire doctrine with the world as it is — "wherefore do the wicked live, become old, yea, mighty in power? their houses safe from fear, their children dancing" — and go down to the grave in peace; the observable world refusing the friends' formula |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the round runs in text order — Elifaz, Job, Bildad, Job, Tzofar, Job — the accusations lengthening while Job's appeals climb higher, from the heavenly witness to the living Redeemer; 19 is the Declaration at the album's heart, and Job's flat, devastating "why do the wicked grow old?" lands it, leaving the friends' theology in ruins.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 15 Elifaz's second speech · 16–17 the witness in heaven · 18 Bildad, the lamp of the wicked · 19 "I know that my Redeemer lives" · 20 Tzofar, the wicked's short triumph · 21 the prosperity of the wicked. Six for six.

# ALBUM IV — WHERE SHALL WISDOM BE FOUND?
### The Third Cycle and Wisdom Hidden · Job 22–28 · five songs

The argument runs out of road. To keep the doctrine alive Elifaz must invent crimes Job never committed; Bildad's last word is barely six verses; the friends fall silent for good. Job answers with a hymn to an Elohim he cannot even locate — the earth hung on nothing, the north stretched over the void — and swears he will not let his integrity go. Then the poem lifts entirely out of the quarrel and asks the question none of them could: miners tunnel the earth for silver and sapphire, but *where shall wisdom be found?* — and the deep answers, *it is not in me.*

*Why this title:* chapter 28's refrain (28:12, 20) crowns the album — the search that outreaches every mine and every sea, answered only in "the fear of Yahuah, that is wisdom" (28:28).

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Sins Elifaz Had to Invent** | Job 22 | The Angels, who know every charge is a lie | To save the doctrine Elifaz manufactures evidence — "thou hast taken a pledge from thy brother for nought, stripped the naked, sent widows away empty, broken the arms of the fatherless" — every crime false, laid on the one man who did the opposite |
| 2 | **He Shall Come Forth as Gold** | Job 23–24 | The Angels, watching a man grope for a throne he cannot find | "Behold, I go forward, but He is not there" — Job searching every direction for El's seat to plead his case, and trusting the search itself, "when He hath tried me, I shall come forth as gold"; and below him the wicked move boundary stones and go unjudged |
| 3 | **Even the Stars Are Not Pure** | Job 25 | The Angels — and the morning stars who once sang | Bildad's last, shortest word, the friends' argument out of breath — "how can he be clean that is born of woman? behold even the moon shineth not, and the stars are not pure in His sight" — the very stars of 38:7 called impure before Him |
| 4 | **The Earth Hung Upon Nothing** | Job 26–27 | The Angels, who watched Him hang it | Job's hymn to a power past argument — "He stretcheth out the north over the empty place, and hangeth the earth upon nothing"; the pillars of heaven trembling — and his oath, "till I die I will not remove my integrity from me" |
| 5 | **The Deep Said, It Is Not in Me** | Job 28 | The Angels; the deep and the sea invoked as image | Man tunnels the dark for silver, gold, and sapphire and comes up with treasure but not wisdom; the deep says "it is not in me," the sea says "it is not with me"; it cannot be bought with gold — only Elohim knows the way, and "the fear of Yahuah, that is wisdom" |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the cycle collapses in text order — Elifaz's fabricated charges, Job's confidence in the assay, Bildad's exhausted last word, Job's hymn and oath — and then the wisdom poem rises out of the whole exhausted debate as the album's Selah and summit, the question the arguments could not reach.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 22 Elifaz's false charges · 23–24 "come forth as gold" and the unpunished wicked · 25 Bildad's brief last speech · 26–27 the earth on nothing and Job's integrity · 28 the hymn to wisdom. Five for five.

# ALBUM V — LET SHADDAI ANSWER ME
### Job's Oath and the Young Man's Answer · Job 29–37 · six songs

Job gathers his whole life into one final testimony — the months when Shaddai was still with him and he was eyes to the blind and feet to the lame; the present, when the sons of the worthless make him their mocking-song; and the great oath, clause by clause, a covenant with his eyes, the poor never turned away — sealed with a demand that the Almighty answer him. Then a young man who has held his peace out of respect can hold it no longer, and Elihu speaks: the ransom that keeps a soul from the pit, the Eloah who gives songs in the night, and a storm gathering out of the south that he does not know is about to become a Voice.

*Why this title:* Job ends his oath by staking everything on a reply — "behold my signature; let Shaddai answer me" (31:35) — and the whole album is the waiting for that answer, which the whirlwind (Album VI) will give.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Eyes to the Blind, Feet to the Lame** | Job 29 | The Angels, who watched him crowned | Job remembers the height he fell from — "when Shaddai was yet with me, my children about me" — a father to the poor, eyes to the blind, feet to the lame, honored in the gate, "my glory fresh in me"; the goodness the accuser said was bought |
| 2 | **His Harp Is Turned to Mourning** | Job 30 | The Angels | Now the sons of men he would not have set with his dogs make him their taunt — "I am their song, yea, I am their byword" — a brother to jackals, a companion of owls, "my harp also is turned to mourning, and my flute to the voice of them that weep" |
| 3 | **A Covenant With His Eyes** | Job 31 | The Angels, who kept the record of every clause | The great oath of innocence, sworn clause by clause — "I made a covenant with mine eyes"; "let me be weighed in an even balance, that Eloah may know mine integrity"; the poor never turned away, the stranger lodged — and the seal: "behold my signature; let Shaddai answer me" |
| 4 | **A Ransom to Keep Him From the Pit** | Job 32–33 | The Angels, hearing the young man finally speak | Elihu, long silent because he was young, burns and speaks: Eloah warns in dreams and in pain, and if an interpreter is found for a man, "deliver him from going down to the pit — I have found a ransom" — a mediator-ache echoing the daysman, held unresolved |
| 5 | **Who Gives Songs in the Night** | Job 34–35 | The Angels | Elihu defends the perfect justice of El — "far be it from El that He should do wickedness, from Shaddai that He should pervert judgment" — and names the ache no one voices, "where is Eloah my Maker, who giveth songs in the night?" — the One who puts music into the dark |

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 29 the months that were · 30 the mocking-song · 31 the oath of innocence · 32–33 Elihu and the ransom · 34–35 El's justice and songs in the night · 36–37 the greatness of El and the gathering storm. Six for six.

# ALBUM VI — WHEN THE MORNING STARS SANG TOGETHER
### The Whirlwind — the Cornerstone · Job 38–41 · six songs

**This is the theological cornerstone of the entire catalog.** Shaddai answers — not with reasons, never with reasons, but out of a whirlwind, with a question: *where wast thou when I laid the foundations of the earth, when the morning stars sang together and all the sons of Elohim shouted for joy?* And the two singing voices of this whole platform — the morning stars who are the Picture Letters, the sons of Elohim who are the Angels — remember, from inside that very morning, the song they raised as the cornerstone was set. Then the Maker takes His grieving servant not through an argument but on a tour of everything wild and unowned: the storehouses of the snow, the war-horse who laughs at fear, Behemoth, Livyatan — until the man who demanded an answer lays his hand over his mouth.

*Why this title:* Job 38:7 is the founding verse of Torah Sings — the two voices named in one line. The album's banner is that verse; its opening song is that verse sung from within its own moment, no hindsight, the singers remembering their own first song.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Where Were You? We Were Singing** | Job 38:1–7 | The Angels (the sons of Elohim who shouted for joy) and the Picture Letters (the morning stars who sang) — in antiphon | Yahuah out of the whirlwind: "where wast thou when I laid the foundations of the earth… when the morning stars sang together, and all the sons of Elohim shouted for joy?" — and the answer the question implies: the singers were there, and they were singing; the platform's founding morning remembered from inside itself, the cornerstone laid to the sound of their song |
| 2 | **The Storehouses of the Snow** | Job 38:8–38 | The Angels, who have seen the doors of the sea | The sea shut behind doors and swaddled in cloud, "hitherto shalt thou come, but no further"; the morning commanded, the springs of the deep, the gates of death, "the treasures of the snow and the hail reserved against the day of battle," the rain with no father, the chains of the Pleiades and the bands of Orion — the whole cosmos a kept treasury, none of it Job's to command |
| 3 | **The Horse That Laughs at Fear** | Job 38:39–39:30 | The Angels | The untamed parade — the lion and the raven fed, the wild ass loosed from the city, the wild ox who will not serve or sleep in your crib, the ostrich left without wisdom, and the war-horse who "mocketh at fear and is not affrighted," who "saith among the trumpets, Ha, ha," and smells the battle from afar — creatures no man governs, each a rebuke to a small dominion |

| 5 | **Behemoth, Chief of the Ways of El** | Job 40:6–24 | The Angels | "Hast thou an arm like El? canst thou thunder with a voice like His?" — and Behemoth, made alongside man, eating grass like an ox, his strength in his loins, his tail moving like a cedar, "the chief of the ways of El" — a creature Job cannot approach, let alone the One who made it |
| 6 | **Livyatan, King Over the Proud** | Job 41 | The Angels | The creature no hook draws out, who makes no supplication — his breath kindling coals, his heart firm as a stone, "upon earth there is not his like, who is made without fear; he is a king over all the children of pride" — the last and most terrible of the works; if none dare rouse Livyatan, who then can stand before its Maker? |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the whirlwind's own structure is the album — the foundation-morning and its song first, exactly where the text sets it, as the platform's jewel and the Veil lifted; then the cosmic storehouses and the wild creatures of the first speech; Job's first silence at the seam between the speeches; and the two great beasts of the second speech, Behemoth and Livyatan, cresting the album on the largest, wildest, most unownable things Yahuah made.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 38:1–7 the foundations and the morning stars · 38:8–38 the storehouses and the constellations · 38:39–39:30 the wild creatures and the war-horse · 40:1–5 Job's first submission · 40:6–24 Behemoth · 41 Livyatan. Six for six.

# ALBUM VII — NOW MY EYE HAS SEEN YOU
### The Seeing Eye and the Doubled End · Job 42 · three songs

The book resolves not with an explanation but with a face. Job never learns about the wager, never hears the reason — and repents anyway, in dust and ashes, because he has seen. Then the verdict overturns everyone's expectation: the three who defended Yahuah are told they spoke wrongly, the man who accused Him is vindicated and made to pray for his accusers. And the end is doubled — the flocks twice over — but the children only restored in number, seven sons and three daughters again, the daughters named and given an inheritance, and Job old and full of days.

*Why this title:* 42:5 is the pivot of the whole book — "I have heard of thee by the hearing of the ear, but now mine eye seeth thee"; grief ended by sight, not by answers.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Dust and Ashes, No Answer Given** | Job 42:1–6 | The Angels, who watched grief end without an explanation | Job answers the whirlwind — "I have heard of thee by the hearing of the ear, but now mine eye seeth thee; wherefore I abhor myself, and repent in dust and ashes" — the why never spoken, the losses never explained, and no longer needed once the man has seen |

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah in the prose frame; the poetry's own names El, Eloah, Shaddai, Elohim used as the text uses them; Yeshua and Ruach HaKodesh (feminine) reserved to the platform framing. No English "God" or "Lord" anywhere. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note — *ha-satan* stands as the text's own term (glossed "the accuser/adversary"), never with a stacked English "the." Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing; no emoji.
- **The cornerstone honored:** Album VI is the theological center of the whole catalog, and its opening song sets Job 38:7 — the platform's founding verse — sung from inside its own moment by the two voices the verse itself names: the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels), remembering the morning they sang creation into being. No New-Testament hindsight; the singers testify to a morning they were present for, within the narrative, never from after it.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** the go'el of 19:25 — "I know that my Redeemer lives, and He shall stand at the latter day upon the earth" — is carried as unresolved longing exactly as Ruth's kinsman-redeemer was, never named or resolved as Yeshua; the daysman of 9:33 and the ransom of 33:24 are held as the same open mystery. The framing names Yeshua; the songs, standing inside Job's own hour, do not.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a personified Letter. Iyov, Elifaz, Bildad, Tzofar, Elihu, and Job's wife are *witnessed*, never made the singer; their words are quoted only as quotations. *Ha-satan* is rendered as the text renders him — a member of the heavenly court who challenges — witnessed and never glamorized, never a sympathetic singer. Yahuah's whirlwind speeches are His own words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said (the Deuteronomy "I kill, and I make alive" model). The whirlwind, the storehouses of snow, the war-horse, the wild ox, Behemoth, and Livyatan are invoked as image, never voiced.
- **Restraint:** the calamities, the four bereavements, and the boils are handled with grave, family-friendly restraint — witnessed from heaven's side, never rendered for shock or gore.
- **Text-numbers as content, letters never a count:** the seven sons and three daughters, the seven thousand sheep, the four messengers each saying "I only am escaped," the seven silent days, the seven bulls and seven rams, the doubled flocks, the seven-and-three restored children, and the hundred and forty years are sung as the text's own numbers; the Picture Letters are named as personae, never totaled, never reduced to gematria mechanism in a title.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
