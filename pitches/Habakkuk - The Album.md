**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Isaiah - The Ten Albums.md`, and the Minor-Prophet pitches through `pitches/Micah - The Album.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Habakkuk is three chapters and one sustained dialogue: a prophet's complaint, Yahuah's answer, a second complaint, a second answer with five woes — and then a chapter that is not prose but an actual scored psalm. So it is one album. Every song below is one narrative unit; the boundaries sit on the text's own seams — the first complaint, the answer that raises the Kasdim, the second complaint, the watchtower-vision, the five woes sealed with silence, and the prayer upon Shigionoth.

**The whole book is built toward a song — the platform's most on-theme prophet.** Habakkuk 3 opens "A prayer of Chavaquq the prophet upon Shigionoth," carries *Selah* three times, and closes with the temple rubric "to the chief singer on my stringed instruments." It is a hymn with musical notation, the prophet's own song handed to the singers of the house. That is the deep note this record sounds: **the argument with heaven resolves into music.** A man accuses the Judge of silence and injustice, is answered twice, and ends by singing a theophany — Elohim from Teman, the sun and moon standing still in their habitation, the everlasting mountains scattered — and a defiant joy in a barren orchard. The Angels sing that summit; all creation is invited to join it.

**The narrative-time gate — 2:4 held within its own hour.** "The just shall live by his faith" (2:4) is sung as **the vision the prophet is told to wait for** — "though it tarry, wait for it; because it will surely come, it will not tarry" — and never resolved forward to any later use of the verse. So too the two great seals of the woes: "the earth shall be filled with the knowledge of the glory of Yahuah, as the waters cover the sea" (2:14) and "thou wentest forth for the salvation of thy people" (3:13) are carried as longing the witnesses cannot yet see fulfilled. No Yeshua, no cross, no fulfillment language inside any song; the ache is held open, exactly as Ruth's *go'el* and Isaiah's servant were held.

**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator — **Yahuah**, the everlasting, the Holy One — and, from *within* each hour of the dialogue, toward the salvation longed for but not yet unveiled. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or a personified Letter — never a human, and never Chavaquq. The prophet's complaints ("how long shall I cry?") and his closing resolve ("yet I will rejoice in Yahuah") are **witnessed and testified to**, never voiced as his. Yahuah's own answers — "I raise up the Kasdim," "write the vision, make it plain upon tables… the just shall live by his faith," "Yahuah is in his holy temple: let all the earth keep silence before him" — are **His words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said**. Impersonal imagery the text supplies (the tables the vision is written on, the drunkard's cup passed round, the stone that cries out of the wall, the sun and moon standing still, the fig tree that will not blossom, the empty stall, the hind's feet on the high places) is **invoked as image, never voiced.**

> *One album. Six songs. No honest zeros — every unit of Habakkuk is sung. All three chapters accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap, and the floor flexes below it: this book holds six songs, and six is what it gets. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — HE ARGUED WITH HEAVEN, AND SANG
### The Complaint, the Answer, and the Psalm · Habakkuk 1–3 · six songs

The book is a dialogue no other prophet dares so plainly: a man stands before Yahuah and accuses Him — of not hearing, of not saving, of watching the wicked swallow the righteous and holding His tongue. Twice he presses, and twice heaven answers — first with a remedy more terrible than the wound, the bitter and hasty Kasdim raised up; then with a vision to be written plain on tables and waited for, and five woes on the proud, sealed with the knowledge of Yahuah's glory filling the earth and the whole earth called to silence. And then the strangest resolution in the prophets: the argument becomes a hymn. The prophet who cried "how long?" ends upon Shigionoth, singing a theophany from Teman and Paran and a joy that will not fail though the orchard does. The Angels sing the whole arc — the accusation witnessed, the answers sung in awe, the psalm carried to its summit.

*Why this title:* Habakkuk is complaint and answer and complaint and answer — and then, in a chapter marked as a scored song and handed "to the chief singer on my stringed instruments," the dialogue resolves into music. The man who arraigned the Judge closes by singing. The title is the book's whole motion in one line, witnessed from heaven's side.

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the dialogue's own order is the album's shape and no reordering improves on it. The first complaint opens in grief — the Veil, the ache stated, a cry toward a heaven that will not answer; the answer that raises the Kasdim is the Turn, a remedy darker than the disease; the second complaint deepens the question the Pure One will not resolve; the watchtower-vision is the still center — "wait for it," the just living by faith; the five woes fall and end in a hush that fills the whole earth; and the prayer upon Shigionoth is the Benediction and the summit — the argument breaking into song, from the theophany's dread to a joy the barren orchard cannot take away. The book that began "how long?" ends handed to the chief singer.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1:1–4 the first complaint · 1:5–11 the answer, the Kasdim raised · 1:12–17 the second complaint, the net · 2:1–4 the watchtower and the written vision, "the just shall live by his faith" · 2:5–20 the five woes, the knowledge-glory, and the silence · 3 the prayer upon Shigionoth. Six for six.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine), and the book's own honest titles as it uses them: "the Holy One," "mine Holy One," "Yahuah my Elohim." The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy — Elohim and Yahuah stand in their place. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled English article on *Ha-* forms). *Selah* and *Shigionoth* are retained as the text's own musical terms; Chavaquq, Teman, Paran, Cushan, and the Kasdim are kept in Hebraic form. Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** inside every song the messianic ache is carried as mystery and longing, never named as fulfilled. "The just shall live by his faith" (2:4) is sung strictly as the vision the prophet is told to wait for — "though it tarry, wait for it; it will surely come" — and never resolved forward to any later use of the verse. "The earth shall be filled with the knowledge of the glory of Yahuah" (2:14) and "thou wentest forth for the salvation of thy people" (3:13) are held as longing the witnesses cannot yet see fulfilled. The framing names and adores Yeshua; the songs, standing inside Chavaquq's own hour, do not.
- **The scored psalm honored:** Habakkuk 3 — "Joy When the Orchard Fails" — is sung as one summit song, honoring the chapter's own nature as a hymn with musical notation ("upon Shigionoth," *Selah* three times, "to the chief singer on my stringed instruments"). It is not split; it is one psalm, one unit, one song. The theophany (Teman and Paran, the sun and moon standing still, the everlasting mountains scattered) and the closing landing ("yet I will rejoice in Yahuah… he will make my feet like hinds' feet") are both carried in full — the reverent, weighty opening rising to the album's soaring close, the argument with heaven resolved into music.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter — the Tav keyed to the vision written plain on the tables (2:2), the Gimel keyed to the feet made like a hind's on the high places (3:19, the foot its pictograph). Chavaquq is *witnessed*, never made the singer; his complaints and his closing resolve to rejoice are testified to from heaven's side, never sung as his first person. Yahuah's own answers — "I raise up the Kasdim," "write the vision… the just shall live by his faith," "let all the earth keep silence" — are His words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said. The tables, the drunkard's cup, the stone that cries from the wall, the sun and moon, the fig tree, the empty stall, and the hind's feet are invoked as image, never voiced.
- **Picture Letters, never a count:** the Letters are named as personae where used; no total is ever stated and no gematria mechanism is sung. The text's own numbers are content, not method — the five woes, the three *Selah*, the two houses of Israel's foe, the appointed time waited for.
- **Restraint:** the Kasdim's violence and the five woes are handled with grave, family-friendly restraint, witnessed from heaven's side, never rendered for shock — the harshness of the answer and the woes serve the paradox the psalm resolves, not spectacle.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5); Habakkuk 3's own rubric ("upon Shigionoth… to the chief singer on my stringed instruments") makes the closing song's Hebraic scoring native to the text.
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
