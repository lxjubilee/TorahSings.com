# FIRST KINGS — THE SIX ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book of the House and the Whisper

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, `pitches/Joshua - The Five Albums.md`, and `pitches/Ruth - The Album.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Every song below is one narrative unit; every unit boundary sits on the text's own seams — the enthronement and succession accounts, the building formulas, the regnal openings ("in the such-and-such year of the king began so-and-so to reign"), and the prophetic-word markers ("the word of Yahuah came unto…") that 1 Kings supplies in place of the Genesis *toledot*. The dense regnal-formula chapters (15–16) are gathered into coherent kings-of-Yehudah and kings-of-Israel units per §3.3 rather than split reign by reign.
**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator, Yahuah — and, from *within* each moment of the story, toward the Son promised but not yet unveiled. **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own place in Scripture, with no New-Testament hindsight — Yeshua is named and adored in the platform's framing, but inside a First-Kings-moment song the house built for a Name the heavens cannot contain, the wisdom that discerns the true mother, the kingdom torn for idolatry, the jar that would not empty, the fire that fell on Carmel, and the sound of thin silence are carried as mystery and ache, never named as accomplished fulfillment. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or the personified Letters — never a human character (not Shlomo, not David, not Eliyahu, not Achav, not Izevel) or anything derived from one. Shlomo's dedication prayer and Eliyahu's cries are *witnessed and testified to* by the Angels, never sung as the human. First Kings supplies its own celestial witnesses above all others — the Angels as the two cherubim beaten and carved into the Holy Place (6–8); heaven itself at Carmel the moment the fire fell (18); and the messenger who touched Eliyahu awake under the broom tree (19); the impersonal creation the text supplies (the cedars of Lebanon, the bronze sea on its oxen, the cloud that filled the house, the ravens, the jar of meal, the little cloud like a hand, the arrow drawn at a venture) may be *invoked as image*, never made the singer. We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *Six albums. Forty-one songs. No honest zeros — every narrative unit of First Kings is sung. All twenty-two chapters accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its stories hold — the Eliyahu cycle runs to nine. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — GIVE YOUR SERVANT A LISTENING HEART
### Enthronement and the Gift of Wisdom · 1 Kings 1–4 · six songs

A dying David's throne is contested before it is even empty — one son crowns himself at a feast by the spring while the true heir rides the old king's mule to Gichon — and the young king who inherits it makes it firm through four reckonings in blood, then kneels at Giv'on and asks heaven for the one thing no king asks: not long life, not riches, not the necks of his enemies, but a hearing heart to judge a people too many to number. The witnesses sing the throne secured and the gift that answered a humble request with more than it asked — a wisdom that hangs a living child on a raised sword to find its mother, and runs wider than the sand of the sea.

*Why this title:* Shlomo's own request at Giv'on — *leb shomea*, a listening heart (3:9) — is the album's spine and the gift that defines the reign before it curdles.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **One Grasped the Throne, One Rode the Mule** | 1 Kings 1 | The Angels, who knew which son the king had named | Adoniyah exalts himself — "I will be king" — and feasts at En-Rogel, while a shivering David can get no heat; and the true heir is sent down to Gichon on the king's own mule, anointed to a trumpet, until Adoniyah's guests scatter and he clings to the horns of the altar |
| 2 | **Be Strong, and Show Yourself a Man** | 1 Kings 2:1–12 | The Angels, who watched the shepherd-king die | David's last charge — keep the charge of Yahuah, walk in his ways — laid alongside the debts of Yoav's blood and Shim'i's curse; then the shepherd who was taken from the sheepfold sleeps with his fathers in the city of David |
| 3 | **The Kingdom Made Firm in Blood** | 1 Kings 2:13–46 | The Angels | Four reckonings settle the throne — Adoniyah's last bid answered with death, Evyatar banished, Shim'i's leash drawn tight — and Yoav, the man of blood, clutching the horns of the altar that could not shelter him; "the kingdom was established in the hand of Shlomo" |
| 4 | **He Asked to Hear, Not to Reign** | 1 Kings 3:1–15 | The Angels, who heard heaven offer everything | At the great high place of Giv'on, offered "ask what I shall give thee," a young king asks not for long life, not riches, not the lives of his enemies, but a hearing heart to discern good and evil — and is given the wisdom, and all the rest he did not ask |
| 5 | **A Sword Raised to Find the Mother** | 1 Kings 3:16–28 | The Angels, who saw the true mother's bowels yearn | Two women, one living child, one dead — and "bring me a sword; divide the living child in two"; the blade lifted not to kill but to reveal, the true mother betrayed by her mercy, and all Israel afraid, for the wisdom of Elohim was in him |
| 6 | **Wisdom Wider Than the Sand** | 1 Kings 4 | The Letters, who carry every proverb and song he spoke | Twelve officers provisioning the king a month each, a people as many as the sand eating and drinking and glad; and a largeness of heart wide as the seashore — three thousand proverbs, a thousand and five songs, the cedar and the hyssop and the beasts all named |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the contested crown opens the album under threat — the Veil; the dying charge and the four reckonings darken and secure it; Giv'on is the turn to grace, the humble request; the sword that finds the mother is the Declaration, wisdom made visible; and the breadth of the wisdom lands the album, the reign at its bright noon before Album III's shadow.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1 the contested throne · 2:1–12 David's charge and death · 2:13–46 the throne made firm · 3:1–15 the dream at Giv'on (3:1–4, the marriage and the high places, absorbed) · 3:16–28 the two mothers and the sword · 4 the officers and the breadth of wisdom. Six for six.

# ALBUM II — WHAT THE HEAVENS COULD NOT HOLD
### The House for the Name · 1 Kings 5–9:9 · seven songs

The One the heaven of heavens cannot contain asks for a house of cedar and gold, and a Phoenician king floats the forests of Lebanon down the sea to build it. It rises without the sound of a single iron tool; and when the ark is carried in, the cloud fills the house so full the priests cannot stand to minister — and the king who built it opens his mouth only to confess that it is far too small for its Guest. The witnesses sing the seven years, the silent stones, the two pillars, the bronze sea on twelve oxen, the cherubim whose wings they wear — and the warning laid over all that gold: that this glory hangs by a thread called faithfulness, and could yet become a heap.

*Why this title:* Shlomo's dedication prayer states the album's whole paradox — "the heaven of heavens cannot contain thee; how much less this house that I have builded" (8:27).

| 2 | **No Iron Was Heard While It Rose** | 1 Kings 6 | The Bet — the house itself — with the Angels | In the four hundred and eightieth year the house is built, and "neither hammer nor axe nor any tool of iron heard in the house while it was in building" — every stone made ready far off, a temple rising in total silence, seven years to the day |

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 5 the treaty with Chiram and the levy · 6 the house built for the Name · 7:1–12 the king's own house · 7:13–51 the pillars, the bronze sea, the vessels · 8:1–21 the ark and the cloud · 8:22–66 the dedication prayer and the feast · 9:1–9 the second appearing and the conditioned covenant. # ALBUM III — HE TURNED, AND THE KINGDOM TORE
### The Glory, the Turning, the Tearing · 1 Kings 9:10–12 · seven songs

The wisest, richest king who ever reigned receives a queen struck breathless by the half she was never told, and a kingdom whose floors are paved in gold and whose silver is counted as stones — and then loves seven hundred foreign wives who turn the listening heart away, until the same heaven that gave him everything decrees the kingdom torn like a garment. The witnesses sing the glory at its peak and the fault-line running through it: the twenty cities a foreign king scorned, the adversaries stirred awake, the prophet's new cloak ripped into ten pieces, the foolish son who answers a plea for mercy with scorpions, and two golden calves set up where the calf of Sinai had already been answered once.

*Why this title:* his heart was turned from Yahuah (11:9), and Achiyah tore the kingdom out of his hand (11:31) — cause and consequence, the whole arc of the album in five words.

| 2 | **The Half Had Not Been Told Her** | 1 Kings 10 | The Angels, who watched a queen lose her breath | The queen of Sheva comes with hard questions and camels of spice and gold, and there is no more spirit left in her — "the half was not told me"; an ivory throne overlaid with gold, six hundred sixty-six talents a year, and silver reckoned as nothing, as the stones of the street |

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 9:10–28 the twenty cities and the fleet · 10 the queen of Sheva and the gold · 11:1–13 the foreign wives and the kingdom decreed torn · 11:14–25 the adversaries raised · 11:26–43 Achiyah's torn cloak and Shlomo's death · 12:1–24 Rechav'am's folly and the revolt · 12:25–33 the golden calves at Dan and Beit-El. # ALBUM IV — WORSE THAN ALL BEFORE HIM
### The Divided Thrones and the Rise of Achav · 1 Kings 13–16 · six songs

The kingdom is two now, and the word of Yahuah dogs both thrones — an altar split at Beit-El, a lion that kills a prophet and will not eat him, a blind seer who knows a disguised queen by her footfall, and the gold of the temple carried off to Egypt and answered with cheap bronze. The witnesses sing the crowns of the north passing from murderer to murderer while a single lamp is kept burning in the south for David's sake — a long descent that bottoms out when Achav takes Izevel to wife and builds Baal a house in Shomron, doing more to provoke Yahuah than all the kings who were before him.

*Why this title:* the verdict pronounced over Achav — "did evil in the sight of Yahuah above all that were before him" (16:30) — is the refrain of the whole northern descent and the hinge into the Eliyahu cycle.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 13 the man of Elohim, the old prophet, and the lion · 14:1–20 Achiyah's oracle and the child's death · 14:21–31 Rechav'am and Shishak's plunder · 15:1–24 the kings of Yehudah, Aviyam and Asa · 15:25–16:28 the northern usurpers, Nadav to Omri · 16:29–34 Achav, Izevel, and Baal in Shomron. Six for six.

# ALBUM V — A SOUND OF THIN SILENCE
### Eliyahu, the Fire and the Whisper · 1 Kings 17–19 · nine songs

One prophet stands against a kingdom that has married its god, and heaven feeds him the whole way — ravens at a drying brook, a widow's jar that will not empty in Baal's own country, breath called back into a dead boy. On Carmel the fire falls and drinks the water in the trench, and all Israel goes down on its face; but the prophet who called down fire then runs from one woman into the wilderness, begs to die under a broom tree, and learns at Chorev that Yahuah was not in the wind or the earthquake or the fire, but in a sound of thin silence. The witnesses — one of them the very messenger who touched him awake — sing the fire and the whisper, and the seven thousand knees that never bowed.

*Why this album is nine:* per the methodology (§3.3), a coherent cycle larger than seven carries all its songs rather than leave a beat unsung — the Eliyahu narrative is its own escalating musical, exactly as the plagues were on Exodus III.

*Why this title:* the answer at Chorev, *qol demamah daqqah* (19:12) — a still small voice, literally a sound of thin silence — the album's deepest paradox: the Elohim of the Carmel fire found in a whisper.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **The Ravens Fed Him Morning and Night** | 1 Kings 17:1–7 | The Angels | Eliyahu swears the sky shut — "no dew nor rain these years but by my word" — and hides at the brook Kerit, where unclean birds bring him bread and flesh at dawn and at dusk and do not devour it, until the brook itself dries for want of rain |
| 2 | **The Jar That Would Not Empty** | 1 Kings 17:8–16 | The Angels (the jar and cruse invoked as image, never voiced) | Sent to Tzarfat in Baal's own Sidon, he asks a widow gathering sticks for her last meal to feed him first; and the jar of meal does not waste and the cruse of oil does not fail, a last handful stretched to feed three through the whole drought |
| 3 | **The Breath Came Back Into the Boy** | 1 Kings 17:17–24 | The Hey — the breath — with the Angels | The widow's son sickens until no breath is left; the prophet carries him to the loft, stretches himself over him three times, and cries for the soul to return — and the breath comes back into the child, and the mother knows the word in his mouth is truth |
| 4 | **A Hundred Hidden in the Caves** | 1 Kings 18:1–19 | The Angels, who knew where each fifty was hidden | In the third year, sent to show himself to Achav, Eliyahu meets Ovadyah — who fears Yahuah greatly and has hidden a hundred prophets by fifties in caves, feeding them bread and water while Izevel cut down the rest; then the meeting: "art thou he that troubleth Israel?" |
| 5 | **The Fire Fell and Licked the Water** | 1 Kings 18:20–40 | The Shin — the fire — and heaven at Carmel the moment it fell, with the Angels | Four hundred fifty prophets cry to Baal from morning to noon and cut themselves till the blood runs, and no voice answers; then twelve stones, a trench flooded three times, one prayer — and the fire of Yahuah falls and eats the offering, the wood, the stones, the dust, and laps up the water; and the people fall: "Yahuah, he is the Elohim" |
| 6 | **A Cloud the Size of a Hand** | 1 Kings 18:41–46 | The Angels, watching the sky over the sea | On Carmel's height, face between his knees, the prophet sends his servant to look toward the sea seven times; on the seventh a little cloud like a man's hand rises — and the whole heaven blackens, and a great rain breaks a three-year drought, and the hand of Yahuah carries him running before the chariot |
| 7 | **We Touched Him Under the Broom Tree** | 1 Kings 19:1–8 | The Angels — the messenger who touched Eliyahu awake | The man who called down fire flees one woman's threat into the wilderness, sits under a broom tree, and begs to die — "it is enough"; and a messenger touches him and sets warm bread and a cruse of water at his head, twice, and in that strength he walks forty days to the mountain of Elohim |
| 8 | **Not in the Wind, Not the Fire** | 1 Kings 19:9–18 | The Angels, who felt the wind and the fire pass and knew He was not in them | In the cave at Chorev, "what doest thou here?" — a wind that rends the mountains, an earthquake, a fire, and Yahuah in none of them; and after the fire a sound of thin silence, and a wrapped face, and a word: seven thousand knees in Israel have never bowed to Baal |

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 17:1–7 the drought and the ravens · 17:8–16 the widow and the jar · 17:17–24 the son raised · 18:1–19 Ovadyah and the meeting with Achav · 18:20–40 Carmel and the fire · 18:41–46 the rain returns · 19:1–8 the broom tree and the messenger · 19:9–18 Chorev and the thin silence · 19:19–21 the call of Elisha. Nine for nine.

# ALBUM VI — AN ARROW DRAWN AT A VENTURE
### The Fall of Achav's House · 1 Kings 20–22 · six songs

Achav wins two wars he was handed and spares the king he was told to destroy, and a prophet in disguise makes him pronounce his own death over a parable. Then a vineyard he covets is bought with an innocent man's blood, and Eliyahu meets him with the question that ends a dynasty — have you killed, and also taken? The witnesses, who stood by the throne of heaven the day a lying spirit went out to lure him to Ramot-Gilad, sing the arrow drawn by no one in particular that found the one gap in his armor, and the dogs that licked his blood exactly where the word had said — and a book that ends with Baal still on the northern throne, the story not yet finished.

*Why this title:* Achav dies not to a champion but to chance — "a certain man drew a bow at a venture, and smote the king of Israel between the joints of the harness" (22:34) — the random arrow that was not random, the fall of the house made law.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **He Held the Valleys Too** | 1 Kings 20:1–34 | The Angels, who fought where Aram swore Yahuah could not reach | Ben-Hadad with thirty-two kings besieges Shomron; twice Israel is handed victory by the word of a prophet, "that thou mayest know that I am Yahuah" — and when Aram swears Yahuah rules the hills but not the valleys, the valley is given too; yet Achav lets the doomed king go and makes a covenant with him |

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine). The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy; the Canaanite deity is rendered **Baal** (the text's own name), and the idols Ashtoret, Milcom, Chemosh, and Molech are named as the text names them. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Personal and place names are kept in their Hebraic forms — Shlomo, Eliyahu, Elisha, Achav, Izevel, Yarov'am, Rechav'am, Yehoshafat, Michayahu, Navot, Chiram, Giv'on, Beit-El, Shomron, Carmel, Chorev, Tzarfat, Etzyon-Gever — never the anglicized Solomon, Elijah, Ahab, Jezebel. Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** inside every First-Kings-moment song the messianic aches — a house for the Name that the heavens cannot contain, a wisdom that discerns the true mother, a jar that will not empty and breath returning to a dead child, a fire from heaven and a voice made of silence, a vineyard bought with innocent blood — are carried as mystery and longing, never named as fulfilled. David is named because the text names him ("for David's sake," 11:13, 15:4); the far end of his line and the Son promised through it are held as an ache the witnesses cannot yet see. The framing names and adores Yeshua; the songs, standing inside the days of the kings, do not.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter — the Bet keyed to the house rising in silence, the Mem to the bronze sea, the Hey to the breath returning to the boy, the Shin to the Carmel fire, the Lamed to the ox-goad by the burned plow, the Letters as the keepers of Shlomo's proverbs and songs. Shlomo, David, Eliyahu, Achav, and Izevel are *witnessed*, never made the singer; Shlomo's dedication prayer and Eliyahu's cries are testified to from heaven's side, never sung as his. The cedars, the bronze sea, the cloud, the ravens, the jar, the little cloud like a hand, and the arrow drawn at a venture are invoked as images, never voiced. First Kings' own celestial witnesses carry the weight: the cherubim over the ark (6–8), heaven at Carmel (18), the messenger under the broom tree (19), and the host of heaven at the throne (22).
- **Picture Letters, never a count:** the Letters are named as personae where used; no total is ever stated and no gematria mechanism is sung. The text's own numbers are content, not method — the three thousand proverbs and thousand and five songs, the seven years and thirteen years of building, the twelve oxen under the bronze sea, the six hundred sixty-six talents of gold, the seven hundred wives and three hundred concubines, the ten pieces of the torn cloak, the four hundred fifty prophets of Baal, the seven looks toward the sea, the seven thousand who never bowed, the thirty-two kings of Aram.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
