# 2 SAMUEL — THE FIVE ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book of the Throne and the Sword

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, and `pitches/Ruth - The Album.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Every song below is one narrative unit; every unit boundary sits on the text's own seams — the regnal formulas ("and David reigned…"), the battle and itinerary markers, the prophetic speech openings ("and Natan said unto David"), the laments and songs that stand apart as their own genre, and the two *toledot*-like appendices (the mighty-men roll, the last words) that close the book in place of the Genesis genealogies.
**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator, Yahuah — and, from *within* each moment of the story, toward the Son of the covenant promised but not yet unveiled. **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own hour of the story, with no later hindsight — Yeshua is named and adored in the platform's framing, but inside a 2 Samuel-moment song the covenant with the house of David ("your house and your throne established forever"), the seed whose kingdom endures, and the threshing floor where the sword is stayed are carried as mystery and longing, never named as accomplished fulfillment. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or the personified Letters — never a human character (not David, not Natan, not Batsheva, not Avshalom, not Yoav) or anything derived from one; David's lament over Sha'ul and Yonatan, his cry "O Avshalom my son," and his song of deliverance are *witnessed and testified to* by the Angels, never sung as David. Impersonal creation the text supplies (the ark on the new cart, the pool at Giv'on, the sword that never departs the house, the great oak, the covered well, the threshing floor) may be *invoked as image*, never made the singer. We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *Five albums. Thirty-nine songs. No honest zeros — every narrative unit of 2 Samuel is sung. Every chapter accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its stories hold. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — HE MOURNED EVERY MAN HE ROSE ABOVE
### The Lament and the Rise to the Throne · 2 Samuel 1–5 · ten songs

Sha'ul is dead on Gilboa, and the throne comes to David the way it will always come to him — over graves he refuses to celebrate. He executes the man who claims the anointed's blood, laments the king who hunted him and the friend who loved him, sees the house of Sha'ul bleed out through betrayal and murder, and weeps at every bier. The witnesses sing the whole slow ascent: the crown that arrives with a confession, the field of sword-edges at the pool of Giv'on, the kingmaker undone by a quarrel, the shepherd made shepherd of a nation, the stronghold that mocked him taken, and the sound of an unseen army marching in the treetops.

*Why this title:* across these five chapters every step up the throne is a death David will not rejoice over — he kills the Amalekite who brings the crown, laments Sha'ul and Yonatan, hangs the murderers of Ish-Boshet, and mourns at Avner's grave; the crown reaches him only over the fallen, and he grieves each one.

# ALBUM II — A THRONE ESTABLISHED FOREVER
### The Ark, the Covenant, the Height of the Reign · 2 Samuel 6–10 · six songs

The reign reaches its summit, and heaven speaks the promise the whole book will ache toward: not a house of cedar for Yahuah, but a house of sons for David, a throne "established forever." Around that keystone the album gathers the height of everything — the ark brought home in terror and in dancing, the nations subdued on every side, a crippled enemy seated as a son, and an insult that kindles a war. The witnesses sing the hand that reached to steady holiness and fell, the king who stripped his glory before the ark while his queen despised him, and the word in the night that turned a gift completely around.

*Why this title:* chapter 7 is the album's and the book's summit — David longs to build Yahuah a house, and Yahuah turns it: "I will make you a house… your throne shall be established forever" (7:11,16); the ark, the victories, and the kindness to Sha'ul's heir all rest under that one promise, carried as longing the witnesses cannot yet see filled.

# ALBUM III — THE SWORD WOULD NOT LEAVE THE HOUSE
### The Sin and Its Harvest · 2 Samuel 11–14 · seven songs

At the height of everything, a king who should have marched stays home, and the sword he calls down on a soldier's life turns and lodges in his own house forever. The album is the sin and its long, grave harvest — carried with restraint, no salaciousness, only the weight. A prophet's parable turns the king's own verdict against him; the child dies and a beloved son is born; a crown of gold is set on a guilty head; and the decreed sword begins to move through the sons — a daughter's ruin, a brother murdered at a feast, an exile brought home to a door that will not open.

*Why this title:* Natan's sentence is the album's spine — "the sword shall never depart from your house" (12:10); the sin with Batsheva is forgiven, but its harvest runs on through the house in blood, and every song after the parable is that sword still moving.

# ALBUM IV — HIS HOUSE ROSE AGAINST HIM
### Avshalom's Revolt and the Wars · 2 Samuel 15–20 · ten songs

The sword decreed for the house now turns the house itself against the king. His own son steals Israel's heart at the gate and seizes the throne; his own city expels him; his own counselor betrays him and then, betrayed, hangs himself. The anointed king walks out barefoot and weeping to spare the city he could defend, sends the ark back rather than clutch it, and takes a curse as though from Yahuah's hand. The witnesses sing the whole revolt: the counsel that was right and ruined, the head caught fast in the oak, the father's cry up the stairs over the gate, the general's brutal truth, a fractured homecoming, and a wise woman on a wall who buys her city with one man's head.

*Why this title:* the harvest reaches the crown — the house Yahuah promised to build is now the house in revolt; his son takes the throne, his city casts him out, his counselor turns; the sword of chapter 12 has walked all the way up to the throne itself.

# ALBUM V — THE SWORD STOPPED AT THE THRESHING FLOOR
### The Appendix — Famine, Songs, and the Floor · 2 Samuel 21–24 · six songs

The story is over; what remains is what still had to be sung. The book's appendix gathers the leftover reckonings — a broken oath avenged in a three-year famine, the last of the giants, two songs of deliverance, the roll of the mighty men, and a plague halted — and all of it bends toward one place: the threshing floor of Aravnah, where the destroying Angel with the drawn sword over Yerushalayim is told "it is enough," sheathes it, and an altar rises on the ground that the witnesses cannot yet name. The Angels sing a mother's vigil on a rock, an aging king nearly killed by a giant, the cherub the Deliverer rode, an everlasting covenant clung to at the last, water too costly to drink, and the sword stayed at the future mountain.

*Why this title:* the appendix is a §3.3 gathering — five loose reckonings bound not by chronology but by a single destination; the census plague culminates at Aravnah's threshing floor (24:16–25), where the Angel's hand is stayed and David builds the altar on the ground that will hold the house he was never permitted to build.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **She Watched the Bones Till the Rains** | 2 Samuel 21:1–14 | The Angels, who kept watch beside a mother's watch | A three-year famine traced to Sha'ul's broken faith with the Giv'onites; seven of his house given up and hung on the hill in the first days of barley harvest; and Ritzpah on sackcloth upon the rock, driving off the birds by day and the beasts by night from harvest until the rain fell from heaven — until the king gathered the bones and the land was answered |
| 2 | **Lest the Lamp of Israel Go Out** | 2 Samuel 21:15–22 | The Angels | The king grown faint in battle, a giant with a new sword closing in, a brother's rescue; and the oath his men swear — "you shall go out no more with us, lest you quench the lamp of Israel" (21:17); and four more of the giant's sons falling in Gath, the six-fingered and the rest, the last of a race that once terrified a nation |
| 3 | **He Rode Upon a Cherub and Flew** | 2 Samuel 22 | The Angels — one of whom bore Him — testifying to the king's song, never singing it as David | The song of deliverance lifted from the far side of every danger: Yahuah his rock and fortress, who bowed the heavens and came down, thick darkness beneath His feet, "riding upon a cherub, flying upon the wings of the wind" (22:11), thundering from heaven to draw His servant out of many waters |
| 4 | **An Everlasting Covenant, Ordered and Sure** | 2 Samuel 23:1–7 | The Angels, who heard the Ruach of Yahuah speak the king's last words | The last words of the anointed, the Ruach of Yahuah on his tongue — a just ruler like the light of a cloudless morning after rain; "although my house be not so with Elohim, yet He has made with me an everlasting covenant, ordered in all things and sure, all my salvation and all my desire" (23:5) — the promise he dies still reaching toward |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* Ritzpah's vigil opens the appendix in grief and atonement — the Veil; the last giants and the two songs of deliverance are the middle, the reign remembered and praised; the roll of the mighty men gathers the loyal — and buries a quiet blade in its final name; and the census, the plague, and the stayed sword at the threshing floor land not only the album but the whole book — the sword that would not leave the house finally sheathed, and an altar smoking on the mount where the promised house will one day stand, an ache the witnesses hold and cannot yet see filled.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 21:1–14 the Giv'onites and Ritzpah · 21:15–22 the last giants · 22 the song of deliverance · 23:1–7 the last words · 23:8–39 the mighty men · 24 the census, the plague, the threshing floor. Six for six.

**Thirty-nine songs. Five albums. No honest zeros — every narrative unit of 2 Samuel is sung. One song per narrative unit, without exception; the count follows the stories, not a ceiling. Every verse of all twenty-four chapters is accounted for — the chapter splits fall on the text's own seams (a poem beginning, a regnal or prophetic hinge, a genre change), and no unit is split into two songs or padded to fill a number.**

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine); Yahuah Tzeva'ot and the Ruach of Yahuah used as the text names Him (5:10; 23:2). The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** inside every 2 Samuel-moment song the covenant ache — "your house and your throne established forever," the seed whose kingdom endures, the everlasting covenant "ordered and sure," and the threshing floor that will hold the house David could not build — is carried as mystery and longing, never named as fulfilled. Shelomoh is named because the text names him (12:24–25), and the far end of the covenant is held as a longing the witnesses cannot yet see. The framing names and adores Yeshua; the songs, standing inside their own hour, do not. No Yeshua/cross/Messiah language appears inside any song.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter. David's lament over Sha'ul and Yonatan, his cry "O Avshalom my son," and his song of deliverance are *witnessed and testified to* by the Angels, never sung as David; Natan, Batsheva, Avshalom, Yoav, Avner, Ritzpah, and the rest are witnessed, never made the singer. The ark on the new cart, Nachon's and Aravnah's threshing floors, the pool of Giv'on, the great oak, the covered well, the gold crown, the rock of Ritzpah's vigil, and the well of Beit-Lechem are invoked as images, never voiced. The destroying Angel with the drawn sword over Yerushalayim (24:16) is a natural angelic witness, sung from within his own moment.
- **Picture Letters, never a count:** the Letters are named as personae where used — the Yod keyed to the hand that reached for the ark (*yad*), the Bet keyed to the house of the covenant (*bayit*), the Gimel (the foot pictograph) keyed to Mefivoshet's lame feet at the table, the Resh (the head) keyed to Avshalom's head caught in the oak (*rosh*). No total is ever stated and no gematria mechanism is sung. The text's own numbers are content, not method — the seven sons of Sha'ul hanged, the three days' pestilence, the seventy thousand fallen, the four giants of Gath, the thirty and the three mighty men, the talent of gold in the crown.
- **Craft:** witness perspective throughout; paradox prized where the text makes it true (the reward-seeker condemned by his own mouth, the kingmaker undone by a quarrel, the dead dog seated as a son, the king who dances stripped while his queen despises him, "you are the man," a crown on a guilty head, the throne promised forever fled barefoot, sound counsel ruined by heaven, water too costly to drink, the sword stayed at the future mountain). No two titles in any album share a formula or cadence; all titles are two to seven words and recognizable from title plus passage.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
