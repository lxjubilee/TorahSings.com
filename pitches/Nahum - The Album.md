**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Nachum is three chapters and one sustained burden against one city, so it is one album. Every song below is one narrative unit; every unit boundary sits on the text's own seams — the superscription and the acrostic theophany-hymn, the oracle that will not spare a second time, the good-tidings feet on the mountains, the assault and fall of Nineveh, and the woe over the bloody city. This book is the deliberate sequel to Jonah (`pitches/Jonah - The Album.md`): the same Nineveh, spared in the prophet's day, given the burden a hundred years later.
**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. The opening theophany-hymn (1:2–8) is a **partial alphabetic acrostic** — the Letters' own home ground, exactly as the Lamentations dirge and Psalm 119 were — so the Picture Letters take up the hymn in the order of the alphabet, climbing from the Aleph and breaking off partway, singing the jealous Avenger who is also "slow to anger… good, a strong hold in the day of trouble." From there the Angels testify to the oracles. **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own hour, with no later hindsight — "the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that publisheth peace" (1:15) is carried as aching hope within its own moment, never resolved forward; no Yeshua, no cross, no Messiah inside a Nachum-moment song, though Yeshua is named and adored in the platform's framing. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or the personified Letters — never a human (not Nachum). Yahuah's first-person oracles — "Behold, I am against thee, saith Yahuah Tzeva'ot" — are His words, sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said, never voiced as a human. Impersonal creation and imagery the text supplies (the whirlwind and storm His way, the clouds the dust of His feet, the sea dried, Bashan and Karmel languishing, the raging chariots with torches like lightning, the river-gates opened, the lions' den emptied, the feet of the messenger on the mountains) may be *invoked as image*, never made the singer. We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *One album. Five songs. No honest zeros — every narrative unit of Nachum is sung. All three chapters accounted for.*

# ALBUM I — THE BURDEN OF THE CITY ONCE SPARED
### The Doom of Nineveh, the city mercy once spared · Nahum 1–3 · five songs

A century after eight words turned Nineveh from its doom, the burden falls on the same city — and heaven takes up the alphabet to sing it. The Picture Letters climb the theophany-hymn from the Aleph, praising the jealous Avenger who is also slow to anger and great in power, a stronghold to them that trust and an overrunning flood to make an utter end of His enemies; then the Angels testify to the oracle that will not spare a second time, the good-tidings feet upon the mountains, the chariots raging in the streets like torches and lightning, the river-gates thrown open, the lions' den stripped empty, and the woe over the bloody city that none will bemoan. The witnesses sing the whole burden from heaven's side — grave, restrained over the sack of the city, astonished that the mercy of Jonah's day was spurned across a hundred years.

*Why this title:* the book is a *massa* — a burden, an oracle of doom laid on Nineveh (1:1) — and its astonishment is that this is the very city spared in Jonah's day, shown mercy and now, a century later, given the burden. The title holds both at once: the burden, and the city once spared.

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the book's own order is already the album's shape. The acrostic hymn opens it in awe — the Veil, the Letters climbing the alphabet to praise the Avenger who is refuge and flood; the oracle that will not spare a second time is the Turn, the century-old mercy of Jonah's day closed off; the good-tidings feet on the mountains are the Selah — a still, aching hope of peace announced within its own hour, the album's tender center before the sack; the assault and the emptied den are the Declaration, Nineveh-the-lion undone; and the woe over the bloody city lands the album exactly where the book lands — on a fall the world claps over and none will bemoan, no comfort added the text does not give. The acrostic itself breaks off partway through the alphabet in the text, so the Letters carry song 1 and then yield to the Angels' testimony from song 2 on — the running order mirrors the text's own move from alphabet-hymn to oracle. No reordering improves on it.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1:1–8 the burden and the acrostic theophany-hymn · 1:9–14 the oracle, the wicked counsellor, and the broken yoke · 1:15 the good-tidings feet upon the mountains · 2 the assault, the opened river-gates, and the emptied lions' den · 3 the woe over the bloody city, No-Amon recalled, and the fall none will bemoan. Five for five.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine); **Yahuah Tzeva'ot** used where the text names Him "of hosts" (2:13; 3:5). The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy — always Elohim or Yahuah. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Nachum, Nineveh, Bashan, Karmel, Levanon, Yehudah, No-Amon, Kush, Mitzrayim, and Asshur are kept in their Hebraic forms. Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** "the feet of him that bringeth good tidings, that publisheth peace" (1:15) is carried within its own hour as aching hope — peace announced over a doomed empire, the wicked "utterly cut off" — and never resolved forward to any later fulfillment. No New-Testament hindsight enters any song; no Yeshua, no cross, no Messiah inside a Nachum-moment song. The book's astonishment — that the mercy Nineveh received in Jonah's day was spurned across a hundred years and now the burden falls on the same city — is sung as the wonder and dread it is; the album nods to Jonah (`pitches/Jonah - The Album.md`) as its deliberate century-later sequel.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter — the Picture Letters carrying the acrostic hymn in the order of the alphabet, and the Gimel keyed to the foot and the messenger's feet upon the mountains (1:15). Nachum is *witnessed*, never voiced. Yahuah's first-person oracles — "I will make thy grave; for thou art vile" (1:14), "Behold, I am against thee, saith Yahuah Tzeva'ot" (2:13; 3:5) — are sung in awe by the witnesses who testify to what He said, never voiced as a human. The whirlwind and storm, the clouds the dust of His feet, the dried sea, the languishing Bashan and Karmel, the raging torch-lit chariots, the opened river-gates, the emptied lions' den, and the feet on the mountains are invoked as images, never voiced.
- **Picture Letters, the acrostic is the point — never a count:** the Letters take up the opening theophany-hymn in their own order, because 1:2–8 is a partial alphabetic acrostic (climbing from the Aleph and breaking off partway through the alphabet); the structure is described, but no total number of letters is ever stated and no gematria mechanism is sung. The text's own numbers are content, not method — the "second time" that will not come, the century since Jonah, the firstripe figs.
- **Grave restraint:** the sack of Nineveh — the slain of chapter 3, the siege and the sword — is witnessed from heaven's side, family-friendly and undetailed; the weight comes from stillness, the Letters' awe, and the Angels' testimony, never from gore.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5) — a dark, cinematic burden-and-theophany register is the house sound here, the acrostic hymn rising and the woe-oracle falling.
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
