# EXODUS — THE TEN ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book of the Going-Out

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Every song below is one narrative unit; every unit boundary sits on the text's own seams — the itinerary formulas ("and they journeyed…"), the speech openings ("and Yahuah spoke unto Mosheh"), the regnal and ritual markers Exodus supplies in place of the Genesis *toledot*.
**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator, Yahuah — and, from *within* each moment of the story, toward the Son promised but not yet unveiled. **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own place in Scripture, with no New-Testament hindsight — Yeshua is named and adored in the platform's framing, but inside an Exodus-moment song the lamb, the blood on the wood, the struck rock that pours out water are carried as mystery and ache, never named as accomplished fulfillment. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or the personified Letters — never a human character (not Mosheh, not Miryam, not Pharaoh) or anything derived from one; impersonal creation the text supplies (a river, a basket, a tree, a rock, a cloud) may be *invoked as image*, never made the singer. We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *Ten albums. Seventy songs. No honest zeros — every narrative unit of Exodus is sung. Every chapter accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its stories hold. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — THE WATER DISOBEYED THE KING
### Bondage and the Basket · Exodus 1–2 · six songs

A new king forgets a savior's name and turns a rescued family into a labor camp, and every instrument he reaches for to hold them down does the opposite. The whip multiplies them. The midwives sworn to kill for him fear a higher throne and let the boys live. The river commanded to drown the sons floats the one who will unmake him. The witnesses sing the four centuries the ground crew of heaven never left — and the four verbs that close the album: He heard, He remembered, He saw, He knew.

*Why this title:* in these two chapters the Nile, the midwives, and the daughter of Pharaoh himself all refuse the death-decree — the empire's own tools turn in its hand.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Every Blow Made Two of Them** | Exodus 1:1–14 | The Angels, watching the arithmetic of oppression fail | "The more they afflicted them, the more they multiplied" (1:12) — slavery breeding the very nation it was built to shrink |
| 2 | **They Feared Elohim More Than the King** | Exodus 1:15–22 | The Angels, who watched two women out-fear an empire | Shiphrah and Puah let the boys live and lie to Pharaoh's face — the first recorded defiance of a throne, "because they feared Elohim," and He built them houses |
| 3 | **The Ark Came Back as a Basket** | Exodus 2:1–10 | The Letters, who know the word *tevah* | The second *tevah* in Scripture — Noach's own word for the ark reused for a reed basket, salvation floated on the very water decreed to drown; the drawn-out one drawn out |
| 4 | **The Sand Gave the Secret Back** | Exodus 2:11–15 | The Angels | He looks this way and that, strikes, and hides the Egyptian in the sand — and by the next day the sand has given it up; the deliverer flees before he delivers |
| 5 | **He Rescued a Well Before a People** | Exodus 2:16–22 | The Angels | The man who will one day part a sea first stands up for seven shepherdesses at a Midian well — a stranger who names his son *stranger* |
| 6 | **He Was Never Not Listening** | Exodus 2:23–25 | The Angels, who stood the whole four hundred years | The king dies, the groaning rises, and the text lays down four verbs at once — Elohim heard, remembered, saw, and knew — heaven was awake the entire time |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the bondage opens and sets the stake; the midwives are the first crack of defiance; the basket carries the hope; the buried blow and the flight darken it; the Midian well is the quiet middle where the deliverer is hidden and formed; and the four verbs of remembering land the album — the hinge on which the whole book turns.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1:1–14 the bondage · 1:15–22 the midwives · 2:1–10 the basket · 2:11–15 the blow and the flight · 2:16–22 Midian and Tsipporah · 2:23–25 the remembering. Six for six.

# ALBUM II — I WILL BE WHAT I WILL BE
### The Bush, the Name, and the Reluctant Mouth · Exodus 3–6 · seven songs

A shepherd turns aside for a fire that will not finish the bush it burns, and comes away carrying a Name that is a verb — Ehyeh Asher Ehyeh — a Name that refuses the past tense and will not be pinned. What follows is the least heroic call in Scripture: a staff he flees from, a mouth he calls heavy, a night on the road when heaven nearly kills him, a first audience that only makes the bricks harder. Pharaoh asks the question the whole book will answer — *who is Yahuah?* — and heaven answers with the Name and seven promises in the future tense.

*Why this title:* the Name given at the bush (3:14) is the album's spine and the answer to Pharaoh's sneer (5:2) — a Name whose bearer is pure, unfinished being.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **The Fire Took Nothing From the Bush** | Exodus 3:1–22 | The Hey and the Aleph — the breath and the ox, the letters of *Ehyeh* — the Angels drawing back from holy ground | A flame that burns without spending, and a Voice that answers "who shall I say sent me?" with a verb — I Will Be What I Will Be; "I have surely seen the affliction of my people" |
| 2 | **The Staff Became What He Feared** | Exodus 4:1–17 | The Lamed — the shepherd's staff itself — with the Pey answering for the heavy tongue | Three signs for a man whose only argument is his mouth; the rod that turns serpent and must be picked up by the tail, the hand that goes leprous and clean |
| 3 | **A Bridegroom Made of Blood** | Exodus 4:18–26 | The Angels, one of whom the text says sought to kill him | On the road back, a night heaven will not explain — a flint knife, a touched foot, "a bridegroom of blood you are to me"; the mystery kept a mystery |
| 4 | **The People Bowed Before the Plagues** | Exodus 4:27–31 | The Angels, who know how soon the bowing will break | Aharon meets Mosheh at the mountain of Elohim, the elders hear, the people believe and bow — the last easy faith before the straw is taken away |
| 5 | **Who Is Yahuah, Said the Throne** | Exodus 5:1–21 | The Angels, who could answer and are told to wait | Pharaoh asks the question the ten strokes will answer — and answers it himself by doubling the labor; bricks with no straw, the foremen beaten for another's defiance |
| 6 | **Seven Times He Said 'I Will'** | Exodus 5:22–6:13 | The Zayin — value seven — counting the seven verbs of redemption; the Hey of the Name | To a man crying "why have you done evil to this people," He answers not with a reason but with a Name and seven futures — bring out, rid, redeem, take, be your Elohim, bring in, give — and the people cannot listen for anguish of spirit |
| 7 | **The Names Before the Breastplate** | Exodus 6:14–27 | The Letters, the ledger-keepers who never drop a name | The story halts to name the sons of Levi down to Aharon — the line that will one day wear the twelve tribes on its heart before Yahuah, inked centuries before the stones are cut |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the bush and the Name open in hush; the signs and the road-night carry the reluctance and the dread; the people's brief belief is the false dawn; Pharaoh's question turns it dark; the Name answered by seven promises is the Declaration; and the Levite ledger lands the album, planting a seam that will not pay off until Album VIII. (6:10–13, the brief re-sending, is absorbed into song 6; 6:28–7:7, the recommission, opens Album III.)

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 3:1–22 the bush and the Name · 4:1–17 the signs · 4:18–26 the bridegroom of blood · 4:27–31 the elders bow · 5:1–21 bricks without straw · 5:22–6:13 the Name and the seven "I will" · 6:14–27 the line of Levi. Seven for seven.

# ALBUM III — THEIR GODS FELL ONE BY ONE
### The Nine Strokes and the Rod · Exodus 7–10 · ten songs

Egypt kept a god for the river, a god for the frogs, a god for the cattle, a god for the sun — and heaven took them down one at a time, in order, until a nation that asked "who is Yahuah?" learned His Name by losing everything it had trusted. These are the ten strokes before the last: a rod that eats rods, a Nile wearing its own blood, a finger Egypt's magicians could not forge, a line drawn through the land, a darkness with weight. The witnesses keep the count — and mark the strangest turn in it, the moment the plague and the hardening both begin to choose themselves.

*Why this album exists:* per the methodology (§3.3), a coherent set larger than seven carries all its songs rather than leave a stroke unsung — the plagues are their own escalating musical, exactly as the genealogies were on Genesis III.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **One Rod Swallowed the Others** | Exodus 7:1–13 | The Lamed — the staff — with the Angels | Mosheh made "a god to Pharaoh," and the opening sign is a rod turned serpent that eats the serpents of Egypt whole — the contest decided before the plagues begin |
| 2 | **The River Wore Its Own Blood** | Exodus 7:14–25 | The Mem — the water | The Nile they worshiped and the river that drowned the sons runs red seven days; the fish die, the land stinks, and the magicians copy the wound instead of curing it |
| 3 | **They Begged for One Dry Night** | Exodus 8:1–15 | The Angels | Frogs in the ovens and the kneading bowls and the king's own bed — and offered relief, Pharaoh says *tomorrow*, choosing one more night of them; the first glimpse of a heart hardening itself |
| 4 | **The Finger They Could Not Forge** | Exodus 8:16–19 | The Yod — the hand, the finger | Dust becomes gnats, and the magicians who matched blood and frogs cannot make lice — "this is the finger of Elohim," they say, and the king will not hear it |
| 5 | **He Drew a Line Through Egypt** | Exodus 8:20–32 | The Angels | The swarms fill Egypt and stop at Goshen's edge — the first stroke that names the difference, a border the flies will not cross, drawn between the two peoples |
| 6 | **Not One of Theirs Had Died** | Exodus 9:1–7 | The Angels, keeping the count of the herds | The murrain takes Egypt's cattle and spares Israel's to the last animal; Pharaoh sends, confirms the miracle, and hardens anyway |
| 7 | **The Magicians Could Not Stand** | Exodus 9:8–12 | The Angels | Soot flung at the sky becomes boils on man and beast, and the wise men of Egypt cannot even stand in the room — the last time they appear |
| 8 | **Fire Walked Inside the Ice** | Exodus 9:13–35 | The Shin and the Mem — fire and water married in one stroke — with the Angels | Hail and fire together, the worst storm since Egypt was a nation; and for the first time some Egyptians fear the word of Yahuah and bring their servants and cattle indoors |
| 9 | **They Ate What the Hail Had Left** | Exodus 10:1–20 | The Angels | An east wind all night, and by morning locusts cover the land dark and eat every green thing the hail had missed — and Pharaoh's own servants ask, "how long shall this man be a snare to us?" |
| 10 | **A Darkness With Weight** | Exodus 10:21–29 | The Ayin — the eye gone blind — with the Angels | Three days of a darkness thick enough to touch, no one rising from his place — yet Israel had light in their dwellings; and Pharaoh says, "see my face no more" |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the rod-sign credentials the messengers; the nine strokes run in the text's own order, escalating from nuisance to ruin to a darkness that pre-echoes the death to come; the Goshen line at the center is the quiet hinge — the first time the plagues distinguish the peoples — and the felt darkness lands the album on the threshold of midnight. The tenth stroke is withheld; it opens the next record, where it belongs — with the blood on the doors.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 7:1–13 the rod · 7:14–25 blood · 8:1–15 frogs · 8:16–19 gnats · 8:20–32 flies · 9:1–7 livestock · 9:8–12 boils · 9:13–35 hail · 10:1–20 locusts · 10:21–29 darkness. Ten for ten. *(6:28–7:7, the recommission, is absorbed into song 1.)*

# ALBUM IV — DEATH HAD TO READ THE DOORS
### Passover and the Going-Out · Exodus 11–13 · seven songs

The last stroke is announced at midnight and made to obey a sign: blood struck on wood tells the destroyer which houses to pass. A lamb without blemish dies so a firstborn need not; a nation is born in a single night, four hundred thirty years old; and heaven itself keeps the vigil — a night of watching that Israel keeps to this day. The witnesses sing the doors, the great cry, the plunder, and the pillar that goes before them — and the bones of Yoseph, carried up at last, an oath from the end of Genesis finally paid.

*Why this title:* the destroyer must read each lintel — the One who strikes is also the One who marks the door He will guard; death itself is put under a sign.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **One More Blow, at Midnight** | Exodus 11:1–10 | The Angels, who know the hour | The last stroke foretold before it falls — every firstborn from the throne to the mill, a cry such as never was; and Israel told to ask their neighbors for silver and gold |
| 2 | **The Mark That Turned Death Aside** | Exodus 12:1–28 | The Tav — in Paleo-Hebrew a mark, two crossed sticks — with the Dalet, the door | A lamb without blemish, its blood struck on the two posts and the lintel with hyssop; a sign on wood that tells the destroyer where not to enter — the ache of a life given so a firstborn need not die |
| 3 | **Not a House Without a Dead** | Exodus 12:29–36 | The Angels — the destroyer's own restraint, passing the marked doors | At midnight the firstborn of Egypt die from the throne to the dungeon; the great cry rises; and the empire that would not let them go now drives them out and loads them with silver |
| 4 | **The Night He Sat Up to Keep** | Exodus 12:37–42 | The Angels, who kept the watch | Six hundred thousand on foot out of Rameses, dough unleavened on their shoulders, four hundred thirty years ending in one night — a night of watching unto Yahuah, kept by Him, and kept still |

| 7 | **He Went Before Them, Cloud and Fire** | Exodus 13:17–22 | The Angels in the pillar; the Shin, the fire by night | Not the near road of the Philistines but the long way, lest they see war and turn back; a pillar that never departs — and Yoseph's bones carried up, the oath from the close of Genesis kept |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the announcement opens on the threshold of midnight; the lamb and the mark are the still, holy center; the death-cry and the going-out break it open; the watch-night is the Selah; the stranger's inclusion and the firstborn's consecration carry the law forward; and the pillar with Yoseph's bones lands the album, turning the camp toward the sea.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 11:1–10 the tenth foretold · 12:1–28 the lamb and the blood · 12:29–36 midnight · 12:37–42 the going-out and the watch-night · 12:43–51 one law for the stranger · 13:1–16 the firstborn consecrated · 13:17–22 the pillar and the bones. Seven for seven.

# ALBUM V — ONE WATER, A WALL AND A GRAVE
### The Sea · Exodus 14–15:21 · six songs

Heaven baits the trap with the people themselves — camp by the sea so Pharaoh thinks you lost — and the chariots take it. Then the hardest command in the book: stand still, say nothing, the fight is not yours. All night one cloud is darkness to the one camp and light to the other, and something stands in the gap that is not made of men. The same water rises as a wall for Israel and closes as a grave for Egypt, and on the far shore the oldest poem in Scripture opens not "we sang" but "I will sing" — a victory hymn that admits it is not over.

*Why this title:* chapter 14's whole paradox in five words — one sea, a wall on the right and the left for the redeemed, a grave for the army behind them.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **He Lured the Chariots to the Shore** | Exodus 14:1–9 | The Angels, who understood the strategy | Israel told to turn back and camp by the sea so Pharaoh will think them trapped — the bait is the people, and six hundred chosen chariots take it |
| 2 | **Hold Your Peace and Watch** | Exodus 14:10–14 | The Angels | Caught between the chariots and the water, the people cry out and long for Egypt's graves — and the answer is the hardest command in the book: stand still, say nothing, Yahuah will fight for you |
| 3 | **We Stood the Watch Between the Camps** | Exodus 14:15–20 | The Angels — the messenger of Elohim who removed from the front and went behind | The pillar crosses to the rear, and one cloud is darkness to Egypt and light to Israel; all night the two camps do not come near — something stood in the gap, and it was not made of men |
| 4 | **The Water Stood Up on Both Sides** | Exodus 14:21–31 | The Chet — the letter of the wall — holding the deep upright; the Angels | An east wind all night, the sea a wall on the right hand and the left, a nation walking through on dry ground — and at dawn the same walls fall, and Egypt lies dead on the shore |
| 5 | **I Will Sing — the Word Would Not End** | Exodus 15:1–18 | The Aleph — the ox, the first letter of *Ashirah* — sounding the tonic, leading the song from the far shore | The oldest poem in Scripture opens forward, not back — *ashirah*, I will sing, a hymn in the unfinished tense; the horse and the rider thrown into the sea, the right hand shattering the enemy |
| 6 | **Miryam Answered With a Drum** | Exodus 15:19–21 | The Angels, joining the antiphon from above | The prophetess takes a timbrel and all the women after her, and she answers the men's song line for line — the response that makes the Song of the Sea a duet |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the trap and the terror open under threat; the watch between the camps is the hush before the miracle; the walls and the drowning are the Unveiling and the Turn in one stroke; the Song of the Sea is the Declaration; and Miryam's timbrel is the Benediction — praise answering praise as the album resolves into a drumbeat.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 14:1–9 the trap set · 14:10–14 stand still · 14:15–20 the watch between the camps · 14:21–31 the walls and the drowning · 15:1–18 the Song of the Sea · 15:19–21 Miryam and the women. Six for six. *(15:1–18, the Song of the Sea, is one poetic unit and one song; 15:22 begins the wilderness on Album VI.)*

# ALBUM VI — THE DESERT SET A TABLE
### The Wilderness Provisions · Exodus 15:22–18 · six songs

The wilderness that should have killed them fed them instead — from sources that make no sense: a tree cast into bitter water turns it sweet, bread falls where nothing grows, a struck stone pours out a river. The witnesses sing the provision and the testing that came with it — a people learning, one impossible morning at a time, to trust the One who leads them by the day's ration. And at the edge of the mountain, an outsider — a Midianite priest — is the one who says "now I know," and the one who teaches the mediator not to carry the whole weight alone.

*Why this title:* Marah, the manna, and the rock are one motif — a table laid in a place that grows nothing, provision drawn from what has none to give.

**Tracklist (play order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **A Tree Made the Bitter Sweet** | Exodus 15:22–27 | The Angels (the tree invoked as image — the ache of wood that heals, carried, not named) | Three days without water, then water too bitter to drink; a tree shown to Mosheh and thrown in, and the bitterness turns sweet — and twelve springs wait at Elim, one for each tribe |
| 2 | **Bread Fell Where Nothing Grows** | Exodus 16 | The Angels — who eat, the psalm says, the bread of the mighty ones | A fine flake on the desert floor each morning, enough for the day and never for tomorrow, doubled before the Sabbath and rotten if hoarded — heaven teaching a freed people to trust the next sunrise; *man hu* — what is it? |
| 3 | **He Struck the Stone and It Wept** | Exodus 17:1–7 | The Lamed — the staff — with the rock invoked as image (the ache carried, not named) | No water at Rephidim, the people ready to stone him, and Yahuah standing on the rock before Mosheh — strike it, and a desert stone pours a river; the place named Massah and Merivah, testing and quarrel |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the three provisions run in the text's order — bitter water sweetened, bread from the sky, water from the rock — each a testing as much as a gift; Amalek is the one battle, won by lifted hands; and Yitro closes the album in two movements, the outsider's confession and the outsider's counsel, the last quiet breath before the mountain.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 15:22–27 Marah and Elim · 16 the manna · 17:1–7 the rock at Rephidim · 17:8–16 Amalek · 18:1–12 Yitro's confession · 18:13–27 the judges appointed. Six for six.

# ALBUM VII — THEY BEGGED THE VOICE TO STOP
### Sinai and the Covenant · Exodus 19–24 · seven songs

The mountain smokes because Yahuah came down in fire, and it trembles under Him, and a slave-people is told it will be a kingdom of priests. Then the only words in the Torah spoken aloud to a whole nation — and the people cannot bear it. They stand far off and beg for a mediator: *you speak with us, but let not Elohim speak, lest we die.* The witnesses sing the terror of the direct Voice, the law that bends down to the goring ox and the widow's cloak, the Angel with the Name inside him, the blood thrown on the people, and the seventy who climbed and saw Elohim and lived to eat.

*Why this title:* the album's devastating hinge (20:19) — humanity, offered the very thing it longed for, asking the Voice to please be quiet.

| 2 | **Ten Words Cut Into the Air** | Exodus 20:1–17 | The Yod — value ten — sounding each word; the Angels | The only words Yahuah speaks directly to a whole people and writes with His own finger — ten sentences that begin "I am Yahuah who brought you out," law framed first as rescue |
| 3 | **They Begged Him to Stop Speaking** | Exodus 20:18–21 | The Angels, watching humanity recoil from the thing it had longed for | The people see the thunder and the smoking mountain and stand far off — "you speak with us, but let not Elohim speak, lest we die" — and Mosheh walks alone into the thick darkness where He is |

# ALBUM VIII — HE ASKED TO LIVE IN A TENT
### The Pattern on the Mountain · Exodus 25–31 · seven songs

The One the whole mountain could not hold asks, from inside the cloud, for a tent — *make me a sanctuary, that I may dwell among them.* What follows is heaven's own throne-room dictated in wood and gold and thread: an ark with two cherubim beaten from the same lid, facing each other over the place the Voice will speak; a tree of light with seven mouths; a veil woven with the covering ones; twelve stones bearing the tribes' names over a heart. The Angels sing the strangest commission they have ever watched — their own likeness hammered into gold, and the Infinite measuring Himself for a curtained room in the middle of a camp.

*Why this title:* the paradox that carries from Album VII — the One no mountain could contain requesting a portable dwelling, the ultimate condescension.

# ALBUM IX — BROKEN, AND CUT AGAIN
### The Calf, the Cleft, and the Second Tablets · Exodus 32–34 · seven songs

While the covenant is being written in stone on the mountain, it is already being broken in gold at the foot of it. The tablets shatter before a single command is kept; the offer that terrifies more than the calf is "I will not go up among you"; and the answer to "show me your glory" is a cleft in the rock and a hand laid over it while the glory passes. Then the summit of the whole book: Yahuah descends and recites His own Name over the stone — merciful, gracious, slow to anger — the One betrayed defining Himself, first, as mercy, and writing the covenant a second time.

*Why this title:* the two tablets and the whole album in four words — broken at the mountain's foot, and cut again by the same hand that was wronged.

# ALBUM X — EVEN MOSES COULD NOT GO IN
### The Building and the Glory · Exodus 35–40 · seven songs

The people who gave their gold to a calf now bring it, willing-hearted, to the dwelling — and bring so much that for the only time in Scripture, generosity has to be halted. Then the pattern from the mountain stands up in the sand, board by board, exactly as shown, each act sealed with a phrase that tolls like a bell: *as Yahuah commanded Mosheh.* On the first day of the first month the tent is raised — and the glory that filled Sinai fills it so completely that Mosheh, who stood inside the cloud on the mountain, cannot enter. The book of the going-out ends with Yahuah moved in, and a whole camp waiting on a cloud.

*Why this title:* the last image in Exodus (40:35) — the mediator who begged to see the glory now locked out of the tent by the fullness of it.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine); the Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** inside every Exodus-moment song the messianic aches — the unblemished lamb, the blood on the wood, the struck rock that gives water, the veil over the shining face — are carried as mystery and longing, never named as fulfilled. The framing names Yeshua; the songs, standing inside their own hour, do not.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter. Mosheh, Miryam, Pharaoh, Yitro, and the priests are *witnessed*, never made the singer; the river, the basket, the tree, the rock, the cloud, and the veil are invoked as images, never voiced.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
