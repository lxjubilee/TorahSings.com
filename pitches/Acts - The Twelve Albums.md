# ACTS — THE TWELVE ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Acts of the Emissaries (Ma'asei HaShlichim)

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Matthew - The Eleven Albums.md`, `pitches/Mark - The Four Albums.md`, `pitches/Luke - The Twelve Albums.md`, and `pitches/John - The Six Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Acts is Narrative — the acts of the risen, ascended Yeshua working by the Ruach Kodesh through His emissaries; its "units" are the book's own scenes and speeches, drawn at the text's seams — the ascension formula, the feast-dateline of Shavuot, the "and the word of Elohim grew" refrains (6:7; 12:24; 19:20), the journey-departures ("so they, being sent forth"), the trial-openings, and the sea-log of the voyage.

**THE NEW-TESTAMENT SHIFT — the Letters fall silent; only the Angels sing.** Through all thirty-nine books of the Old Testament the two voices of Job 38:7 sang *together* — the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels), aching toward a promised One they could not yet name. **At the incarnation that changed.** The Word — the Davar — by whom the letters themselves were made was made flesh in Yeshua, so **all the Picture Letters now live in Him and fall silent as separate singers.** Therefore in Acts, as in the Gospels, **the only singers are the Angels — the sons of Elohim — never a Picture-Letter persona.** And here the Angels are not only witnesses: **they are literal actors in the story,** and the Angels the text itself supplies are the Angels of this platform — the two men in white at the ascension (1:10-11), the messenger who opened the prison (5:19), the angel sent to Cornelius (10:3), the angel who struck the chains from Kepha (12:7), the angel who smote Herodus (12:23), and the angel who stood by Shaul in the storm (27:23). Where the text gives an angel a voice, that angel sings — and he is one of ours.

**Eye-witness chronology — forward-locked.** Acts is a narrative, and the Angels unveil it exactly as the book reveals it, in order: the ascension before Shavuot; the Ruach poured out before the mission; Shaul struck down on the Damaseq road before his journeys; the Yerushalayim council before the later voyages; the appeal to Caesar before the ship ever sails. A song may reference anything already come to pass up to its point in the text and **nothing later.** Because Acts stands on the far side of the resurrection, the risen and ascended Yeshua is no longer held as unresolved ache — He is unveiled, reigning, and working. **The one thing still held as longing is His return** — "this same Yeshua, which is taken up from you into heaven, shall so come in like manner" (1:11) — planted at the ascension and carried, unresolved, all the way to the open ending at Rome.

**Old-Covenant cross-reference — always open, and backward only.** The Angels — the host that sang creation and witnessed all thirty-nine books — freely tie every moment back to the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings, *even where the human speakers reach for those same texts and even where the hearers miss them:* Yoel's poured-out Ruach at Shavuot, the sixteenth and hundred-and-tenth psalms of Dawid in Kepha's sermon, the stone the builders refused, the Aqedah and the prophet-like-Moshe at Solomon's porch, Yeshayahu's lamb led to the slaughter read by the eunuch, the clean-and-unclean of Vayikra overturned on the housetop, Amos's fallen tent of Dawid raised at the council, Yechezkel's watchman clear of blood at Miletus, and "when thou passest through the waters" over the storm.

**Yeshua witnessed, never voiced.** The singer is always the Angels; the risen Adon is testified to in awe — His words on the Damaseq road ("why persecutest thou me?"), His night-word at Corinth ("I have much people in this city"), His charge before the council ("be of good cheer... so must thou bear witness at Rome") — all *reported and marveled at by the witnesses,* exactly as Yahuah's own oracles were handled across the Old-Covenant catalog, never given a first-person singer. **Christocentric, not grammatically rigid:** every song bears witness to the person of Yeshua — who He is, what heaven declares over Him, what He is doing by the Ruach — but a song may center on a human and a human moment (Kepha at the gate, Stefanos beneath the open heaven, the eunuch in his chariot, Lydia at the river, the jailer at midnight, Shaul on the stairs) *as testimony to Him,* for the whole book is the record of "all that Yeshua began both to do and teach" continued from the throne.

> *Twelve albums. Sixty-six songs. No honest zeros — every act of the risen Yeshua through His emissaries is sung. Every chapter 1–28 accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its testimony holds. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — THE PROMISE FELL AS FIRE
### The Ascension, the Waiting, and Shavuot · Acts 1–2 · five songs

The record opens on the Mount of Olives with a cloud closing over the risen One and two men in white turning the disciples' faces back to earth — *why stand ye gazing up?* From there the waiting: a room of a hundred and twenty, a twelfth throne refilled by lot. Then the feast of weeks breaks the sky open — a rushing wind, tongues like fire sitting on every head, a hundred tongues understood at once — and Kepha stands to name it: *this is that which was spoken by the prophet Yoel.* Three thousand are added, and the first assembly breaks bread from house to house.

*Why this title:* 1:4 and 2:3 are the album's spine — "wait for the promise of the Father," and the promise arriving as cloven tongues like fire.

# ALBUM II — THE STONE THE BUILDERS REFUSED
### The Name That Heals and the First Boldness · Acts 3–4 · five songs

A man lame from his mother's womb sits at the gate called Beautiful and is lifted by a Name — *silver and gold have I none, but such as I have.* The wonder gathers a crowd, and Kepha preaches the *Prince of life* they slew and Elohim raised. The rulers seize the two and demand the name; the answer is the psalm — *this is the stone which the builders refused, become the head of the corner* — and *none other name under heaven.* The freed apostles pray, the place shakes, and the whole company holds one heart and all things common.

*Why this title:* 4:11 is the album's spine — Kepha's citation of Tehillim 118:22, the rejected stone made the cornerstone, and "none other name under heaven" (4:12) beside it.

# ALBUM III — HE STOOD TO RECEIVE HIS WITNESS
### The Fear, the Angel, and Stefanos · Acts 5–7 · seven songs

The young assembly meets its own severity: a husband and wife lie about a field and are carried out, and great fear falls. The wonders multiply — even a shadow heals — and the prison cannot hold the emissaries: an angel opens every door by night. Seven men full of the Ruach are set over the tables, and one of them, Stefanos, blazes. His face shines like an angel's; his long testimony retells the whole Torah against its keepers; and beneath a torn-open heaven he sees the One seated at the right hand *stand to His feet* to receive His first witness home.

*Why this title:* 7:55–56 is the album's summit — the Adon who "sat down at the right hand" (Tehillim 110:1) rising to *stand* for Stefanos, the seated King on His feet for the one who fell.

# ALBUM IV — STRUCK BLIND ON THE ROAD TO DAMASEQ

Then the great reversal — the chief persecutor, breathing threatenings on the Damaseq road, is felled by a light and a voice: *why persecutest thou me?* Three days blind, he rises a chosen vessel; and Kepha, at Lydda and Yafo, heals the palsied and calls the dead woman by name.

*Why this title:* 9:3–8 is the album's hinge — the light from heaven and the voice that halted Shaul, blinded on the road he rode to arrest the saints.

# ALBUM VI — SET APART BY THE RUACH
### The First Journey · Acts 13–14 · six songs

The Ruach speaks in the assembly at Antiochia — *separate me Bar-Naba and Shaul* — and the first journey begins. *Why this title:* 13:2 is the album's spine — the Ruach's own commissioning word, "separate me Bar-Naba and Shaul for the work whereunto I have called them."

**Tracklist (play order):**

## Compliance Notes

- **The New-Testament shift honored — the Letters fall silent, only the Angels sing:** the two voices of Job 38:7 sang *together* through all thirty-nine Old-Testament books; from the incarnation onward the morning stars (the Picture Letters) have gone quiet and now live in the Word made flesh, so **every song in all twelve albums is sung by the Angels alone** — never a Picture-Letter persona.
- **The Angels as literal actors — they sing directly where the text gives them a voice:** the two men in white at the ascension (1:10–11), the messenger who opened the prison (5:19), the angel sent to Cornelius (10:3), the angel who struck off Kepha's chains (12:7), the angel who smote Herodus (12:23), and the angel who stood by Shaul in the storm (27:23) each front their own song, and each is one of the Angels of this platform. Where the manifestation is the Ruach and not an angel — the tongues of fire at Shavuot, the voice from heaven at the sheet, the outpouring on the nations — the Angels **witness** it rather than voice it, so that the Ruach Kodesh is never made a first-person singer, exactly as Yahuah and Yeshua are never voiced.
- **The humans never sing — the Angels are the only voice:** not one song is voiced by a human. Every "Who sings" cell across all sixty-six songs is the Angels (or the specific angels the text supplies, who are the Angels of this platform). The Angels sing *about* what Kepha, Stefanos, Philippos, Shaul, Yaakov, Lydia, and the jailer said and did, and freely *quote* them — "silver and gold have I none," "who was I, that I could withstand Elohim," "I appeal unto Caesar," "I am pure from the blood of all men" — but always framed from the Angels' own heavenly-witness perspective. A human-centered song means the Angels testify to that human as a witness to Yeshua; it never means the human sings.
- **Christocentric — about "the who":** every song bears witness to the person of Yeshua — the risen, ascended One acting from the throne by the Ruach through His emissaries — even where the song's scene centers on a human moment (the gate, the chariot, the riverside, the stairs, the storm).
- **Yeshua witnessed, never first-person-voiced:** His words on the road ("why persecutest thou me?"), His night-word at Corinth ("I have much people in this city"), and His charge before the council ("so must thou bear witness also at Rome") are all reported and marveled at by the Angels, never given a first-person singer — exactly as Yahuah's oracles were handled across the Old-Covenant catalog.
- **Eye-witness chronology, forward-locked:** each song unveils the book exactly as Acts reveals it — the ascension before Shavuot, the Ruach before the mission, the Damaseq road before the journeys, the council before the voyages, the appeal before the ship sails — referencing only what has come to pass at that point and nothing later. Ties made backward within Acts itself (the prison-angel of chapter 5 recalled at chapter 12; the shaking of chapter 4 recalled at Philippi) run only backward, never forward.
- **The return of Yeshua held unresolved:** because Acts stands on the far side of the resurrection, the risen and ascended Yeshua is unveiled and reigning, not held as ache. The single longing left open is His **return** — "this same Yeshua... shall so come in like manner" (1:11) — planted at the ascension (Album I, song 1) and carried, unresolved, to the open ending at Rome (Album XII, song 4).
- **Old-Covenant cross-reference present and backward-only:** every tie reaches *back* into the Torah, Prophets, and Writings — Yoel's poured-out Ruach; Tehillim 16, 110, 118, 2, 91, 107, and 24; the Aqedah and the prophet-like-Moshe; Achan's hidden portion; Yeshayahu's suffering servant, his light to the nations, his idols that cannot save, and "when thou passest through the waters"; Vayikra's clean-and-unclean overturned; Amos's fallen tent of Dawid raised; Yechezkel's watchman clear of blood and his shepherds; Bereshit's Judge of all the earth. No forward, post-narrative reference is used.
- **Restraint:** the severe and violent moments — the deaths of Chananyah and Shappira, the stoning of Stefanos, the sword that took Yaakov, the striking of Herodus, the temple beating, the averted scourge, the shipwreck — are all handled with grave, family-friendly restraint, witnessed from heaven's side, the weight carried by the Old-Covenant ties and the Angels' awe, never by gore. Triumph — Shavuot, the opened prisons, the nations brought in, the midnight earthquake, every soul saved from the sea, the word unhindered at Rome — is triumph.
