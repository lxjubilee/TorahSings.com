# PSALMS — THE TWENTY-ONE ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Songbook of Israel — the Book That Is Already Music

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, and `pitches/Job - The Seven Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Psalms is not narrative; it is Israel's hymnal — one hundred fifty discrete, self-contained poems. **The unit is the individual psalm** — the strongest possible seam, each with its own beginning and end — so one song per psalm is the honest default; only a genuinely unified pair shares a song (the acrostic pair 9–10; the split psalm 42–43). **The five-book division is the backbone** — Book I (1–41), Book II (42–72), Book III (73–89), Book IV (90–106), Book V (107–150), each sealed by a doxology — and the psalms are grouped within and across those books into coherent albums by type and theme: the Songs of Ascents, the Egyptian Hallel, the great Hallelujah finale, the Asaph collection, the sons of Korach, the acrostics, the enthronement cycle, the creation and historical psalms, the penitential and royal sets.
**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator — **Yahuah**, and, in the Elohistic Psalter (42–83) where the psalms' own mouth prefers it, **Elohim** — and, from *within* each moment, toward the Anointed One promised but not yet unveiled. **The human prayer is witnessed, never voiced as the human:** the psalms of David, Asaph, the sons of Korach, Heman, Ethan, and Mosheh — every lament, penitence, trust, and thanksgiving — are carried and testified to by the Angels, who sing *about* the tears and the trust, never as the one who wept (exactly as David's laments were witnessed on `pitches/2 Samuel - The Five Albums.md`). **The cosmic, creation, enthronement, and praise psalms the Angels and the Letters sing as their own liturgy** — the heavens declaring (19), "bless Yahuah, you His angels" (103:20), "praise Him, all His angels, all His hosts" (148), the King enthroned on the cherubim (99). **The acrostics are the Letters' own home ground** — 9–10, 25, 34, 37, 111, 112, 119, 145 are built on the alef-bet, and Psalm 119 is the whole alphabet at prayer; there the Letters sing their own names and their own pictographs, the most on-theme material Torah Sings will ever touch after Job 38:7. **Narrative-time rule:** no New-Testament hindsight — the royal and messianic aches ("this day have I begotten you," 2:7; "they pierced my hands and my feet… they part my garments," 22:16–18; "sit at my right hand," 110:1; the wedding of the king, 45; "the stone the builders refused," 118:22) are held as unresolved longing, the mystery the witnesses carry toward the One promised, never named, never resolved. Impersonal creation the text supplies — the tree by the water, the sun like a bridegroom, the cup that overflows, the deep calling to deep, the watchman waiting for morning, the harps on the willows — may be invoked as image, never made the singer. We overhear, and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *Twenty-one albums. One hundred fifty-eight songs. No honest zeros — every one of the one hundred fifty psalms is sung. Every psalm accounted for exactly once.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its collection holds — the Asaph book eleven, the Ascents fifteen, the great acrostic its own record. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — HE PLANTED A MAN BY THE WATER
### Book I · the Two Ways and the first cries · Psalms 1–8 · eight songs

The songbook opens by dividing the whole world into two ways — a tree drinking at a stream and chaff the wind drives off — then lays a Son on Zion the nations rage against in vain, and turns to the first hunted cries of the night: a shield lifted, a bed of tears, a morning prayer. Between the raging kings and the weeping man the album lands on the oldest wonder of all — a heaven full of moon and stars, and the question of why the Maker would think of a man at all.

*Why this title:* Psalm 1 sets the album's root image — the blessed one planted by the water, bearing fruit in season while the wicked blow away as chaff.

**Tracklist (play order — canonical psalm order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **A Tree That Drinks in Secret** | Psalm 1 | The Tsade — the tzaddik, the righteous one — with the tree invoked as image | The two ways: one planted by the water, leaf unwithered, and the wicked as chaff the wind drives away — the whole book's doorway |
| 2 | **This Day I Have Begotten You** | Psalm 2 | The Angels, carrying the decree as a mystery not yet unveiled | The nations rage and the kings take counsel in vain; a Son set on Zion, "kiss the Son" — the heaviest royal ache, held as longing |
| 3 | **A Shield Around My Head** | Psalm 3 | The Angels, witnessing the hunted king lie down and wake | "Many say there is no help for him" — yet he sleeps and rises, for Yahuah is a shield about him, the lifter of his head |
| 4 | **I Will Lie Down in Peace** | Psalm 4 | The Angels, over a man who commanded his own heart on his bed | Gladness greater than harvest, and sleep in safety — trust laid down in the dark when others cry "who will show us good?" |
| 5 | **Give Ear to My Words at Morning** | Psalm 5 | The Angels, testifying to the dawn prayer | The voice directed up in the morning and kept waiting; a straight path asked for against the flattering throat that is an open grave |
| 6 | **All Night I Flooded the Bed** | Psalm 6 | The Angels, witnessing the first penitential tears | "I am weary with my groaning; all the night I make my bed to swim" — the first of the seven penitential cries, grief soaking the dark |

*Sequencing (canonical order):* the Two Ways open the book in hush; the raging nations and the begotten Son lift the stakes to the throne; the night-cries — shield, peaceful sleep, morning prayer, the flooded bed — carry the hunted trust downward into penitence; the righteous Judge steadies it; and the star-strewn wonder of the eighth psalm lands the album, the Letters singing the heavens they helped hang.

**Narrative-unit receipt (canonical order):** 1 the two ways · 2 the nations and the Son · 3 the shield · 4 peace on the bed · 5 the morning prayer · 6 the flooded bed · 7 the righteous Judge · 8 what is man. Eight for eight.

# ALBUM II — HE NEVER FORGOT THE CRY
### Book I · refuge, the fool, the apple of the eye · Psalms 9–17 · eight songs

Book I turns to the long question that will haunt the whole psalter — why the wicked stand while the afflicted fall — and answers it first in an alef-bet: the twin acrostic 9–10, one poem climbing the alphabet from praise to "why do you stand far off?" The album gathers the fool who says there is no Elohim, the pure words tried like silver in a furnace, and the man who asks only to be kept like the small dark center of an eye, hidden under the shadow of wings.

*Why this title:* Psalm 9:12 is the album's spine — "He does not forget the cry of the afflicted" — the answer held out against every "how long" that follows.

|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|

| 2 | **How Say You, Flee to Your Mountain** | Psalm 11 | The Angels, witnessing a refusal to run | "In Yahuah I take refuge; how say you to my soul, flee?" — the man who will not scatter like a bird, though the foundations are shaken |

**Narrative-unit receipt (canonical order):** 9–10 the acrostic (one poem, one song) · 11 refuge · 12 pure words · 13 how long · 14 the fool · 15 who shall dwell · 16 the michtam of the path of life · 17 the apple of the eye. Eight songs, nine psalms.

# ALBUM III — WHO IS THIS KING OF GLORY
### Book I · deliverance, the heavens, the shepherd, the gates · Psalms 18–24 · seven songs

The centerpiece of Book I climbs from a rescued king to the highest questions the songbook asks: a deliverance so total that Yahuah rides a cherub through the sky to reach one man; a heaven that declares glory without a word and a Torah sweeter than honey; the abandoned cry of the pierced one; the shepherd who lays a table in the valley; and the gates commanded to lift their heads for the King of glory coming in. It holds the psalter's deepest wound and its highest coronation back to back.

*Why this title:* Psalm 24 crowns the album — the gates lifting their heads while the ancient doors are asked, twice, "who is this King of glory?"

# ALBUM IV — TASTE AND SEE THAT HE IS GOOD
### Book I · the acrostics of trust and rescue · Psalms 25–34 · ten songs

This stretch of Book I is the psalter's first great school of trust, and the Letters run through two full acrostics inside it — 25 lifting the soul up the alphabet, 34 spelling out "taste and see." Around them the album gathers Yahuah as light and salvation, the voice of thunder over the waters, mourning turned to dancing, a spirit committed into the hand at nightfall, and the blessedness of the man whose sin is carried away.

*Why this title:* Psalm 34's acrostic invitation is the album's heart — "taste and see that Yahuah is tov" — the Tet's own word for good, offered to any who will try it.

# ALBUM V — UP FROM THE MIRY CLAY
### Book I · the close of the first book · Psalms 35–41 · seven songs

Book I ends where it began, in the mouth of the hunted — but now the trust has teeth: a plea for the Advocate to take up shield and spear, a stark portrait of wickedness with no fear of Elohim before its eyes, and the third acrostic, 37, counseling a soul not to fret while the meek quietly inherit the land. The record climbs out of a pit of clay into a new song, moves through the last penitential wasting of the flesh, and closes on the doxology that seals the first book: blessed is the one who considers the poor.

*Why this title:* Psalm 40's rescue is the album's turning image — drawn up out of the miry clay and the horrible pit, feet set on a rock, a new song put in the mouth.

# ALBUM VII — CREATE IN ME A CLEAN HEART
### Book II · Asaph's judge and the great penitence · Psalms 50–56 · seven songs

The album opens with the one Asaph psalm outside the Book III collection — Elohim summoning heaven and earth to judge a people who trust in ritual, wanting thanksgiving, not bulls — and then descends into the most searching penitence in all of Scripture: David's Miserere, the plea for a clean heart and a broken spirit that will not be despised. Around it the album gathers the boastful tyrant like a green tree soon uprooted, the fool who again denies Elohim, and the tears kept in a bottle by the One who counts every wandering.

*Why this title:* Psalm 51 is the album's core — "create in me a clean heart, O Elohim" — the great confession that asks not for escape but for a remade inward being.

**Tracklist (play order — canonical psalm order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |

# ALBUM XVII — THE ALPHABET OF LONGING
### Book V · the great acrostic · Psalm 119 · eleven songs

This is the Letters' own record — the single most on-theme album Torah Sings will ever make after Job 38:7. Psalm 119 is the alphabet at prayer: the longest chapter in Scripture, built as one self-contained eight-verse poem for every letter of the alef-bet in order, each stanza its own meditation on the Word. Uniquely in the whole psalter, the psalm's own structure is the platform's founding conceit made literal — so here, and here alone, the one-song-per-unit rule operates at the stanza level (each letter's octave is its own textual seam), and the letter-stanzas are sung in pairs, eleven songs, each carried by the two Letters whose names it bears, hanging on their two pictographs.

*Why this title:* Psalm 119 is not a hymn of arrival but of ache — a soul that pants, faints, melts, and clings for the Word through every letter of the alphabet; the Letters spell out longing itself.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine); Yah retained where the text sings it (Hallelu-Yah, 150:6). The Elohistic Psalter (42–83) uses Elohim as the psalms' own mouth prefers. No English "God" or "Lord" appears in any title or copy; the Hebrew article rule holds (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms — the priest-king oracle is sung as Malki-Tzedek, not "the HaMelech"); no anglicized divine names; internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing; no emoji.
- **The Picture Letters, never a count:** the Letters are named as personae — Aleph, Bet, Tsade, Tet, Ayin, Pey, Dalet, Resh, Vav, Shin, Lamed, and the rest — abundantly, as the acrostics invite. Their total is never stated; the psalms' own numbers are content, not mechanism (the ten-stringed harp of 33 and 92, the sevenfold refining of 12, the watches of the night of 130), and no gematria mechanism is sung.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter. The human psalmists — David, Asaph, the sons of Korach, Heman, Ethan, Mosheh, Shelomoh — and their laments, penitence, and thanksgiving are *witnessed and testified to*, never voiced as the human who wept (the great penitence of 51, the darkness of 88, the depths of 130, the exile of 137, the searched soul of 139). The cosmic, creation, enthronement, and praise psalms are sung as the Angels' and Letters' own liturgy (8, 19, 29, 33, 96–100, 103, 104, 113, 117, 148, 150). Impersonal creation the text supplies — the tree by the water, the sun like a bridegroom, the cup that overflows, the deep, the watchman, the willow-hung harps, the ark — is invoked as image, never made the singer.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** the royal and messianic aches are carried as unresolved longing, never resolved forward — the begotten Son of 2, the pierced hands and parted garments of 22, the reproach and vinegar of 69, the priest-king at the right hand of 110, the wedding of 45, the throne-oath of 132, and the rejected stone become cornerstone of 118 are all held as the mystery the witnesses carry toward the One promised, never named as fulfilled. The framing names Yeshua; the songs, standing inside their own hour, do not.
- **The acrostics given their due:** the Letters sing their own alef-bet explicitly at 9–10, 25, 34, 37, 111, 112, 145, and above all across the whole of 119 — the platform's most on-theme material after Job 38:7.
