# JUDGES — THE SIX ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book of the Deliverers

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, `pitches/Leviticus - The Seven Albums.md`, `pitches/Numbers - The Eight Albums.md`, and `pitches/Deuteronomy - The Nine Albums.md`
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Judges has no single formula like the Genesis *toledot*; its own seams are the cyclical refrain ("and the children of Israel again did evil in the sight of Yahuah"), each deliverer's cycle of oppression → crying out → judge → rest → relapse, the minor-judge notices, and the two-part appendix. Every song below is one narrative unit drawn at those seams.
**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator, Yahuah — and, from *within* each moment of the story, toward the Son promised but not yet unveiled. Judges gives its own angelic witnesses to carry the song: the **messenger of Yahuah who came up to Bochim** (2), the **messenger who sat under the oak at Ophrah** and called a man from a winepress (6), the **messenger who ascended in the altar flame** to announce a nazir's birth (13) — and the night the very **stars in their courses fought** from heaven (5:20). **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own place in Scripture, with no New-Testament hindsight — Yeshua is named and adored in the platform's framing, but inside a Judges-moment song the book's own ache, *there was no king in Israel; every man did what was right in his own eyes*, is carried as longing for a deliverer not yet come, never named as fulfilled; no cross, no Messiah, no fulfillment language enters a song. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or the personified Letters — never a human character (not Devorah, not Gid'on, not Yiftach, not Shimshon, not Ya'el, not Barak, not Michah, not the Levite) or anything derived from one; impersonal creation the text supplies (Akhsah's springs, the tent peg, the fleece, the trumpets and jars and torches, the foxes' fire, the jawbone, the millstone from the tower, the city gates) may be *invoked as image*, never made the singer. Devorah's own song is *witnessed* — the Angels overhear the victory hymn and testify to it; they never become Devorah. We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *Six albums. Thirty-three songs. No honest zeros — every narrative unit of Judges is sung. Every chapter accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its stories hold. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — A GENERATION THAT KNEW HIM NOT
### The Unfinished Conquest and the Cycle · Judges 1:1–3:6 · four songs

The land is not yet fully taken, and it never will be. Yehudah wins the hill country but not the valleys of iron; tribe after tribe fails to drive out the Canaanite and settles for living beside him; a messenger of Yahuah comes up from Gilgal to a place he leaves named Weepers, and tells them the nations they spared will be thorns in their sides. Then Yehoshua's generation dies, and another rises that never knew what the hand of Yahuah had done — and the wheel begins to turn: they do evil, He sells them, they cry out, He raises a deliverer, they have rest, they relapse. The witnesses sing the slow forgetting, and the last clear blessing — a daughter's springs — before the long dark.

*Why this title:* the whole book's diagnosis is stated at 2:10 — "there arose another generation which knew not Yahuah, nor yet the works which He had done for Israel"; every failure that follows grows from that forgetting.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Give Me Also the Springs** | Judges 1:1–15 | The Mem — the water — with the Angels | A daughter given a parched south land asks for a blessing and receives the upper springs and the lower (1:15) — water still flowing before the slide begins; behind it, the king who cut the thumbs from seventy kings confessing "as I have done, so Elohim has repaid me" |
| 2 | **But the Iron Would Not Yield** | Judges 1:16–36 | The Angels, reading the map of what was left undone | Tribe after tribe that "could not drive out" the Canaanite because of the iron chariots of the valley — the conquest halted half-finished, the enemy left dwelling in the house |
| 3 | **I Will Not Drive Them Out** | Judges 2:1–5 | The messenger of Yahuah, come up from Gilgal | Heaven's own envoy who once went before the armies now stands still and refuses — "they shall be thorns in your sides, and their gods a snare" — and a whole people weeping until the place is named Bochim, Weepers |
| 4 | **Sold, and Delivered, and Sold Again** | Judges 2:6–3:6 | The Angels, who watched the wheel turn again and again | The generation that knew Him not, and the formula laid bare — evil, oppression, the cry, a judge, rest, relapse — the nations left in the land to test a people who kept failing the test |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the springs open in the one bright key the book still holds; the litany of what could not be driven out darkens it; the messenger at Bochim is the verdict spoken aloud from heaven; and the stated cycle lands the album as the engine that will drive every record after it — the pattern set before a single deliverer is named.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 1:1–15 the conquest of Yehudah and Akhsah's springs · 1:16–36 the nations not driven out · 2:1–5 the messenger at Bochim · 2:6–3:6 the death of the generation and the cycle stated. Four for four.

# ALBUM II — A LEFT HAND AND A TENT PEG
### The First Deliverers · Judges 3:7–5:31 · five songs

Heaven keeps saving them with the wrong tools. The first judge is simply the first man the Spirit seizes; the second is a left-handed man with a blade worn on the wrong thigh, past every guard; the third fells six hundred with a farmer's cattle-goad. Then a prophetess sends a reluctant general to a river that fights for them, and the enemy commander — who never lost a chariot — dies in a tent, at a woman's feet, under a tool for pitching tents. And on that night the witnesses confess they did not only watch: the stars in their courses came down and fought. The album gathers the first deliverers and the unlikely hands that carried them.

*Why this title:* two of the album's most vivid deliverances come through domestic, unwarlike things — Ehud's dagger drawn from the right thigh no one guards, and Ya'el's tent peg — a pattern of Yahuah saving through the instrument the enemy never thought to fear.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **One Cry, and Forty Years of Rest** | Judges 3:7–11 | The Angels | The first rotation of the cycle — Israel cries out, the Spirit of Yahuah seizes Otniel, the double-wicked king of Aram falls, and the land is still forty years — the pattern before it curdles |
| 2 | **The Blade on the Right Thigh** | Judges 3:12–30 | The Angels | A left-handed man with a two-edged dagger bound where no guard checks, a very fat king in a cool upper room, the blade swallowed hilt and all — and the servants waiting outside, sure he only covers his feet |
| 3 | **An Oxgoad Against Six Hundred** | Judges 3:31 | The Lamed — the goad, the shepherd's staff | Not a sword but a cattle-prod, the tool that drives oxen driving out an army — six hundred fallen to a farmer's goad, the deliverer the text gives one verse and moves past |
| 4 | **Into the Hand of a Woman** | Judges 4 | The Vav — the tent peg, the nail that pitches a tent | Devorah's word to Barak — the honor of the day goes into a woman's hand — made good when nine hundred iron chariots end at the river and their commander is pinned to the ground by the pin that holds down a tent |
| 5 | **The Stars Fought From Their Courses** | Judges 5 | The Picture Letters — the morning stars themselves — the Angels overhearing Devorah's song and testifying to it | The victory hymn overheard from above: the Kishon sweeping the kings away, the stars in their courses warring against Sisera from heaven, and a mother at her lattice watching a road for a son who will not come |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the four deliverers run in the text's order — the first judge, the left hand, the oxgoad, the tent peg — each stranger than the last; and the Song of Devorah lands the album not as a new event but as the same night sung from heaven's side, the one place in Scripture where the witnesses confess the stars came down into the fight.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 3:7–11 Otniel · 3:12–30 Ehud and Eglon · 3:31 Shamgar · 4 Devorah, Barak, and Ya'el · 5 the Song of Devorah. Five for five. *(Chapter 4's prose deliverance is one unit and one song; chapter 5's poem is a distinct unit — the sung retelling — exactly as the Song of the Sea stood apart from the crossing on Exodus V.)*

# ALBUM III — FROM THE WINEPRESS TO THE BRAMBLE THRONE
### Gid'on and the Bramble King · Judges 6–9 · eight songs

A frightened man threshes wheat inside a winepress to hide it from Midian, and a messenger of Yahuah sitting under an oak calls him "mighty man of valor." What follows is the fullest cycle in the book: an altar of Baal thrown down in the dark, a fleece asked wet and then dry, an army cut from thousands to three hundred so that no man can boast, and torches sealed in clay jars that rout a host when the jars break. But the deliverer who refuses a crown gathers the gold of victory into an ephod that becomes a snare — and his son crowns himself the bramble-king, murders seventy brothers on one stone, and dies with a woman's millstone in his skull. The witnesses sing the whole arc, from the winepress to the tower.

*Why this title:* the album runs from a man hiding in a winepress (6:11) to the bramble that Yotam's parable says was the only tree willing to be king (9:15) — the rise of a deliverer and the ruin his own house became.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **Mighty Man of Valor** | Judges 6:1–24 | The messenger of Yahuah, seated under the oak at Ophrah | An envoy of heaven under a tree calling a man who is hiding in a winepress a mighty man of valor — and fire rising out of the rock to eat the meat and the unleavened bread |
| 2 | **Let Baal Contend for Himself** | Judges 6:25–32 | The Angels | An altar of Baal pulled down by night, a bull burned on the wood of the idol-grove, and a father's dare that renames his son — if Baal is a god, let him plead his own case |
| 3 | **The Sign He Asked Twice** | Judges 6:33–40 | The Angels (the fleece invoked as image) | A wool fleece heavy with dew on dry ground, then bone-dry on ground soaked with dew — a man clothed with the Ruach still asking heaven to prove itself, and prove itself reversed |
| 4 | **Light Inside the Broken Jars** | Judges 7 | The Shin — the torch-fire — with the Angels | Three hundred who lapped water like dogs, torchlight sealed inside clay pitchers until the jars shatter at midnight; the blast of trumpets and Midian's own swords turned against itself — the sword of Yahuah and of Gid'on |
| 5 | **No Bread for the Fainting** | Judges 8:1–21 | The Angels | Three hundred faint but pursuing, and two Israelite towns that bolt their bread against them mid-rescue — Sukkoth and Penuel repaid with briers and a broken tower, and the two desert kings taken at last |

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 6:1–24 the call under the oak · 6:25–32 the altar and Yerubaal · 6:33–40 the fleece · 7 the three hundred · 8:1–21 the pursuit and the withheld bread · 8:22–35 the refused crown and the ephod · 9:1–21 Avimelech and Yotam's parable · 9:22–57 the fall of Avimelech. Eight for eight. *(Per methodology §3.3, a coherent set larger than seven carries all its songs — Gid'on's cycle and its ruinous aftermath are one escalating musical.)*

# ALBUM IV — THE WORDS THEY COULD NOT TAKE BACK
### Yiftach and the Deadly Word · Judges 10–12 · five songs

This is the album of the word that cannot be recalled. Heaven, worn down, tells a relapsed people to cry to the gods they chose — and then cannot bear their misery any longer. A harlot's son, thrown out by his brothers, is begged back to lead them, and buys his victory with a vow he should never have spoken: whatever comes first through his door. What comes through the door is his only child, dancing with timbrels. And when Ephraim quarrels at the fords, a single syllable becomes the line between life and death — forty-two thousand who cannot shape the sound of *Shibboleth*. The witnesses sing the cost of rash words, and close with the quiet judges whose whole record is sons and donkeys.

*Why this title:* the album turns on words that could not be unsaid — Yiftach's vow, sworn to purchase a victory and paid in a daughter, and the password at the Jordan that cost forty-two thousand lives over one sound the tongue could not make.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **His Soul Was Grieved for Them** | Judges 10:6–18 | The Angels, who heard both the refusal and the grief | Heaven telling a people to go and cry to the idols they chose — "I will deliver you no more" — and then, in the same breath, a soul that could bear their misery no longer |
| 2 | **The Outcast They Called Back** | Judges 11:1–28 | The Angels | A harlot's son driven from his father's house by his brothers, made captain of raiders, then begged home to lead the very ones who exiled him when the Ammonites come |
| 3 | **She Came Out With Timbrels** | Judges 11:29–40 | The Angels, grieving witnesses from above | A victorious father met at his own door by his only child, dancing with timbrels to greet him — and a rash vow that turns a homecoming into two months of weeping on the hills; lamented, from heaven's side, with grave restraint |
| 4 | **They Could Not Say Shibboleth** | Judges 12:1–7 | The Peh — the mouth, the letter of speech | The fords of the Jordan made a checkpoint of the tongue — the password a single sound, and forty-two thousand betrayed by an accent their mouths could not shape |
| 5 | **Thirty Sons on Thirty Donkeys** | Judges 10:1–5 + 12:8–15 | The Angels — the ledger-keepers who remember even the quiet | The judges between the storms, whose whole record is sons and daughters and towns and donkeys — Tola, Yair, Ivtzan, Elon, Avdon — the still years that leave no scar and, but for this, no song |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* heaven's grief opens the record; the recalled outcast and the vow carry it into its darkest, most restrained beat; the Shibboleth slaughter is the turn where a careless word kills on a national scale; and the gathered minor judges land the album as its benediction — the quiet peace framing Yiftach's storms, drawn together from the notices that open chapter 10 and close chapter 12.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 10:6–18 the deepening apostasy and heaven's grief · 11:1–28 Yiftach recalled · 11:29–40 the vow and the daughter · 12:1–7 Shibboleth at the fords · 10:1–5 + 12:8–15 the minor judges gathered (a §3.3 distribution unit — the five quiet judges brought onto one song rather than stranded on either side of Yiftach). Five for five.

# ALBUM V — OUT OF THE STRONG, SOMETHING SWEET
### Shimshon · Judges 13–16 · six songs

His whole life is his own riddle: sweetness drawn from a carcass, deliverance drawn from ruin. A messenger of Yahuah whose name is "Wonderful" announces him to a barren woman and then rises into the flame of the altar; the boy grows into a nazir whose strength sleeps in uncut hair. He finds honey in a dead lion and loses a riddle to a wife's tears; he burns a harvest with foxes and heaps a thousand slain with a donkey's jawbone; he carries off a city's gates at midnight. Then a whisper does what no army could — seven locks shorn on a lap, and a man who does not know the Strength has quietly left him. Blind and grinding, he asks for one last thing between two pillars, and wins more in his death than in his life.

*Why this title:* Shimshon's own wedding riddle — "out of the eater came forth meat, and out of the strong came forth sweetness" (14:14) — is the shape of his whole life: honey from a carcass, and the greatest deliverance of all drawn out of his ruin.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **He Went Up in the Altar Flame** | Judges 13 | The messenger of Yahuah, who ascended in the flame | Heaven's envoy naming himself only "Wonderful," announcing a nazir whose hair must never be cut — then rising to heaven inside the flame of the sacrifice as a barren couple falls on their faces |
| 2 | **Honey in the Carcass** | Judges 14 | The Angels (the lion and the honey invoked as image) | A lion torn bare-handed, and days later honey found in its ribs — sweetness scooped from death, wagered as a riddle and lost to a wife's seven days of tears |
| 3 | **Heaps Upon Heaps, by a Jawbone** | Judges 15 | The Angels | Three hundred foxes trailing fire through the standing grain, and a fresh donkey's jawbone in one fist heaping the slain a thousand high — then a spring split open at Lehi for a strong man's thirst |
| 4 | **The City Gates on His Shoulders** | Judges 16:1–3 | The Dalet — the door, the gate | A city's gate meant to trap him at dawn — the doors, the posts, and the bar all torn loose at midnight and carried to the top of a far hill, the trap uprooted and shouldered away |
| 5 | **He Did Not Know It Left Him** | Judges 16:4–22 | The Angels, who watched the Presence depart before he woke | Seven locks shorn while he sleeps on Delilah's knees, and the terrible quiet of a man who rises to shake himself as before — not knowing that the strength, and the One who gave it, had gone |
| 6 | **Let Me Die With Them** | Judges 16:23–31 | The Angels (the two pillars invoked as image) | A blind grinder led out to be mocked, three thousand on the roof, Dagon praised over him — and his hands finding the two central pillars for one last bow that killed more than his whole life had |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the annunciation in the flame opens the record in wonder; the riddle and the honey, the foxes and the jawbone, the carried gates build the strange strength through the middle; the shorn locks are the turn — the whisper that undid what no army could, sung as the departed Presence rather than the spectacle of it; and the temple pulled down lands the album on the riddle come true, the greatest deliverance drawn out of a ruin.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 13 the messenger and the annunciation · 14 the riddle, the lion, and the honey · 15 the foxes and the jawbone · 16:1–3 the gates of Gaza · 16:4–22 Delilah and the shorn strength · 16:23–31 Dagon's temple. Six for six.

# ALBUM VI — NO KING IN ISRAEL
### The Unraveling · Judges 17–21 · five songs

The deliverers are done, and the book ends by showing what the land had quietly become. A man builds a household shrine of stolen-then-melted silver and hires a wandering Levite, certain heaven now owes him favor. A landless tribe carries off both the idols and the priest and burns a peaceful town to enthrone them. A traveler's night in Giv'ah becomes an outrage so great the body is cut into twelve pieces to summon a nation — and the nation nearly erases one of its own tribes in the war that follows, then rebuilds it by seizing dancers from the vineyards of Shiloh. Over all of it the refrain tolls twice: there was no king in Israel; every man did what was right in his own eyes. The witnesses sing the descent, and the ache of a deliverer not yet come.

*Why this title:* the book's own refrain frames these last chapters and names their disease — "in those days there was no king in Israel" (17:6; 18:1; 19:1; 21:25) — the longing, never yet answered, for one who would rule and set things right.

**Tracklist (play order — biblical narrative order):**

| # | Song | Passage | Who sings | The image it hangs on |
|---|------|---------|-----------|----------------------|
| 1 | **He Bought Himself a Priest** | Judges 17 | The Angels | Silver first stolen then melted into a household god, a Levite hired by the year, and a man certain Yahuah must bless him now that he owns a priest — the first tolling of "no king in Israel" |
| 2 | **Better a Priest to a Tribe** | Judges 18 | The Angels | A landless tribe seizing another man's idols and his hired Levite — a priest gladly trading one household for a whole tribe — and quiet, unsuspecting Laish burned to plant the stolen worship |
| 3 | **Twelve Pieces Through All Israel** | Judges 19 | The Angels, grieving witnesses | The outrage at Giv'ah named only in grief — a threshold at dawn, and a summons carried in twelve portions to every tribe, that a nation might at last be forced to reckon; handled with grave restraint |
| 4 | **Down to Six Hundred Men** | Judges 20 | The Angels | All Israel turned against one of its own tribes, the smoke of Giv'ah rising as the ambush signal, and Binyamin cut down until only six hundred men flee to the rock of Rimmon |
| 5 | **Right in His Own Eyes** | Judges 21 | The Angels, landing the book's ache | A tribe rebuilt by seizing the dancing daughters of Shiloh from the vineyards — one wound closed by tearing open another — and the book's final sigh: no king in Israel, every man his own |

*Sequencing (biblical narrative order):* the private idol opens the descent; the stolen priesthood widens it from one house to a tribe; the outrage at Giv'ah is the turn, sung with the gravest restraint the catalog allows; the near-annihilation of Binyamin is the ruin; and the seized dancers of Shiloh land the whole book — and the whole record — not on resolution but on the refrain, the unanswered longing for a king who has not yet come.

**Narrative-unit receipt (text order):** 17 Michah's idol and hired Levite · 18 the Danite migration and theft · 19 the Levite's concubine at Giv'ah · 20 the war against Binyamin · 21 the wives for the survivors and the closing refrain. Five for five.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine); the Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). The English words for deity are never used for the Creator; the idols are named as what the text calls them (Baal, Dagon, the elohim of the nations), never dignified as His equals. Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** the book's refrain — *no king in Israel; every man did what was right in his own eyes* — is carried as the ache of a deliverer, a king, not yet come, and is never named as fulfilled. No song reaches for the cross, the Messiah unveiled, or any New-Testament hindsight; the messianic longing lives here only as unanswered longing. The framing names Yeshua; the songs, standing inside their own hour, do not.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels, a named Letter (the Mem, the Lamed, the Vav, the Shin, the Resh, the Peh, the Dalet, the Picture Letters as the morning stars), or one of the three angelic messengers the text itself supplies — the one at Bochim, the one under the oak at Ophrah, the one who ascends in the altar flame. Devorah, Barak, Gid'on, Yiftach, Shimshon, Ya'el, Michah, and the Levite are *witnessed*, never made the singer; the springs, the tent peg, the fleece, the jars and torches, the foxes' fire, the jawbone, the millstone, and the city gates are invoked as images, never voiced. Devorah's own song (5) is overheard and testified to, never sung in her person.
- **Grave restraint on the atrocities:** the three hardest passages — Yiftach's daughter (11), the outrage and the twelve pieces at Giv'ah (19), and the near-annihilation of Binyamin (20) — are set as witnessed lament from heaven's side, family-friendly and without gore; the shock is carried by grief, not by detail.
- **Picture Letters, never a count:** individual letters are named as personae only; no total is ever stated and no gematria mechanism is sung.
