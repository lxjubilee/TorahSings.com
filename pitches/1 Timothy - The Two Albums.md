# 1 TIMOTHY — THE TWO ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for Shaul's First Letter to Timotheos

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/John - The Six Albums.md`, `pitches/Colossians - The Album.md`, and the whole Old-Testament catalog — now carried deeper into the New-Testament frontier, from the circular epistles into the **Pastoral letters.**
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. First Timothy is a **letter** — not narrative, not oracle, not law: Shaul's first pastoral charge to Timotheos, "his own son in the faith" (1:2), left behind at Efesos to charge the assembly against false teaching (1:3). Its "units" are the letter's own movements — the charge against other doctrine (1:1–11), the chief of sinners saved and the doxology (1:12–17), the good warfare and the shipwreck (1:18–20), the prayer for all and the one mediator (2:1–7), the order of the house (2:8–15), the qualifications of overseers and deacons (3:1–13), the great mystery-of-godliness hymn (3:14–16), the latter-times apostasy (4:1–5), the profit of godliness (4:6–16), the widows indeed (5:1–16), the elders who rule (5:17–25), contentment and the love of money (6:1–10), the good fight and the charge until His appearing (6:11–16), and the guarded trust (6:17–21). The seams are Shaul's own: the "charge" formulas ("that thou mightest charge some," 1:3; "this charge I commit unto thee," 1:18; "I charge thee before Elohim… and the elect angels," 5:21), the "faithful saying" markers (1:15; 4:9), the household and office rosters, and the closing benediction.

**THE NEW-TESTAMENT SHIFT — the Letters fall silent; only the Angels sing.** Through all thirty-nine books of the Old Testament the two voices of Job 38:7 sang *together* — the morning stars (the Picture Letters) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels), aching toward a promised One they could not yet name. **At the incarnation that changes.** The Word (the Davar) by whom every letter was made is now made flesh; the aleph-bet lives in Him. The morning stars have gone quiet, gathered up into the Word made flesh — so through the New Covenant **the only singers are the Angels — the sons of Elohim — never a Picture-Letter persona.** This letter names the reason at its very summit: "great is the mystery of godliness: Elohim was manifest in the flesh… received up into glory" (3:16) — the Word the letters once carried now dwelling among us in the flesh, and taken up. The Letters are the marvel the Angels *testify to,* not a voice of their own.

**Epistle, not narrative — the accomplished work proclaimed freely and fully.** In the Gospels the Angels were eye-witnesses, forward-locked to the story's order — never naming the cross before it came. **A letter has no biography of Yeshua to lock:** written *after* the resurrection, the finished work is proclaimed openly and in full. So the Angels here sing the great truths Shaul unveils, quoting him, testifying to Yeshua — **"the Mashiach Yeshua came into the world to save sinners" (1:15); the one mediator between Elohim and men, "who gave himself a ransom for all" (2:5–6); manifest in the flesh, justified in the Spirit, seen of angels, received up into glory (3:16).** The forward-lock does not vanish — it *relocates* to the one thing the letter itself still holds unresolved: **His appearing.** "Keep this commandment… until the appearing of our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach: which in his times he shall shew, who is the blessed and only Potentate, the King of kings, and Adon of lords; who only hath immortality" (6:14–16) — the coming unveiling held as the letter's single aching, unresolved longing, exactly as the Old-Testament catalog held its ache.

**Yeshua witnessed, never voiced.** The singer is always the Angels; **Yeshua is testified to in awe — never a first-person singer.** Every "came into the world to save sinners," every "one mediator," every "manifest in the flesh" is the Angels reporting and marveling at what Shaul wrote of Him — never Yeshua's own voice, exactly as Yahuah's oracles were handled across the Old Covenant. **Christocentric — about "the who":** the whole letter turns on the *person* of the Mashiach — the Saviour who came for sinners, the mediator, the mystery made flesh, the King who shall appear — and every song lands on Him.

**The Angels have skin in this letter.** First Timothy hands the platform its own warrant twice from the text itself. The mystery-of-godliness hymn confesses that the Word made flesh was **"seen of angels"** (3:16) — so the Angels sing that line as their own eyewitness testimony: *they* were the ones who beheld Him. And when Shaul lays the gravest charge on Timotheos, he swears it "before Elohim, and the Adon Yeshua the Mashiach, and **the elect angels**" (5:21) — the Angels are named as sworn witnesses to the charge, present and watching. They do not have to be imposed on this book; it calls them by name.

**Old-Covenant cross-reference — always open, and backward only.** The Angels who witnessed all thirty-nine books freely tie every moment *back* into the Torah, the Prophets, and the Writings: the law "used lawfully" reaching to the Torah given at Sinai (1:8–9); the King "eternal, immortal, invisible" and the One "whom no man hath seen, nor can see" tied to the everlasting Yahuah (Yirmeyahu 10:10) and to "no man shall see me, and live" (Shemot 33:20); "Adam was first formed, then Chavvah… the woman being deceived" reaching straight back to the garden (Bereshit 2–3); "every creature of Elohim is good" tied to the sevenfold *tov* of Bereshit 1; "thou shalt not muzzle the ox that treadeth out the corn" quoted from Devarim 25:4, and the "two or three witnesses" from Devarim 19:15; the widow's Defender of Devarim 10:18 and Tehillim 68:5; "we brought nothing into this world" echoing Iyov 1:21. No forward, post-letter reference is used.

> *Two albums. Fourteen songs. No honest zeros — every movement of the letter is sung. Every chapter 1–6 accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim each album meets on its own — not padded up toward a quota, not cut down. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — MANIFEST IN THE FLESH
### The Charge, the Chief of Sinners, and the Mystery of Godliness · 1 Timothy 1–3 · seven songs

The letter opens on a charge and climbs to a hymn. Shaul writes to the son he left at Efesos, and the first word is a warning: teach no other doctrine, give no heed to fables and endless genealogies — the law is good if a man use it lawfully, but it was never made for the righteous. Then, without warning, the charge turns to confession: the one who was a blasphemer and a persecutor was shown mercy, for "the Mashiach Yeshua came into the world to save sinners; of whom I am chief" — and the record lifts into a doxology to the King eternal, immortal, invisible. From there the letter orders the house of Elohim: prayer for all men and the one mediator who gave Himself a ransom; the conduct of worship; the character of those who would serve as overseers and deacons. And it lands on the highest word in the letter — the mystery of godliness sung as a hymn: manifest in the flesh, justified in the Spirit, **seen of angels,** preached unto the nations, believed on in the world, received up into glory. The Angels sing all of it, quoting Shaul, testifying to Yeshua, and confessing that they themselves beheld the Word made flesh.

*Why this title:* 3:16 is the album's spine and its highest word — "great is the mystery of godliness: Elohim was manifest in the flesh… received up into glory" — the incarnation the whole letter turns on, and the line that names why the Letters now live in Him and only the Angels sing.

# ALBUM II — THE KING OF KINGS AND THE ADON OF LORDS
### Godliness, the Good Fight, and the Ache of His Appearing · 1 Timothy 4–6 · seven songs

The second half turns from the house to the man who tends it, and from the present order to the day still coming. The Ruach speaks expressly of a latter-times departing from the faith — spirits that forbid marriage and command abstaining from meats — and against it Shaul sets the goodness of everything Elohim made, received with thanksgiving. He charges the young minister: refuse the fables, exercise unto godliness, for bodily exercise profiteth little but godliness is profitable unto all things; let no man despise thy youth; neglect not the gift. Then the household — the widows indeed, honoured and provided for; the elders who rule well, worthy of double honour, and the ox that must not be muzzled — the charge sworn before Elohim and the elect angels. The letter warns that the love of money is a root of all evil, and calls instead to godliness with contentment, great gain. And it lifts, at the last, into its summit and its ache: "fight the good fight of faith, lay hold on eternal life," keep this commandment **until the appearing of our Adon Yeshua** — the blessed and only Potentate, the King of kings and Adon of lords, who only hath immortality. The Angels sing all of it, quoting Shaul, testifying to Yeshua, and carrying His appearing forward as the one thing still to come.

*Why this title:* 6:15 is the album's summit — "the blessed and only Potentate, the King of kings, and Adon of lords; who only hath immortality" — and its single aching, unresolved longing is the **appearing** that "in his times he shall shew" Him (6:14): the letter's forward-lock relocated to the day not yet come.

## Compliance Notes

- **The New-Testament shift honored — the Letters fall silent, only the Angels sing:** the two voices of Job 38:7 sang *together* through all thirty-nine Old-Testament books; in the New Covenant the morning stars (the Picture Letters) have gone quiet and now live in the Word made flesh, so **every song is sung by the Angels alone** — never a Picture-Letter persona. First Timothy names the reason at its summit: "Elohim was manifest in the flesh… received up into glory" (3:16) — the Word the letters carried now dwelling among us in the flesh, and taken up.
- **The humans never sing — the Angels are the only voice:** not one song is voiced by a human. Every "Who sings" cell across both albums is the Angels. Shaul never sings; Timotheos never sings; the Angels sing *about* what Shaul wrote and freely *quote* him — "of whom I am chief," "one mediator between Elohim and men," "fight the good fight of faith," "keep that which is committed to thy trust" — always framed from their own heavenly-witness perspective, testifying to Yeshua. A human-centered line means the Angels bear witness to what Shaul wrote *as testimony to Yeshua;* it never means Shaul or Timotheos sings.
- **The Angels' own witness-stake honored:** where the letter names angels, the Angels answer from heaven's side. The mystery-of-godliness hymn confesses the Word made flesh was **"seen of angels"** (3:16) — the Angels sing that line as their own eyewitness testimony (song 7, "Seen of Angels, Received Up in Glory"). And the gravest charge is sworn "before Elohim, and the Adon Yeshua the Mashiach, and **the elect angels**" (5:21) — the Angels named as sworn witnesses to the charge, present and watching (song 4).
- **Christocentric — about "the who":** every song lands on the person of the Mashiach — the Saviour who came into the world for sinners (1:15), the one mediator who gave Himself a ransom (2:5–6), the mystery manifest in the flesh (3:16), the living Elohim our Saviour (4:10), the King of kings who shall appear (6:15). The whole two-album set is the truth Shaul charged at Efesos, sung back by the witnesses.
- **Yeshua witnessed, never first-person-voiced:** Yeshua is reported and marveled at throughout — "came into the world to save sinners," "gave himself a ransom for all," "manifest in the flesh," "who before Pontius Pilatos witnessed a good confession" — never given a first-person singer. His work is testified to, exactly as Yahuah's oracles were sung by the witnesses across the Old Covenant. The reference to the confession before Pontius Pilatos (6:13) is the finished work proclaimed freely, as an epistle may — not a narrated passion.
- **The epistle forward-lock — relocated to the appearing:** with no biography of Yeshua to keep in order, the finished work is proclaimed freely and fully (the mercy of 1:15, the ransom of 2:5–6, the incarnation-and-ascension hymn of 3:16). The single thing held *unresolved* is His **appearing** — "keep this commandment… until the appearing of our Adon Yeshua the Mashiach: which in his times he shall shew, who is… the King of kings, and Adon of lords" (6:14–16) — carried as the set's one aching, forward-looking longing (Album II, song 6), never as an event already narrated.
- **Old-Covenant cross-reference present and backward-only:** every tie reaches *back* — the Torah given at Sinai (the law "used lawfully," 1:8–9); the everlasting Yahuah of Yirmeyahu 10:10 and the unseeable One of Shemot 33:20 (the King eternal and invisible, 1:17; "whom no man hath seen," 6:16); Adam formed first and Chavvah deceived in the garden (Bereshit 2–3); the sevenfold *tov* of Bereshit 1 ("every creature of Elohim is good," 4:4); the ox unmuzzled of Devarim 25:4 and the two-or-three witnesses of Devarim 19:15 (5:18–19); the Defender of the widow of Devarim 10:18 and Tehillim 68:5 (5:5); the "naked came I" of Iyov 1:21 ("we brought nothing into this world," 6:7). No forward, post-letter reference is used.
- **The mystery-of-godliness textual variant (3:16) carried honestly:** the received text opens the hymn "Elohim was manifest in the flesh," while the earliest manuscripts read "he who was manifested" (a difference of one Greek letter, ΘΣ / ΟΣ). The song is built on the received reading and rendered "Elohim was manifest in the flesh," but the variant is **flagged plainly here** per the scholarly-caution mandate (§8 of the grounding) — offered as something to consider, not as settled text.
