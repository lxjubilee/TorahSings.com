# 1 SAMUEL — THE EIGHT ALBUMS
## The Torah Sings Catalog Pitch for the Book of the First King

**Platform:** Torah Sings (TorahSings.com) · Zev Inspire, project lead · all twelve Inspire Family members sing where suitable
**Governed by:** `_METHODOLOGY - Album & Song Creation.md` (signed standard) and `_GROUNDING - Torah Sings Project.md`
**Built by:** the process in `.prompts/music_torahSings - pitch process.md` — the same one that produced `pitches/Genesis - The Nine Albums.md`, `pitches/Exodus - The Ten Albums.md`, and the rest of the shipped catalog
**Scope:** High-level album and song titles only — no lyrics yet. Every song below is one narrative unit; every unit boundary sits on the text's own seams — the battle and campaign narratives, the regnal and anointing markers, the speech openings ("and Yahuah said unto Shmuel"), the sacrifice-and-vow formulas, and the "unto this day" memorials 1 Samuel supplies in place of the Genesis *toledot*.
**The voices:** Job 38:7, followed precisely — the morning stars are the Picture Letters; the sons of Elohim are the Angels. They sing praise to the Creator, Yahuah — and, from *within* each moment of the story, toward the Son promised but not yet unveiled. **Narrative-time rule:** every song is sung from inside its own hour of the story, with no New-Testament hindsight — Yeshua is named and adored in the platform's framing, but inside a 1 Samuel-moment song the horn of oil poured on a shepherd boy, the anointed-but-not-yet-enthroned, "Yahuah looks on the heart," the king Israel demanded set against the king Yahuah chose, the faithful priest promised, the stone of help named Even-ha-Ezer are carried as mystery and ache, never named as accomplished fulfillment. The word *mashiach* — the anointed — is sung only as the text sings it over Sha'ul and David, never resolved forward to Yeshua. **Voice rule:** the singer is always the Angels and/or the personified Letters — never a human character (not Shmuel, not Chanah, not Eli, not Sha'ul, not David, not Yonatan, not Avigayil) or anything derived from one; Chanah's song, Shmuel's oracles, and David's refusals are *witnessed and testified to* from heaven's side, never sung as the human. Impersonal creation the text supplies — the ark, the fallen idol Dagon, the lamp of Elohim not yet gone out, the sling-stone, the spear at the bolster, the cut robe-corner, the cruse of water — may be *invoked as image*, never made the singer. The medium of Ein-Dor (28) is handled with grave restraint: witnessed dread from heaven's side, no occult glamor. We overhear — and join in, or stand in shock and awe.

> *Eight albums. Forty-one songs. No honest zeros — every narrative unit of 1 Samuel is sung. All thirty-one chapters accounted for.*
> *Seven is the aim, not a cap: each album carries as many songs as its stories hold. Not canon — something to consider.*

# ALBUM I — THE WORD CAME BACK TO A CHILD
### Chanah and the Boy Shmuel · 1 Samuel 1–3 · five songs

The word of Yahuah was rare in those days, and there was no open vision — the priesthood at Shiloh had rotted down to two sons who did not know Yahuah. Into that silence a barren woman prays without a voice, is mistaken for a drunk, and vows the son she begs for back to the One who gives him. She sings a song about thrones before Israel has a king; her boy grows in a linen ephod beside priests who steal the fat from the fire; a nameless prophet reads the doom of the house of Eli; and one night, before a lamp not yet burned out, a Voice calls a child by name. The witnesses sing the return of the word — through a woman's silent lips and a boy's waking ear, while the old order gutters low.

*Why this title:* the album's whole arc lands at 3:1–10 — the word of Yahuah "precious," no open vision, and then the Voice comes not to the priest but to the boy asleep by the lamp; the word returns to Israel through a child.

# ALBUM II — THE ARK FOUGHT ITS OWN WAR
### The Ark Captured and Returned · 1 Samuel 4–7 · five songs

Israel drags the ark to the battlefield like a charm and loses it — thirty thousand fall, both wicked priests die, the old man breaks his neck at the news, and a dying mother names her son "the glory is gone." Then the strangest campaign in Scripture: the captured ark, with no army behind it, fights alone. Dagon falls on his face before it and wakes up dismembered on his own threshold; tumors chase it from city to city until the cry of Philistia goes up to heaven; and it comes home crowned with the enemy's gold, drawn by two cows who leave their calves and will not turn. The witnesses sing the box that needed no soldiers — and the stone Shmuel sets up at last on the very ground where the glory was lost.

*Why this title:* through chapters 5 and 6 the ark, taken as a trophy and left utterly defenseless, topples an idol, plagues five cities, and dictates the terms of its own return — a war waged and won with no hand of Israel in it.

| 2 | **Dagon Fell Before the Captured Ark** | 1 Samuel 5:1–5 | Heaven watching the prize humble the captor's god | The fish-god set beside the ark as a trophy, found face-down at dawn, set up, found down again — head and both hands severed on the threshold, only the stump of Dagon left, and the priests ever after stepping over the sill |
| 3 | **The Cry of the Cities Rose to Heaven** | 1 Samuel 5:6–12 | The Angels | The hand of Yahuah heavy with tumors on Ashdod, then Gath, then Ekron, each city shoving the ark to the next like a curse, a deadly panic, and the cry of the towns going up to heaven |

Five for five. *(Chapter 5 splits at the threshold — the idol, then the plague; 7:1 closes the ark's homeward road, 7:2 opens the twenty years' repentance.)*

# ALBUM III — THEY REJECTED THE KING THEY HAD
### The Demand for a King · 1 Samuel 8–12 · five songs

Shmuel grows old, his own sons take bribes, and the elders ask for the one thing the covenant never offered — a king like all the nations. Yahuah reads it plainly: they have not rejected the prophet but the Throne over the throne. Still He grants it, and the search runs through the least likely door: a tall young man out looking for lost donkeys is anointed in secret, then chosen by lot in public and found hiding in the baggage. The Spirit rushes on him and he saves a city whose right eyes were forfeit; the kingdom is renewed with rejoicing. And the old judge lays it all down with clean hands and thunder in the wheat harvest — a sign that asking for a king was itself the sin.

*Why this title:* Yahuah's own verdict on the demand (8:7) — "they have not rejected you, but they have rejected me, that I should not reign over them"; a nation asks for a king while the King already reigns.

# ALBUM IV — A MAN AFTER HIS OWN HEART
### Sha'ul Rejected, David Anointed · 1 Samuel 13–17 · seven songs

The first king unravels the moment the pressure comes. He will not wait the seven days for Shmuel and seizes the sacrifice; he binds the army with a rash oath that nearly kills his own brave son over a taste of honey; he spares Agag and the best of the sheep and learns that to obey is better than sacrifice — and the kingdom is torn from him like a robe. So Yahuah goes looking for a man after His own heart, and finds him keeping sheep: a horn of oil is emptied over the youngest, overlooked son, because heaven does not see as men see. The same boy is brought to soothe the tormented king with a harp — and then, in a valley, brings down six cubits of Philistine defiance with a single stone. The witnesses sing two kings at once: the one being unmade, and the one being made.

*Why this title:* the thread that binds the album (13:14; 16:7) — the kingdom taken from a man who would not keep the word, and given to "a man after his own heart," a heart Yahuah alone can see.

# ALBUM V — LOVED BY ALL BUT THE KING
### David at Sha'ul's Court · 1 Samuel 18–20 · five songs

Everyone loves David except the one man who matters. The king's own son strips off his robe and sword and hands them to the shepherd; the women come out dancing and credit David with ten thousands to the king's thousands; the king's daughter loves him, and all Israel with her. And every ounce of that love curdles in Sha'ul into a spear — thrown at the wall, thrown again, hidden inside a bride-price of a hundred Philistine foreskins, hidden behind messengers sent to a bed where only an idol lies. The witnesses sing the closing of the court against the anointed: the covenant of the arrows, the window let down in the dark, the friend who shoots a signal to say *run* — a man driven out of the palace by the poison of being loved.

*Why this title:* the engine of these three chapters (18:16, 18:28) — "all Israel and Yahudah loved David," Yonatan loved him, Mikhal loved him — while Sha'ul alone eyed him from that day and sought his life; the more he was loved, the more the king feared.

# ALBUM VI — THE PRICE OF THE HOLY BREAD
### The Outlaw and the Priests of Nov · 1 Samuel 21–22 · four songs

A fugitive with no bread and no sword comes to the priests of Nov, and walks out with the holiest loaf in Israel and the sword of the giant he once killed. But a man is watching from the shadow of the tabernacle — Do'eg the Edomite — and that loaf will cost a town its life. First David runs to the giant's own city and has to claw at the gate and drool in his beard to survive as a madman; then the debtors and the desperate and the bitter of soul gather to him in a cave and become an army. And then the reckoning: Sha'ul, told the priest fed David, orders the priests of Yahuah killed — his own guards refuse, so the Edomite does it, eighty-five men in linen, and a whole town after them. The witnesses sing the terrible price of one act of kindness to the hunted.

*Why this title:* the loaf of the Presence given to a starving fugitive (21:6) becomes, through Do'eg's report, the pretext for the slaughter of the priests of Nov (22:9–19) — a holy kindness paid for in the blood of a whole priestly town.

# ALBUM VII — HE WOULD NOT TOUCH THE ANOINTED
### The Hunted Anointed Who Spares · 1 Samuel 23–26 · five songs

Hunted through the wilderness, David keeps proving to be more merciful than the man chasing him. He saves a city from the Philistines and learns the city would have handed him over; he is trapped on one side of a mountain with Sha'ul closing on the other, and only a Philistine raid pulls the noose loose. Twice the king walks straight into his hands — asleep in a cave, asleep in a camp — and twice the anointed will not stretch out his hand against the anointed, taking a corner of a robe and a spear and a water jug instead of a life. And in between, a wise woman rides down a mountain to keep him from becoming a murderer over an insult. The witnesses sing the strangest restraint in the book: the hunted one who could end the hunt with one blow, and refuses.

*Why this title:* David's own principle, sung twice into the dark (24:6; 26:9) — "who can stretch forth his hand against Yahuah's anointed and be guiltless?"; the anointed of tomorrow will not lift a hand against the anointed of today.

# ALBUM VIII — TOMORROW YOU AND YOUR SONS
### The End of Sha'ul · 1 Samuel 27–31 · five songs

The book ends with two men walking opposite roads. David, sure he will die by Sha'ul's hand, takes shelter among the Philistines and is providentially turned back from the one battle he could never fight — the war against his own people. Sha'ul, with heaven gone silent — no dream, no Urim, no prophet — crawls in disguise to a medium in the night and hears his own doom spoken by a dead man: tomorrow, you and your sons, with me. David comes home to a burned Ziklag, strengthens himself in Yahuah, and recovers everything; Sha'ul climbs Gilboa and falls on his own sword as his sons die around him. And the men of Yavesh-Gilad, whose eyes he once saved, cross the night for his bones. The witnesses sing a reign that ran out of answers, and a mercy repaid at the very end.

*Why this title:* the sentence the raised Shmuel speaks over Sha'ul at Ein-Dor (28:19) — "tomorrow shalt thou and thy sons be with me" — the doom the album marches toward, fulfilled on Gilboa while the chosen one is kept safe.

## Compliance Notes

- **Sacred names:** OHI mode throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine); the text's own divine forms welcome (Yahuah Tzeva'ot, El, Elohim). The English words "God" and "Lord" appear nowhere in any title or copy. The Hebrew article rule holds in every title and note (no doubled article on *Ha-* forms). Honest Hebrew is welcomed where singable — Ikhavod, Even-ha-Ezer, cherem, *mashiach*, *kanaf*, *ner*, *chets*. Internal labels appear nowhere listener-facing.
- **Narrative-time rule honored:** inside every 1 Samuel-moment song the aches — the horn of oil on a shepherd boy, the anointed not yet enthroned, "Yahuah looks on the heart," the king Israel demanded set against the king Yahuah chose, the faithful priest promised to Eli's fallen house, the stone of help named where the glory was lost — are carried as mystery and longing, never named as fulfilled. The word *mashiach*/anointed is sung only as the text sings it over Sha'ul and David; David is named because the text names him, and the far end of his line is held as a longing the witnesses cannot yet see. The framing names and adores Yeshua; the songs, standing inside their own hour, do not.
- **Voice rule honored:** every singer is the Angels and/or a named Letter (the Nun keyed to *ner*, the lamp; the Chet keyed to *chets*, the arrow and the parting; the Kaph keyed to *kanaf*, the cut robe-corner and the wing). Chanah, Eli, Shmuel, Sha'ul, David, Yonatan, Mikhal, Avigayil, and the rest are *witnessed*, never made the singer; Chanah's song, Shmuel's oracles, and David's refusals are testified to from heaven's side, never sung as theirs. The ark, the fallen idol Dagon, the lamp not yet gone out, the milk cows, the stone Even-ha-Ezer, the kinnor, the brook-stone, the ephod, the spear at the bolster, and the cruse of water are invoked as images, never voiced. The medium of Ein-Dor is handled with grave restraint — heaven's dread, no occult glamor.
- **Picture Letters, never a count:** the Letters are named only as personae (the Nun, the Chet, the Kaph), never totaled and never sung as gematria mechanism. The biblical text-numbers are content: the thirty thousand fallen, the five golden tumors and five golden mice, the twenty years at Kiryath-Yearim, the quarter-shekel, Golyat's six cubits and a span, the thousands and ten thousands of the women's song, the hundred (and two hundred) foreskins, the eighty-five priests in linen, the six hundred men, the deep sleep on Sha'ul's camp.
- **Hebraic Music Mandate:** when these songs enter production, the Hebraic music type will be primary or secondary in every track, per the grounding (§5).
- **Posture:** none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading — held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider.
