## The shift — why the New Testament sounds different

For thirty-nine books, **two voices sang together** — the morning stars (the Picture Letters, the aleph-bet) and the sons of Elohim (the Angels), the two choirs of Job 38:7 who *sang together* at the foundations of the world. Across the whole Old Covenant they held one ache unresolved: the coming One the letters spelled and the Angels awaited, longed for and never seen.

At the incarnation, **the ache is answered — and the singing changes.**

- **The Picture Letters fall silent.** The Word (the Davar) by whom every letter was made is now made flesh; the aleph-bet lives in Him. The morning stars do not vanish — they come home into the incarnate Word — so through the Gospels **only the Angels sing.** John's Prologue is where that silence is sung: "The Davar Who Spoke Us Took Flesh."
- **The humans never sing either.** Not Miryam, not Yochanan the Immerser, not Kepha, not a crowd. The Angels are the *only* voice. They sing *about* what the humans said and did, and freely *quote* them — the Magnificat, Kepha's confession, the centurion's word, the crowd's Hoshana — but always framed from the Angels' own eye-witness testimony. A human-centered song bears witness to that human *as testimony to Yeshua;* it never means the human sings.
- **Yeshua is witnessed, never voiced.** He is testified to in awe — His words reported and marveled at, exactly as Yahuah's oracles were sung by the witnesses across the Old Covenant — never given a first-person singer. The seven "I AM" sayings, "this is my body," "Abba, not what I will," "it is finished": all are the Angels reporting what He said.
- **Christocentric — about "the who."** Every song is ultimately about the person of Yeshua: who He is, what heaven declares over Him, what He said and did.
- **Eye-witness chronology, forward-locked.** The Angels unveil Yeshua exactly as each book reveals Him, in narrative order — never referencing an event before it happens in that book. Where Yeshua foretells His death or return, the Angels witness Him *speaking* the dark saying and hold it unresolved; the cross and empty tomb are never depicted before their place.
- **Old-Covenant cross-reference — always open, always backward.** The one thing that stays free: the Angels, who witnessed all thirty-nine books, tie every moment back to the Torah, Prophets, and Writings — even where the humans miss it. This is the payoff of the whole platform: the aches once carried unresolved, now seen fulfilled.

The old footer was *"The stars sang. The angels sang."* The new one: **"Now the stars live in the Word — and the angels sing on."**

Each Gospel is pitched to its own shape — Matthew's five discourses and fulfilment-formulas; Mark's breathless *immediately* and the messianic secret; Luke's canticles and the long journey to Yerushalayim; John's seven signs and seven "I AM" sayings. One song per narrative unit, no honest zeros save Luke's literary prologue (1:1–4).

### The cornerstones

- **Matthew** opens the New Covenant itself: the genealogy running down to one cradle, the *almah*'s sign drawing breath, and the inclusio that frames everything — "Immanu-El, El with us" (1:23) answered at the last by "I am with you alway" (28:20).
- **Mark** sets the heavens *torn* at the Yarden (1:10) against the veil *torn* at the cross (15:38) — the same word — and lets the secret break only twice, both times by Yeshua Himself.
- **Luke** gives the platform its payoff moment: the Gloria (2:8–14) sung as the Angels' *own native liturgy* — the heavenly host who sang at the foundations now singing over the manger.
- **John** sings the shift itself in its Prologue — the Word made flesh, the aleph-bet that sang at creation now living in Him — the platform's doctrinal cornerstone.

## Compliance — the New-Testament ruleset (applies to all four)

1. **Only the Angels sing.** The Picture Letters are silent (they live in the incarnate Word); the humans never sing. Every "Who sings" cell across all 264 songs is the Angels — including the specific angels the texts supply (Gavriel; the messenger in Yosef's dreams; the host over Beit-Lechem; the strengthening angel of Gat-Shmanim; the young man / the two men / the angel of Yahuah at the tomb), all of whom are the Angels of this platform.
2. **Christocentric.** Every song bears witness to Yeshua; human-centered songs testify to Him.
3. **Yeshua witnessed, never first-person-voiced.**
4. **Eye-witness chronology, forward-locked** within each book; no event referenced before it occurs.
5. **Old-Covenant cross-reference free and backward-only.**
6. **Sacred names — OHI throughout:** Yeshua (never "Jesus"), the Mashiach (never "Christ"; no doubled English article on *Ha-* forms), Ben-Elohim, Ben-David, Ben-Adam, Yahuah, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine), Abba, Adon/Adonai. Hebraic names and places throughout. No English "God," "Lord," "Jesus," or "Christ" in any title or copy.
7. **Restraint on the passion** — witnessed from heaven's side, no wound shown; the weight carried by the Old-Covenant ties and the cosmic signs. The resurrection is the triumph.
8. **Textually disputed passages carried honestly** — the pericope adulterae (John 7:53–8:11) and the longer ending of Mark (16:9–20) are flagged as such per the scholarly-caution mandate.
9. **Hebraic Music Mandate** — the Hebraic music type primary or secondary in every track.
10. **Posture** — none of this is offered as canon. It is a reading, held at arm's length and turned. Something to consider. Nothing publishes without approval per `J:\README-CONTRACT.md`.

## The road ahead (not yet pitched)

The Gospels are the frontier's first landing. Beyond them lie the Acts of the emissaries (44), the letters of Shaul and the general epistles (45–65), and the Revelation of Yochanan (66) — where, in the final book, **the singing returns in full:** the living creatures and the elders and the myriads of angels, and every creature in heaven and earth, take up a new song to the Lamb. The two voices that fell to one at the manger become the great multitude no one could number. But that is another frontier, for another day.
