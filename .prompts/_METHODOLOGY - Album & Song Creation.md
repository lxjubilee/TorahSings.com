## 1. Purpose

TorahSings.com sets Scripture to music at scale. Because the source material is the biblical text itself, the project will draw attention — theological, cultural, and critical — and it should. This document exists so that every album and every song can be defended on principle: not manufactured arbitrarily, not padded to hit a number, and not cherry-picked from favorite passages, but drawn faithfully and deliberately from the text.

The method below is built to hold up under scrutiny while still leaving full room for creativity. Discipline in *what* becomes a song and *how albums are grouped* is precisely what frees the music to be bold in execution.

Two ideas run through everything here:

1. **The text sets the boundaries.** Songs follow the natural seams of Scripture, not a production quota.
2. **The grouping carries the meaning.** Albums are coherent theological and artistic statements, never arbitrary blocks of verses.

---

## 2. The Foundational Rule — One Song Per Narrative Unit

**A single narrative unit yields, at most, a single song.**

If Genesis 1 is one story — the seven-day creation account — then one song comes out of it. Not two. Even where the text is rich enough to support more, a second song is not pulled from the same narrative. Each song must come from its own distinct story.

This is the load-bearing rule of the entire platform. Everything else serves it.

### 2.1 What is a "narrative unit"?

A narrative unit is a self-contained story or movement within the text — a passage with its own beginning, middle, and end, distinct from what surrounds it. A chapter is *not* automatically one unit. A chapter may contain one unit, several units, or be part of a larger unit that spans multiple chapters.

### 2.2 How to find the boundary of a unit

A new narrative unit generally begins where the text shifts in one or more of these ways:

- **Characters change** — a new primary figure enters or the focus moves to different people.
- **Setting changes** — the location moves (garden → field → city).
- **Time changes** — a clear temporal jump ("and it came to pass after these things…").
- **Subject or genre changes** — narrative gives way to genealogy, law, poetry, or blessing, or the reverse.
- **A literary seam appears** — an explicit structural marker in the text.

**Genesis-specific tool — the *toledot* formula.** Genesis is literally organized by the recurring phrase "these are the generations of…" (*eleh toledot*). These formulas are natural, textually-grounded unit boundaries and should be treated as primary seams when drawing lines in Genesis. Using the text's own structure to justify song boundaries is the single strongest defense against any claim that selection is arbitrary.

### 2.3 A chapter can yield zero, one, or several songs

- **One narrative in the chapter → one song.**
- **Multiple distinct narratives in one chapter → multiple songs** (one per narrative). Example: Genesis 4 can carry Cain and Abel (vv. 1–16), the line of Cain (vv. 17–24), and Seth/Enosh (vv. 25–26) — up to three units, therefore up to three songs.
- **No song-worthy narrative in the chapter → zero songs.** Some chapters legitimately contribute nothing, and that is a valid, honest outcome. We do not invent a song to fill a chapter.

The count is never forced. The text decides.

## 3. Album Grouping Methodology

Songs are gathered into albums by **theme and story context**, not by chronological chunking of verses. An album is a complete movement of the biblical story that a listener experiences from start to finish.

### 3.1 The seven-song target

**Seven songs per album is the goal.** Seven is a meaningful biblical number and a natural album length. Aim for it whenever the grouping supports it.

- **Seven is a target, not a cap (owner directive, 2026-07-08):** the narrative governs the count. Where a coherent grouping genuinely holds more than seven units, the album carries more — it may run to eight, nine, ten, or beyond rather than leave a real story unsung. The load-bearing safeguard stays **one song per narrative unit** (§2), which blocks padding from the other side: never split one story to inflate a count, and never drop a genuine unit to protect a number. *Do not leave songs behind.*
- **Floor is flexible:** an album of three or four songs is completely acceptable when that is all the natural grouping holds. A tight four-song album that is genuinely unified beats a padded seven-song album every time.

> **Note on the count:** TorahSings deliberately targets **seven** songs per album, which differs from the general Jubilee Music twelve-song standard. This is intentional and project-specific. On TorahSings the *narrative-boundary rule governs the count* — the number of songs follows the stories, rather than a fixed album length dictating how many songs must be produced. Do not "correct" TorahSings albums up to twelve.

### 3.2 How to group

Group around a coherent theme or story arc. For example, in Genesis:

- **Creation and the Fall** — the making of the world through humanity's first rebellion and its immediate aftermath.
- **Genealogy and the Flood** — the generations from Adam onward, gathered toward the flood narrative.
- **Covenant beginnings** — the call and journey of Abraham.

The album's identity should answer the question: *why are these songs together?* If the answer is clear and the songs reinforce one another, the grouping is right.

### 3.3 Songs may distribute across different albums

A single chapter's songs do not have to stay together. A narrative song and a genealogy song from the same chapter can land on different albums if that serves the grouping better — e.g., narrative songs on a story album, genealogy songs gathered onto a "generations" album. Distribution across albums is allowed and encouraged wherever it produces tighter, more coherent records.

## 4. Track Sequencing

**Ordering within an album is artistic, not chronological.**

> **OVERRIDE (owner directive, 2026-07-08 — supersedes this section):** present songs in **biblical narrative order**, not a cinematic reordering. An out-of-order sequence may read well as film but confuses listeners following the story — narrative clarity wins. Keep the opener/closer craft below *within* the constraint of the text's own chronology.

The tracklist does not have to follow biblical order. Sequence for album flow — energy, key, mood, and thematic build. The opener should establish the album; the closer should land it. Standard album craft governs the running order, exactly as it would for any produced record. Chronology is available as a tool when it happens to serve the flow, but it is never a requirement.

---

## 5. Titling

Album titles should be **creative and evocative** while remaining honestly tied to the grouping. A listener seeing the title and tracklist should understand what the album is about. Creativity is welcome; obscurity that hides the album's actual content is not. The title sells the album *and* tells the truth about it.

## 6. Sacred-Name & Language Conventions (Lyrics)

All lyric output follows house convention:

- **OHI mode is the default** throughout — Yahuah, Yeshua, Elohim, Ruach HaKodesh (feminine). CCI mode (Lord, Jesus, God, Holy Spirit) is used only when specifically requested for a given deliverable.
- **Hebrew article rule:** never write "the Ruach HaKodesh." Use **Ruach HaKodesh** or **the Ruach Kodesh**. The same rule applies to HaMashiach, HaTorah, and similar forms — the article does not stack on the *Ha-* prefix.
- **"OHI" and "CCI" are internal labels only.** They never appear in any listener-facing lyric, title, or liner text.

## 7. Why It's Done This Way

This section states the reasoning, so the method can be explained and defended by anyone applying it.

**Why one song per narrative unit.**
It forces every song to earn its place. It prevents padding and dilution, and it removes the temptation to mine three or four songs from a beloved chapter while neighboring stories go unset. Most importantly, it makes the whole catalog defensible: for any song, we can point to exactly one passage and say *one song, one story*. That discipline reads as reverence, not convenience.

**Why thematic grouping instead of chronological chunking.**
Slicing Scripture into fixed blocks of verses produces arbitrary albums. Grouping by story and theme produces albums that are complete statements — a listener experiences a full movement of the biblical narrative rather than a random segment. The grouping is where the meaning lives.

**Why seven, and why it flexes.**
The count serves the narrative, never the reverse. Forcing every album to twelve (or any fixed number) would require either splitting a single story into multiple songs — a direct violation of the foundational rule — or padding with weak material. Seven is a meaningful, natural target that bends down when a grouping is smaller. The rule protects the integrity of the stories.

**Why artistic sequencing.**
Album flow — energy, mood, key, and thematic arc — rarely matches chronological order. Sequencing for the listener's experience is simply good album-making, and Scripture set to music deserves the same craft as any serious record.

**Why defensibility is the point.**
TorahSings puts the biblical text to music at scale and invites scrutiny by doing so. The method has to demonstrate discipline and reverence — songs drawn faithfully, one per story, grouped with intent, titled honestly. When the process itself is principled, the creativity on top of it is free to be ambitious.

## 8. Worked Example — Genesis 1–4

This shows the system end to end.

**Step 1 — Identify narrative units.**

| Passage | Narrative unit | Songs |
|---|---|---|
| Genesis 1 | The seven-day creation account | 1 |
| Genesis 2 | The garden of Eden / forming of the man | 1 |
| Genesis 3 | The Fall and the expulsion | 1 |
| Genesis 4:1–16 | Cain and Abel | 1 |
| Genesis 4:17–24 | The line of Cain | 0–1 (optional) |
| Genesis 4:25–26 | Seth and the beginning of calling on the Name | 0–1 (optional) |

**Step 2 — Group into an album.**

*Option A — a tight four-song album:*
Creation → Eden → The Fall → Cain and Abel. Four songs, fully unified around "the beginning and the break." A legitimate, complete album at four.

*Option B — build toward seven:*
Add the line of Cain and the Seth passage, then extend into Genesis 5 (the generations from Adam) and the opening of the flood arc, to approach seven. Or — following §3.3 — split the genealogical material off onto a separate "generations" album and keep this one as the tight narrative record.

**Step 3 — Title and sequence.**

Give the album an evocative, honest title tied to its arc. Sequence the tracks for musical flow — a strong opener, a closer that lands — rather than strict chapter order.

Both options are correct. Which one ships depends on the grouping judgment in §3, not on a quota.
